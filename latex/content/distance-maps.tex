\chapter{Point Cloud Registration with Distance Maps}\label{chap:distance-maps}

This chapter presents a novel approach for 2D~point cloud registration
using distance maps, developed within the scope of this PhD~Thesis
in collaboration with Prof.~Dr.~Giorgio~Grisetti from
Sapienza Università di Roma.
Distance maps encode the Euclidean distance to the nearest reference point
at every cell of a precomputed discrete grid, decoupling data association
from the iterative optimization loop.
By replacing the explicit nearest-neighbor search performed at every
iteration of classical
\glslink{icp}{\glsentryfull{icp}}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991}
with an $O(1)$ grid lookup on distance map-based data structures,
the proposed approach provides both point-to-point and point-to-plane error
formulations compatible with Gauss-Newton optimization on the $SE(2)$ manifold.

This chapter is organized as follows. \secref{sec:distance-maps:background}
covers point cloud registration methods from the literature and
the non-linear least-squares framework used throughout this work.
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment} formulates the proposed approach,
introducing the distance map representation,
the registration problem on the $SE(2)$ manifold, and both
point-to-point and point-to-plane error formulations with their Jacobians.
\secref{sec:distance-maps:results} presents experimental results on
synthetic and real-world data, evaluating registration accuracy and
uncertainty estimation.
The real-world data refers to the
\emph{IILABS 3D -- \underline{i}ilab \underline{I}ndoor
\underline{L}iD\underline{A}R-\underline{b}ased \underline{S}LAM 3D}
dataset~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025}, a complimentary contribution of this
PhD~Thesis (see~\appxref{appx:iilabs3d}).
The work \emph{Integrating Multimodal Perception into Ground
Mobile Robots}~\cite{sousa:icarsc:2025}, developed within the scope of this
PhD~Thesis, describes the platform used to collect the dataset
(see~\appxref{appx:hangfaq2}).
Lastly, \secref{sec:distance-maps:conclusions} summarizes the main
conclusions and directions for future work.

\section{Background}\label{sec:distance-maps:background}

Point cloud registration is a fundamental problem in mobile robotics,
consisting of estimating the rigid-body transformation that best aligns
two point clouds acquired by a range sensor. This problem arises in a wide range
of applications, including \gls{slam}, odometry estimation, and map-based
localization. Thus, this section introduces the background underpinning
the proposed distance map-based registration approach.
\secref{sec:distance-maps:background:registration} reviews 2D~and
3D~point cloud registration methods from the literature,
ranging from classical \gls{icp} variants to probabilistic and
precomputed spatial gradient formulations, motivating the use of
distance maps as a unified and correspondence-free registration framework.
\secref{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares} presents the
non-linear least-squares framework used throughout this chapter,
covering the Gauss-Newton algorithm, uncertainty estimation,
robust estimators, and the generalization to non-Euclidean state spaces.

\subsection{Point cloud registration}\label{sec:distance-maps:background:registration}
One of the most popular approaches for point cloud registration is
the \glslink{icp}{\glsentryfull{icp}} algorithm.
Besl and McKay~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} address registration of 3D~shapes
represented as point clouds, introducing the standard \gls{icp} algorithm.
The following two steps can summarize this algorithm:
(i) compute correspondences between the source ($\mathbf{p}_i$) and target ($\mathbf{z}_i$)
cloud points (closest nearest-neighbor based on Euclidean distance); and
(ii) estimate the rigid transformation of the source \gls{wrt} target cloud
($\mathbf{x}$) that minimizes a point-to-point distance measure between
corresponding points (see \eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:point-to-point}).
Chen and Medioni~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991} exploit the tendency of
most range data to be locally planar and introduce a point-to-plane variant
of \gls{icp}. The proposed method exploits surface normal information
on the target cloud ($\mathbf{n}_{z_i}$).
Instead of minimizing the difference between the projected source and
the target points, the point-to-plane error metric minimizes
the squared dot product of the surface normal with
the difference between the expected projected source and
the reference target points
(see \eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:point-to-plane}).
Indeed, Rusinkiewicz and Levoy~\cite{rusinkiewicz-levoy:im:2001} compared
\gls{icp} variants with respect to point selection,
data association, weighting of associated pairs, outlier rejection, and
error metrics. The point-to-plane~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991}
metric demonstrated significantly better performance than
the point-to-point~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} formulation in the experiments.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:point-to-point}
\mathbf{x} = \argmin_\mathbf{x} \sum_{i=1}^M \| \mathbf{x} \mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{z}_i \|^2
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:point-to-plane}
\mathbf{x} = \argmin_\mathbf{x} \sum_{i=1}^M \| \mathbf{n}_{z_i}^T \cdot
\left( \mathbf{x} \mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{z}_i \right) \|^2
\end{equation}

Unlike traditional \gls{icp} methods, which rely on explicit point
correspondences, the \glslink{ndt}{\glsentryfull{ndt}}
proposed by Biber and Strasser~\cite{biber-strasser:iros:2003} performs
2D~point cloud registration by representing the environment
with local normal distributions. The 2D~space is discretized into cells.
Each cell is associated with a Gaussian distribution defined by
the mean and covariance of the cell points,
which provide information about the surface those points represent.
Then, Biber and Strasser~\cite{biber-strasser:iros:2003} formulate
2D~point cloud registration as the minimization of an error function
derived from these distributions, optimized using the Gauss-Newton algorithm.
Experimental results showed that \gls{ndt}~\cite{biber-strasser:iros:2003} was
able to map large areas without requiring odometry.
Magnusson~\etal~\cite{magnusson:jfr:2007} later extended the original
\gls{ndt}~~\cite{biber-strasser:iros:2003} to the
3D~point cloud registration problem by subdividing space into voxels,
each modelled as a normal distribution estimated from
the points contained within it. The alignment is obtained by
evaluating the likelihood of points from one scan under
the distributions of the other scan.
Compared with point-to-point \gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} using
kd-tree nearest-neighbor search~\cite{bentley:cacm:1975},
3D-\gls{ndt}~\cite{magnusson:jfr:2007} achieves
lower computation cost by avoiding explicit correspondence search,
while also offering a more compact representation of 3D~point cloud
compared to raw data.

While leveraging
\gls{ndt}~\cite{biber-strasser:iros:2003,magnusson:jfr:2007}-like
representations of the scene, \gls{gicp}~\cite{segal:rss:2009} and
\gls{nicp}~\cite{serafin-grisetti:iros:2015} still retain the \gls{icp}
structure for cloud registration.
\gls{gicp}~\cite{segal:rss:2009} retains the standard \gls{icp}
structure for correspondence estimation, typically using Euclidean
closest-neighbor search accelerated with a kd-tree~\cite{bentley:cacm:1975}.
The main modification lies in the minimization objective function,
introducing a plane-to-plane error metric. \gls{gicp} models
local surface information around each point, estimated from
neighboring points via \gls{pca}. By accounting for the covariance of
the matched points, \gls{gicp} weights residuals based on
local geometric uncertainty. Experiments showed that
\gls{gicp}~\cite{segal:rss:2009} was more robust to incorrect correspondences
and more accurate than standard point-to-point and point-to-plane
\gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991}.
\gls{nicp}~\cite{serafin-grisetti:iros:2015} extends
\gls{gicp}~\cite{segal:rss:2009} not only in the optimization stage but also
during correspondence search.
Rather than relying purely on Euclidean nearest-neighbor matching,
\gls{nicp}~\cite{serafin-grisetti:iros:2015} uses a line-of-sight-based
association step and rejects candidate pairs when normals are not well defined,
when point distance exceeds a threshold, when curvature mismatch is too large,
or when the normals are insufficiently aligned. In the optimization step,
\gls{nicp}~\cite{serafin-grisetti:iros:2015} augments each point with
its normal and minimizes a Mahalanobis distance in this 6D space.
In the experiments, \gls{nicp}~\cite{serafin-grisetti:iros:2015} was more
accurate than \gls{gicp}~\cite{segal:rss:2009} and
\gls{ndt}~\cite{magnusson:jfr:2007}
registration algorithms on the evaluated datasets.

Furthermore, Montesano~\etal~\cite{montesano:iros:2005} present
a probabilistic scan matching method for 2D~point cloud registration.
The method~\cite{montesano:iros:2005} builds a set of possible correspondences
using an individual compatibility test based on the Mahalanobis distance
between pairs of points, while accounting for correspondence uncertainty.
Then, Montesano~\etal~\cite{montesano:iros:2005} maximize
the probability of scan alignment over the points correspondence distribution.
The algorithm~\cite{montesano:iros:2005} showed faster convergence
rates than standard \gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992}.
Instead of selecting a single set of correspondences as
Montesano~\etal~\cite{montesano:iros:2005}, Censi~\cite{censi:icra:2006}
considers all possible correspondences and generates alignment hypotheses
through the \gls{ght}, while avoiding explicit point-to-point correspondences.
Censi~\cite{censi:icra:2006} computes alignment within
a probabilistic framework that incorporates a motion model and estimates
for registration uncertainty, accounting for surface normals.

Similarly, the GMapping~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007} and
Cartographer~\cite{hess:icra:2016} \gls{slam} algorithms use \gls{mle}
probabilistic formulations to estimate the scan alignment \gls{wrt} the map.
The scan matcher in GMapping~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007} seeks
the most likely robot pose by matching the current observation against
the map built so far. The \gls{mle} formulation estimates
the robot pose given the map, the current scan, and an initial pose guess.
This formulation performs a gradient-descent search over
the observation likelihood function defined on the grid map, based on the
beam-endpoint sensor model~\cite{thrun:book:2005}.
Cartographer~\cite{hess:icra:2016} optimizes the scan pose relative to
the current local submap using a Ceres~\cite{ceres:sw:2023}-based scan matcher.
This matcher estimates the pose that maximizes the occupancy probabilities of
the scan points projected onto the submap. The problem is formulated as
a non-linear least-squares optimization, using a smooth version of
the occupancy probability values associated with the projected scan points.

Precomputed spatial gradients may be leveraged for the 2D~point cloud
registration without requiring explicit point-to-point correspondences.
HectorSLAM~\cite{kohlbrecher:ssr:2011} uses spatial gradients over the
2D~occupancy grid map ($\mathcal{M}$).
The scan matching finds the pose that maximizes
the occupancy probability of where the beam endpoints $\mathbf{z}_i$ land on occupied
cells (see \eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:hectorslam}).
The method uses bilinear interpolation over the occupancy map and approximates
its spatial derivatives, applying then a Gauss-Newton update.
PerfectMatch~\cite{lauer:robocup:2006,sobreira:jint:2019} focuses on
a soccer field markings environment for robot soccer.
This method formulates the 2D~registration problem based on distance maps, using
the \gls{rprop}~\cite{riedmiller-braun:icnn:1993} gradient-descent algorithm.
Each point is evaluated by the distance map ($\mathcal{D}_\mathcal{M}$)
that gives the distance to the closest field marking through a
robust M-estimator ($\rho$)
(see \eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:perfectmatch}).
The algorithm considers that the distance map is over the entire soccer field
environment, only considering scan-to-map alignment.
Although distance maps are continuous and thus differentiable,
the authors approximate the final estimation covariance based on the second
derivative of the error assuming an environment with orthogonal walls,
equivalent to the line markings of soccer fields.
Sobreira~\etal~\cite{sobreira:jint:2019} benchmarked in a simulation environment
PerfectMatch~\cite{lauer:robocup:2006} against
\gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} and
\gls{ndt}~\cite{biber-strasser:iros:2003}.
PerfectMatch~\cite{lauer:robocup:2006} was faster than both
\gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} and
\gls{ndt}~\cite{biber-strasser:iros:2003}, given that
PerfectMatch~\cite{lauer:robocup:2006} uses lookup tables
for the error function.
Additionally, the PerfectMatch~\cite{lauer:robocup:2006} algorithm showed a
higher tolerance to orientation initialization errors than
\gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} and
\gls{ndt}~\cite{biber-strasser:iros:2003} in the experiments made by
Sobreira~\etal~\cite{sobreira:jint:2019}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:hectorslam}
\mathbf{x} = \argmin_\mathbf{x} \sum_{i=1}^M
\left[ 1 - \mathcal{M}\!\left(\mathbf{x} \mathbf{z}_i\right) \right]^2
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:perfectmatch}
\mathbf{x} = \argmin_\mathbf{x} \sum_{i=1}^M
\rho\!\left( \mathcal{D}_\mathcal{M}\!\left( \mathbf{x} \mathbf{z}_i \right) \right)
\end{equation}

Overall, most \gls{icp} methods rely on explicit point-to-point correspondences,
requiring two steps per optimization iteration: data association and
pairwise error computation between source and target points.
As for probabilistic-based approaches, these methods typically require solving a
\gls{map} problem and having a map estimate of the environment over time, which
is not compatible with scan-to-scan registration~\cite{segal:rss:2009}.
In contrast, distance maps
should be compatible with both scan-to-scan and scan-to-map alignment,
providing a nearest-neighbor framework between source and target clouds.
While the PerfectMatch~\cite{lauer:robocup:2006} algorithm is specific
to scan-to-map registration, an adaptation of the original approach
should be compatible with scan-to-scan alignment.
Moreover, distance maps provide a precomputed lookup table
that directly allows evaluating the registration error of each source point
without requiring an explicit data association step.
This property allows faster computation during point cloud registration than
\gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} and
\gls{ndt}~\cite{biber-strasser:iros:2003}, as showed in
Sobreira~\etal~\cite{sobreira:jint:2019}.

\subsection{Least-squares minimization}
\label{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares}

State estimation in mobile robotics requires inferring an unknown state
from noisy sensor measurements~\cite{grisetti:itsm:2010,grisetti:robotics:2020}.
First, \secref{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:problem-formulation}
formulates the problem as a \gls{map} inference problem
and demonstrates how, under Gaussian noise assumptions, it reduces to
a non-linear least-squares problem. Next,
\secref{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:gn} shows how
the Gauss-Newton algorithm solves the non-linear least-squares problem
through a first-order approximation from the Taylor series.
\secref{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:uncertainty} presents
an analysis of the uncertainty of the recovered state estimate based on
the approximate Hessian matrix retrieved from the Gauss-Newton solver. Then,
\secref{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-estimators} extends
the non-linear least-squares problem to use robust estimators to handle
outliers. Finally,
\secref{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold} generalizes
the non-linear least-squares problems for non-Euclidean state spaces.

\subsubsection{Problem formulation}
\label{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:problem-formulation}

Least-squares minimization arises in robotics when estimating an unknown state
from noisy sensor measurements~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020}.
Let $\mathbf{x}$ denote non-observable
state variables and $\mathbf{z}$ the measurements.
In the scope of this PhD~Thesis, the measurements in this chapter are the observations of the
environment acquired by the robot's on-board 2D~laser scanner.
The state is assumed to follow a prior distribution $p(\mathbf{x})$,
while the conditional distribution of the measurements given the state,
$p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$, is assumed known,
commonly referred to as the \emph{observation model}. Then, the goal
is to estimate the most probable state given the measurements,
\ie, the posterior $p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})$.
This problem represents a \gls{map} estimation problem, as shown in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:map}~\cite{barfoot:book:2017}.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:map}
\mathbf{x}^* = \argmax_\mathbf{x} p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})
\end{equation}

By applying Bayes' rule, shown in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:bayes}, the posterior
distribution $p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})$ becomes proportional to
the likelihood of the measurements given the state
$p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$ multiplied by the prior $p(\mathbf{x})$.
If a uniform prior over the admissible values of $\mathbf{x}$ is assumed,
then $p(\mathbf{x})$ is constant and the
posterior $p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})$ depends only on the measurement
distribution conditioned by the state $p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:bayes}
p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z}) =
\frac{p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})p(\mathbf{x})}{p(\mathbf{z})}
\propto p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})p(\mathbf{x})
\propto p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})
\end{equation}

Next, considering $M$ measurements $\mathbf{z}^{[i]}$ that are
conditionally independent given $\mathbf{x}$, the likelihood
$p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})$ results in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:independence}.
Under the assumption that each measurement follows a Gaussian distribution
with mean $\mu_{z|x}^{[i]}=\mathbf{h}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})$
(predicted measurement given the state $\mathbf{x}$) and covariance
$\mathbf{\Sigma}^{[i]} = {\mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}}^{-1}$ (where
$\mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}$ represents the information matrix of each measurement),
the individual likelihoods $p(\mathbf{z}^{[i]}|\mathbf{x})$ result in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:measurement}, based on the
probability density function of normal distributions.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:independence}
p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z}) \propto \prod_{i=1}^M p(\mathbf{z}^{[i]}|\mathbf{x})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:measurement}
\begin{aligned}
p(\mathbf{z}^{[i]}|\mathbf{x})
  & = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{z}^{[i]} ; \mathbf{h}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}),
                                    {\mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}}^{-1})\\
  & \propto \exp\!\left(
      -\frac{1}{2}
      \left( \mathbf{h}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}) - \mathbf{z}^{[i]} \right)^T
      \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}
      \left( \mathbf{h}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}) - \mathbf{z}^{[i]} \right)
  \right)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Furthermore, \eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:error} defines
the residual error $\mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})$ for each measurement.
The squared Mahalanobis distance ${\chi^2}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})$ of the residual
error is formulated in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:chi2}.
Thus, \eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:measurement-detailed}
illustrates the proportionality of the individual likelihoods
$p(\mathbf{z}^{[i]}|\mathbf{x})$ over the squared Mahalanobis distance
${\chi^2}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})$.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:error}
\mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{h}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}) - \mathbf{z}^{[i]}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:chi2}
{\chi^2}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}) =
\mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})^T \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]} \mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:measurement-detailed}
\begin{aligned}
p(\mathbf{z}^{[i]}|\mathbf{x})
  & \propto \exp\!\left(
      -\frac{1}{2}
      \mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})^T
      \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}
      \mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})
  \right)\\
  & \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}{\chi^2}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})\right)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Under the assumption of having a uniform prior,
the original \gls{map} problem formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:map} can be rewritten as
a \glslink{mle}{\glsentryfull{mle}} problem that maximizes
the probability of the measurements given
the state $p(\mathbf{z}^{[i]}|\mathbf{x})$
(see \eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:mle-log-likelihood}).
Through the negative log-likelihood
(see \defref{def:literature:slam:log-likelihood}), dropping constant factors,
and assuming the measurements under Gaussian noise,
the original \gls{map} problem for robotics state estimation reduces to a
\gls{mle} problem, which in turn reduces to minimizing
the sum of squared Mahalanobis residuals.
This problem is equivalent to a weighted non-linear least-squares problem,
as formalized in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:mle-log-likelihood}.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:mle-log-likelihood}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{x}^*
  & = \argmax_\mathbf{x} \prod_{i=1}^M p(\mathbf{z}^{[i]}|\mathbf{x})\\
  & = \argmax_\mathbf{x} \prod_{i=1}^M \exp\!\left( -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})^T \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]} \mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}) \right)\\
  & = \argmin_\mathbf{x} \sum_{i=1}^M \mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})^T \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]} \mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})\\
  & = \argmin_\mathbf{x} \sum_{i=1}^M {\chi^2}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Gauss-Newton algorithm}
\label{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:gn}

Following the non-linear least-squares formulation commonly used in
robotics state estimation and discussed in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:problem-formulation},
one possible solution is an iterative minimization of
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:iterative-function}~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020}.
At each iteration $k$, the state is updated by a
perturbation $\mathbf{\Delta x}_k$, as shown in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:perturbation}.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:iterative-function}
F\!\left(\mathbf{x}_k\right) =
\sum_{i=1}^M \mathbf{e}^{[i]}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_k\right)^T
               \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}
               \mathbf{e}^{[i]}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_k\right)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:perturbation}
\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k + \mathbf{\Delta x}_k
\end{equation}

In order to compute $\mathbf{\Delta x}_k$,
each residual is linearized around the current estimate $\mathbf{x}_k$
via a first-order Taylor approximation, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:error-function-taylor}~\cite{barfoot:book:2017,nocedal-wright:book:2006},
where $\mathbf{J}^{[i]}\in\mathbb{R}^{m_i\times n}$ is the Jacobian of the
$i$-th residual evaluated at $\mathbf{x}_k$, with $m_i$ the dimension of the
residual $i$ and $n$ the dimension of the state.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:error-function-taylor}
\mathbf{e}^{[i]}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_k + \mathbf{\Delta x}_k\right)
\simeq
\underbrace{\mathbf{e}^{[i]}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_k\right)}_{\mathbf{e}^{[i]}} +
\underbrace{\left.\frac{\partial \mathbf{e}^{[i]}\!\left(\mathbf{x}\right)}{\partial \mathbf{x}}
\right|_{\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x}_k}}_{\mathbf{J}^{[i]}}
\mathbf{\Delta x}_k
\end{equation}

Next, \eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:products-expansion}
substitutes the first-order Taylor approximation into the objective function and
expands the products in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:iterative-function},
where the scalar symmetry
$\mathbf{\Delta x}^T {\mathbf{J}^{[i]}}^T \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}
\mathbf{e}^{[i]} = {\mathbf{e}^{[i]}}^T \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}
\mathbf{J}^{[i]} \mathbf{\Delta x}$ has been used to combine the two
cross-terms. Also, note that $\mathbf{b}^{[i]} = {\mathbf{J}^{[i]}}^T
\mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]} \mathbf{e}^{[i]} \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$ is defined as
a column vector. The notation ${\mathbf{b}^{[i]}}^T \mathbf{\Delta x}$ in
the expansion below reflects the transpose of that column vector.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:products-expansion}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{e}^{[i]}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_k + \mathbf{\Delta x}_k\right)^T
\mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}
\mathbf{e}^{[i]}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_k + \mathbf{\Delta x}_k\right)
& \simeq \left(\mathbf{e}^{[i]} + \mathbf{J}^{[i]} \mathbf{\Delta x}\right)^T
         \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}
         \left(\mathbf{e}^{[i]} + \mathbf{J}^{[i]} \mathbf{\Delta x}\right)\\
& = \mathbf{\Delta x}^T \underbrace{{\mathbf{J}^{[i]}}^T \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]} \mathbf{J}^{[i]}}_{\mathbf{H}^{[i]}} \mathbf{\Delta x} +
    2 \underbrace{{\mathbf{e}^{[i]}}^T \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]} \mathbf{J}^{[i]}}_{{\mathbf{b}^{[i]}}^T} \mathbf{\Delta x} +
    \underbrace{{\mathbf{e}^{[i]}}^T \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]} \mathbf{e}^{[i]}}_{c^{[i]}}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Summing over all measurements and defining
$\mathbf{H} = \sum_{i=1}^M \mathbf{H}^{[i]} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$,
$\mathbf{b} = \sum_{i=1}^M \mathbf{b}^{[i]} \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$, and
$c = \sum_{i=1}^M c^{[i]} \in \mathbb{R}$,
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:iterative-function-linearized}
formulates the linearized cost derived in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:products-expansion}
to a quadratic form in $\mathbf{\Delta x}$.
The information matrices of the measurements $\mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}$ are
symmetric positive semi-definite. Consequently, the matrix $\mathbf{H}$
inherits the same property by construction, where
$\mathbf{H}^T=\mathbf{H}$~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:iterative-function-linearized}
F\!\left(\mathbf{x}_k + \mathbf{\Delta x}_k\right)
\simeq
\mathbf{\Delta x}_k^T \mathbf{H} \mathbf{\Delta x}_k +
2 \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{\Delta x}_k +
c
\end{equation}

The optimal perturbation minimizes the quadratic approximation presented in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:iterative-function-linearized}.
Setting the gradient \gls{wrt} $\mathbf{\Delta x}$ to zero, as shown in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:perturbation-minimization-solution},
yields the normal equations presented in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:b-hx}, whose solution
$\mathbf{\Delta x}_k$ is then used to update the state via
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:perturbation}, constituting
one iteration of the \emph{Gauss-Newton} method.
The method linearizes the residual error functions around the current estimate
and minimizes the resulting quadratic approximation of the weighted non-linear
least-squares cost~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020}.
\algref{alg:distance-maps:background:least-squares:gauss-newton} details the
steps for a single iteration of the minimization procedure.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:perturbation-minimization-solution}
\mathbf{0} =
\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{\Delta x}}
\left[
  \mathbf{\Delta x}_k^T \mathbf{H} \mathbf{\Delta x}_k
  + 2 \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{\Delta x}_k + c
\right] = 2\mathbf{H} \mathbf{\Delta x}_k + 2\mathbf{b}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:b-hx}
\mathbf{H} \mathbf{\Delta x}_k = -\mathbf{b}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Least-squares and uncertainty}
\label{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:uncertainty}

At convergence, the approximate Hessian matrix $\mathbf{H}$ of the Gauss-Newton
algorithm provides an estimate of the uncertainty for the recovered state.
Consider a small perturbation $\mathbf{\Delta x}$ away from
the optimum $\mathbf{x}^*$.
Under the first-order linearization of each measurement function
$\mathbf{h}^{[i]}$, the estimated measurement
$\mathbf{h}^{[i]}\!\left(\mathbf{x}^*+\mathbf{\Delta x}\right)$ around the optimum
can be approximated by
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:measurement-approximation},
where $\mathbf{J}^{[i]}\in\mathbb{R}^{m_i \times n}$ is the
Jacobian of the $i$-th measurement function evaluated at the optimum,
$m_i$ is the dimension of the $i$-th measurement, and
$n$ is the dimension of the state.
This linearization is valid as long as the measurement function is
sufficiently smooth and $\|\mathbf{\Delta x}\|$ is small.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:measurement-approximation}
\mathbf{h}^{[i]}\!\left(\mathbf{x}^* + \mathbf{\Delta x}\right)
\simeq
\mathbf{h}^{[i]}\!\left(\mathbf{x}^*\right)
+
\underbrace{
  \left.
    \frac{\partial \mathbf{h}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})}{\partial \mathbf{x}}
  \right|_{\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x}^*}
}_{\mathbf{J}^{[i]}}
\mathbf{\Delta x}
\end{equation}

Next,
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:perturbated-state-conditional}
represents the conditional probability of the $i$-th measurement given the
perturbed state, where ${\mathbf{z}^{[i]}}^* = \mathbf{h}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}^*)$
is the predicted measurement at the optimum.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:perturbated-state-conditional}
p(\mathbf{z}^{[i]}|\mathbf{x}^* + \mathbf{\Delta x})
= \mathcal{N}\!\left(
    \mathbf{z}^{[i]};\;
    \mu_{z|x}^{[i]}={\mathbf{z}^{[i]}}^* + \mathbf{J}^{[i]}\mathbf{\Delta x},
    {\mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}}^{-1}
  \right)
\end{equation}

Since measurements are conditionally independent given the state,
the joint conditional likelihood
$p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}^*+\mathbf{\Delta x})$ over all $M$ measurements
factorizes into a product of Gaussian distributions of
each individual measurement. As a result, the joint conditional likelihood
$p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}^* + \mathbf{\Delta x})$ formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:joint-perturbated-state-conditional}
is also a Gaussian over the stacked mean and the block-diagonal information
matrix, where
$\mathbf{J} = \left[{\mathbf{J}^{[1]}}^T, \ldots, {\mathbf{J}^{[M]}}^T\right]^T\in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$
is the stacked Jacobian, $m = \sum_{i=1}^{M} m_i$, and
$\mathbf{\Omega}_z = \mathrm{diag}\!\left(\mathbf{\Omega}^{[1]}, \ldots, \mathbf{\Omega}^{[M]}\right)$
is the block-diagonal information matrix reflecting the conditional independence
of the measurements.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:joint-perturbated-state-conditional}
p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}^* + \mathbf{\Delta x})
= \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mathbf{z};
    \mu_{z|x} = \mathbf{z}^* + \mathbf{J} \mathbf{\Delta x},
    \mathbf{\Omega}_z^{-1}
  \right)
\end{equation}

Assuming an uninformative (uniform) prior on $\mathbf{\Delta x}$ around the
optimum $\mathbf{x}^*$, this corresponds to a zero information matrix with
a zero mean for the $\mathbf{\Delta x}$, as shown in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:joint-perturbated-state-distribution}.
The distribution over the state perturbation $\mathbf{\Delta x}$ is not
a separate variable, given that $\mathbf{\Delta x} = \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}^*$.
Applying the Gaussian chain rule~\cite{barfoot:book:2017},
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:joint-information-matrix}
defines the information matrix $\mathbf{\Omega}_{x,z}$
for the joint distribution over
$(\mathbf{\Delta x}, \mathbf{z})$.
The prior contributes no information to the top-left block, so
$\mathbf{\Omega}_{xx} = \mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{\Omega}_z \mathbf{J}$.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:joint-perturbated-state-distribution}
p(\mathbf{\Delta x}) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mathbf{\Delta x};\mathbf{0},\mathbf{\Omega}_{x}^{-1}\right),
\; \mathbf{\Omega}_{x} = \mathbf{0}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:joint-information-matrix}
\mathbf{\Omega}_{x,z} =
\begin{pmatrix}
  \mathbf{\Omega}_{x} + \mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{\Omega}_{z} \mathbf{J} &
  \mathbf{J}^{T}\mathbf{\Omega}_{z}\\
  \mathbf{\Omega}_{z}\mathbf{J} &
  \mathbf{\Omega}_{z}
\end{pmatrix} =
\begin{pmatrix}
  \mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{\Omega}_{z} \mathbf{J} &
  \mathbf{J}^{T}\mathbf{\Omega}_{z}\\
  \mathbf{\Omega}_{z}\mathbf{J} &
  \mathbf{\Omega}_{z}
\end{pmatrix} =
\begin{pmatrix}
  \mathbf{\Omega}_{xx} & \mathbf{\Omega}_{xz} \\
  \mathbf{\Omega}_{zx} & \mathbf{\Omega}_{zz}
\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}

When conditioning the state posterior $\mathbf{x}$ on the measurements $\mathbf{z}$ from the joint
probability and under the Gaussian assumption,
Sch\"{o}n and Lindsten~\cite{schon-lindsten:tech-report:2011}
demonstrate that the mean and the covariance matrix for the state posterior
given the measurements can be formulated by
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:state-posterior-mean}
and
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:state-posterior-covariance},
respectively. Since at the optimum state the residual
$\mathbf{z} - \mathbf{z}^* \simeq \mathbf{0}$, the correction term vanishes
in the mean of the state posterior given the measurements.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:state-posterior-mean}
\begin{aligned}
\mu_{x|z} & = \mu_x -
              \mathbf{\Omega}_{xx}^{-1} \mathbf{\Omega}_{xz}
              \left(\mathbf{z} - \mu_z\right)\\
& = \mathbf{x}^* -
    \mathbf{\Omega}_{xx}^{-1} \mathbf{\Omega}_{xz}
    \left(\mathbf{z} - \mathbf{z}^*\right)\\
& \simeq \mathbf{x}^*
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:state-posterior-covariance}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{\Sigma}_{x|z} & =
\mathbf{\Sigma}_{xx} - \mathbf{\Sigma}_{xz} \mathbf{\Sigma}_{zz}^{-1} \mathbf{\Sigma}_{zx}\\
& = \mathbf{\Omega}_{xx}^{-1}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Therefore, the covariance of the optimized state $\mathbf{x}^*$ is estimated by
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:state-covariance}, where
$\mathbf{\Omega}_{xx} = \mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{\Omega}_z \mathbf{J} = \mathbf{H}$
is the approximate Hessian recovered from the Gauss-Newton solver.
The approximation sign reflects the fact that $\mathbf{H}$ is itself a
first-order approximation to the true Hessian of the cost function,
obtained by discarding the second-order residual terms in the linearization.
The diagonal entries of $\mathbf{\Sigma}_{x|z}$ represent the marginal
variances of each state component, and the off-diagonal entries encode
the correlations induced by the measurement model.
This result requires $\mathbf{H}$ to have full column rank, \ie, the stacked
Jacobian $\mathbf{J}$ must be full column rank.
When this condition does not hold (\eg, when some state components are
unobservable given the available measurements), $\mathbf{H}$ is singular and
the covariance is undefined for those directions~\cite{barfoot:book:2017}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:state-covariance}
\mathbf{\Sigma}_{x|z}
= \mathbf{\Omega}_{xx}^{-1}
= \left(\mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{\Omega}_z \mathbf{J}\right)^{-1}
\approx \mathbf{H}^{-1}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Robust estimators}
\label{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-estimators}

The weighted non-linear least-squares algorithm formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:mle-log-likelihood}
assumes that all measurements follow a Gaussian noise model.
Under this assumption, the cost
$F(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{M} {\chi^2}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})$
grows quadratically with the residual error $\mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})$,
meaning that a single outlying measurement with a large ${\chi^2}^{[i]}$ can
dominate the sum and severely corrupt the optimum.
M-estimators address this limitation by replacing the quadratic penalty with
a robust loss function $\rho(\cdot)$ that down-weights large residuals,
resulting in the robust cost $F_{\rho}(\mathbf{x})$ presented in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-cost},
where $s^{[i]}$ is the scalar Mahalanobis distance of the $i$-th
measurement~\cite{huber:book:1981}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-cost}
F_{\rho}(\mathbf{x}) =
\sum_{i=1}^{M} \rho\!\left(s^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})\right),
\;
s^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}) = \sqrt{{\chi^2}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})}
\end{equation}

The standard least-squares cost $F(\mathbf{x})$ is recovered as
a special case of $F_{\rho}(\mathbf{x})$ when $\rho(s) = s^2$.
Consider the gradient of the standard least-squares with respect to $\mathbf{x}$
presented in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:standard-gradient},
using the identity
$\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}\!\left(\mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})^T \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]} \mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})\right) = 2 \cdot {\mathbf{J}^{[i]}}^T \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]} \mathbf{e}^{[i]}$
because $\mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]}$ is symmetric.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:standard-gradient}
\begin{aligned}
\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} F
& = \sum_{i=1}^{M}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}
    \!\left(\mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})^T \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]} \mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})\right)
  = \sum_{i=1}^{M} 2 \cdot {\mathbf{J}^{[i]}}^T \mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]} \mathbf{e}^{[i]}\\
& = \sum_{i=1}^{M} \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} F^{[i]}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

When expanding the gradient of the robust cost
$F_{\rho}(\mathbf{x})$ presented in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-gradient},
the derivation chain rule is applied using the gradient identity from
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:standard-gradient},
and the influence of the robust estimator $\rho$ appears as a scalar weight $w$
of each measurement factor in $F_{\rho}(\mathbf{x})$ when compared to
the gradient of the standard cost $F(\mathbf{x})$.
This influence results in a re-weighted least-squares problem,
introducing the effective information matrix for each measurement factor
$\mathbf{\tilde{\Omega}}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}_k)$ formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:effective-info}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-gradient}
\begin{aligned}
\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} F_{\rho} =
\sum_{i=1}^{M}
\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}
\!\left(\rho\!\left(s^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})\right)\right)
& = \sum_{i=1}^{M}
    \rho'\!\left(s^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})\right)
    \cdot
    \frac{\partial \left(\sqrt{{\chi^2}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})}\right)}{\partial \mathbf{x}}
  = \sum_{i=1}^{M}
    \frac{\rho'\!\left(s^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})\right)}{2 \cdot s^{[i]}(\mathbf{x})}
    \cdot
    \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} F^{[i]}\\
& = \sum_{i=1}^{M}
    w(s^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}))
    \cdot
    \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} F^{[i]}, \;
    w(s) = \frac{\rho'(s)}{2s}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:effective-info}
\mathbf{\tilde{\Omega}}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}_k)
= w^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}_k)\,\mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]},
\end{equation}

Subsequently,
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-hb}
defines the robust approximate Hessian matrix $\mathbf{\tilde{H}}$ and
gradient vector $\mathbf{\tilde{b}}$ for the Gauss-Newton
method to solve least-squares problems with robust estimators.
The only modification with respect to the standard formulation is the
substitution $\mathbf{\Omega}^{[i]} \leftarrow \mathbf{\tilde{\Omega}}^{[i]}$,
so any sparse linear solver used for the standard Gauss-Newton step can be
reused without modification.
\algref{alg:distance-maps:background:least-squares:gauss-newton:robust} details
the steps for a single iteration of the Gauss-Newton minimization procedure
with robust estimators.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-hb}
\mathbf{\tilde{H}}
= \sum_{i=1}^{M}
  {\mathbf{J}^{[i]}}^T
  \mathbf{\tilde{\Omega}}^{[i]}
  \mathbf{J}^{[i]},
\;
\mathbf{\tilde{b}}
= \sum_{i=1}^{M}
  {\mathbf{J}^{[i]}}^T
  \mathbf{\tilde{\Omega}}^{[i]}\,
  \mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}_k).
\end{equation}

\input{algorithms/distance-maps/gauss-newton_robust.tex}

At convergence, the robust approximate Hessian $\mathbf{\tilde{H}}$ provides
a covariance estimate analogous to
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:state-covariance}, as presented
in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-covariance}.
Given that outlier factors receive $w^{[i]} \approx 0$, they contribute
negligibly to $\mathbf{\tilde{H}}$, not inflating the reported uncertainty of
the inlier-driven solution~\cite{huber:book:1981}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-covariance}
\mathbf{\tilde{\Sigma}}_{x|z}
\approx \mathbf{\tilde{H}}^{-1}.
\end{equation}

Lastly, \tabref{tab:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-losses}
summarizes popular choices for the robust loss function $\rho$, together with
their first-order derivative  $\psi(s) = \rho'(s)$ and
weight function $w(s) = \psi(s)/(2s)$~\cite{babin:icra:2019}.
The tuning constant $c$ controls the threshold between the inlier region,
where the loss behaves near-quadratically, and the outlier region,
where large residuals are down-weighted.

\subsubsection{Non-Euclidean spaces}
\label{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold}

State estimation in robotics frequently involves state spaces that are not
flat Euclidean spaces. For example, robot poses include orientations that
are elements of the special orthogonal group $SO(n)$ ($SO(2)$ and $SO(3)$
for 2D~and 3D~rotation matrices), or full rigid-body transformations that
are elements of the special Euclidean group $SE(n)$, combining rotations
and translations in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ ($SE(2)$ and $SE(3)$ for 2D~and 3D~poses).
These groups are smooth manifolds that do not support
standard Euclidean addition and subtraction of state variables.
Thus, the geometry of the state space must be accounted for explicitly during
optimization~\cite{barfoot:book:2017,grisetti:robotics:2020}.

A smooth manifold $\mathcal{M}$ is a space that locally resembles a Euclidean
space $\mathbb{R}^n$. At each point $\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{M}$,
the \emph{tangent space}
$\mathcal{T}_{\mathbf{x}}(\mathcal{M})\cong\mathbb{R}^{n}$ provides
the appropriate linear structure for computing increments,
required for iterative least-squares minimization.
A small perturbation $\mathbf{\Delta x}\in\mathbb{R}^{n}$ expressed
in the tangent space is mapped back onto the manifold through the
\emph{exponential mapping}, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-exp-plus-operator}.
The operator $\oplus$ replaces standard Euclidean
addition throughout the optimization, ensuring that every iteration remains on
$\mathcal{M}$, while preserving geometric constraints intrinsic to
the state representation (\eg, the orthogonality constraint on rotation
matrices, or the unit-norm constraint on quaternions).
The inverse operation maps an element $\mathbf{y}\in\mathcal{M}$ back to
the tangent space at $\mathbf{x}$ through the \emph{logarithmic map},
as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-log-minus-operator}~\cite{barfoot:book:2017}.
In the Euclidean case ($\mathcal{M} = \mathbb{R}^n$), the operators $\oplus$ and
$\ominus$ reduce to standard vector addition and subtraction operations.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-exp-plus-operator}
\mathbf{x} \oplus \mathbf{\Delta x}
\triangleq
\mathrm{exp}_{\mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{\Delta x})
\in \mathcal{M}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-log-minus-operator}
\mathbf{y} \ominus \mathbf{x}
\triangleq
\mathrm{log}_{\mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{y})
\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathbf{x}}(\mathcal{M})
\end{equation}

Given the current state estimate $\mathbf{x}_k\in\mathcal{M}$ and
a tangent-space perturbation $\mathbf{\Delta x}\in\mathbb{R}^{n}$,
the first-order Taylor expansion of the residual is formulated by
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-linearization}.
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-error} represents the
\emph{logarithmic map} of the predicted measurement
$\mathbf{h}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}_k)$ into the tangent space at the reference
measurement $\mathbf{z}^{[i]}$.
The \emph{manifold Jacobian} (see
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-jacobian})
is the derivative of the residual \gls{wrt}
$\mathbf{\Delta x}\in\mathbb{R}^{n}$, evaluated at the origin of the tangent
space (equivalent to $\mathbf{\Delta x} = 0$)~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020}.
The choice of left versus right-sided perturbation update in Lie groups affects
the form of the Jacobian. Hence, \emph{exponential} and \emph{logarithmic map}
operations should be consistent throughout the implementations.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-linearization}
\mathbf{e}^{[i]}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_k \oplus \mathbf{\Delta x}\right)
\approx
\mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}_k)
+ \mathbf{J}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}_k) \mathbf{\Delta x}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-error}
\mathbf{e}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}_k) =
\mathbf{h}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}_k) \ominus \mathbf{z}^{[i]}
\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathbf{z}^{[i]}}(\mathcal{M})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-jacobian}
\mathbf{J}^{[i]}(\mathbf{x}) =
\left.
  \frac{\partial \mathbf{e}^{[i]}\!\left(\mathbf{x} \oplus \mathbf{\Delta x}\right)}
        {\partial \mathbf{\Delta x}}
\right|_{\mathbf{\Delta x}=\mathbf{0}}
\in \mathbb{R}^{m_i \times n}
\end{equation}

As a result, the definition of the approximate Hessian and gradient vectors
based on the Jacobian computed \gls{wrt} the tangent-space perturbation rather
than a Euclidean increment (see
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-jacobian})
and the error from the \emph{logarithmic map} of the measurement and prediction
estimation make the iterative least-squares on manifolds similar to
\algref{alg:distance-maps:background:least-squares:gauss-newton} and
\algref{alg:distance-maps:background:least-squares:gauss-newton:robust},
differing only in how $\mathbf{H}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are computed.
Once $\mathbf{\Delta x}^*$ has been computed in the tangent space through
standard linear solvers as in the original Gauss-Newton method, the state is
retracted back onto the manifold via the \emph{exponential map}, as formulated
in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-update}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold-update}
\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k \oplus \mathbf{\Delta x}^*
= \mathrm{exp}_{\mathbf{x}_k}\!\left(\mathrm{solve}\!\left(
  -\mathbf{H}_k^{-1} \mathbf{b}_k
\right)\right)
\end{equation}

\section{Registration Methodology}
\label{sec:distance-maps:alignment}

This section presents the proposed approach for 2D~point cloud registration
using distance maps, formulated as a non-linear least-squares problem and
solved via the Gauss-Newton algorithm on the $SE(2)$ manifold.
First, \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps} introduces the distance map
representation considered in this work, its spatial derivatives, the associated
representation levels, and the data structures used for computation and querying.
Next, \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:problem} states the registration
problem, defining the pose parametrization and the manifold update. Then,
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt} derives the point-to-point error
formulation and its Jacobian matrix via right-sided perturbation on $SE(2)$.
Lastly, \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl} derives the point-to-plane
error formulation and its Jacobian, together with the surface normal
estimation procedure used to extract normals from the moving point cloud.

\subsection{Distance maps}
\label{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps}

Distance maps, also referred in the literature as distance transforms,
are scalar fields that associate to every cell of a discrete grid
the Euclidean distance to the nearest element of a given reference set.
In the context of point cloud registration, the reference set is
the target point cloud and the distance map is precomputed only once before
the iterative optimization begins, in contrast to classical
\gls{icp} methods~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991}.
This precomputation encodes data association implicitly,
removing the explicit nearest-neighbor search that traditional
\gls{icp} methods~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991} perform at
every iteration and replacing it with a constant-time grid $O(1)$ lookup.

Therefore, \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:overview} defines the
distance map over the reference point cloud and discusses how it removes the
explicit data association step required by classical
\gls{icp} methods~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991}.
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:derivation} derives the first- and
second-order spatial derivatives of the distance map, providing both
analytical expressions and finite-difference approximations used in
the implementation of the proposed approaches.
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:representation} describes the three
representation levels of increasing precomputed information content considered
in this work. Lastly, \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:computation}
details the computation pipeline and the data structures underlying each variant
of the distance map representations considered in the scope of this PhD~Thesis.

\subsubsection{Overview}
\label{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:overview}

Let $\mathcal{P} = \{\mathbf{p}_1, \mathbf{p}_2, \dots, \mathbf{p}_n\} \subset \mathbb{R}^2$
represent a reference 2D~point cloud,
comprising $n$ points acquired by a planar laser scanner.
These points are expressed in the sensor coordinate frame $S$,
where each point is $\mathbf{p}_i = (x_i, y_i)^T$.
The distance map $\mathcal{D}: \mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}_0^+$ is defined
as the function that maps any query point $\mathbf{y}\in\mathbb{R}^2$ to
its Euclidean distance to the nearest point in $\mathcal{P}$, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:definition},
where $\mathbf{p}^*(\mathbf{y}) = \argmin_{\mathbf{p}_i \in \mathcal{P}}
\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{p}_i\|_2$ is the nearest-neighbor in
the reference cloud $\mathcal{P}$.
Note that $\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y}) \geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{y}$, making
this an \emph{unsigned distance function}, as opposed to
\glspl{sdf} used in volumetric reconstruction. An important property of
\emph{unsigned distance fields} is that they are continuous everywhere,
but not everywhere differentiable~\cite{jones:tvcg:2006}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:definition}
\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y}) =
\{ \mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}_0^+: \| \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{p}^*(\mathbf{y}) \|_2\}
\end{equation}

As discussed in \secref{sec:distance-maps:background:registration},
classical \gls{icp}-based registration
approaches~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991} implement a
two-step iterative process: data association and optimization steps.
These methods require reevaluating the correspondences at every iteration,
since the pose estimate evolves and changes the transformed positions of the
moving points, potentially altering the data association results from which
result the error factors.
In contrast, the distance map formulation removes this coupling entirely.
The distance map $\mathcal{D}$ is precomputed over a discrete grid
with a given cell resolution $\delta$ (m/px) covering the environment from
the reference cloud $\mathcal{P}$ prior to the registration process.
Subsequently, this structure is queried during optimization without requiring
any further reference to $\mathcal{P}$. The structure $\mathcal{D}$ encodes
information on the nearest-neighbor point from $\mathcal{P}$ in each cell of the
distance map. Data association is thus resolved implicitly within
the error function evaluation itself, transforming a moving point
by the current pose estimate. At the resulting point location,
looking up into $\mathcal{D}$ yields information
(\eg, distance, its derivatives) on the nearest reference point
in $O(1)$, without explicit correspondence search.

\subsubsection{Spatial derivatives}
\label{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:derivation}

While the distance map $\mathcal{D}$ is continuous everywhere but not everywhere
differentiable~\cite{jones:tvcg:2006}, its spatial derivatives
exist almost everywhere and are central to the registration error formulations
derived in \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt} and
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl}.
This section derives the first- and second-order spatial derivatives
of $\mathcal{D}$, providing both analytical expressions and their
finite-difference approximations used in our implementation.

\paragraph{First-order derivative (gradient vector)}
The gradient $\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y}) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ of the
distance map at a query point $\mathbf{y}$ can be obtained analytically
wherever $\mathcal{D}$ is smooth, \ie, at points admitting a unique
nearest-neighbor in $\mathcal{P}$. At such a point,
$\mathbf{p}^*(\mathbf{y})$ is unique and the gradient is a unit vector pointing
from the nearest reference point toward $\mathbf{y}$, as given in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:1st-deriv:analytical-derivation},
which results from differentiating
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:definition}.
Indeed, the unit-norm property $\|\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}\|_2 = 1$ holds
almost everywhere for \emph{unsigned distance fields},
with the exception of points equidistant from two or more reference points in
$\mathcal{P}$, where the gradient is not defined
(\eg, at the center of a circumference).
This property represents the Eikonal equation, a fundamental property
of Euclidean distance fields~\cite{jones:tvcg:2006}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:1st-deriv:analytical-derivation}
\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y}) =
\frac{\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{p}^*(\mathbf{y})}
    {\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{p}^*(\mathbf{y})\|_2},
\; \|\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y})\|_2 = 1
\end{equation}

In the discrete grid setting, the gradient is estimated numerically using
central finite differences along the $y_1$ and $y_2$ directions of
$\mathbf{y} = (y_1, y_2)^T$ on the distance map $\mathcal{D}$, as
formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:1st-deriv:numerical-derivation},
with cell resolution $\delta > 0$ and one-sided (forward or backward)
differences used at grid borders.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:1st-deriv:numerical-derivation}
\left.\frac{\partial \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_1}\right|_{\mathbf{y}}
\approx
\frac{\mathcal{D}(y_1 + \delta,\, y_2) -
      \mathcal{D}(y_1 - \delta,\, y_2)}{2\delta},
\;
\left.\frac{\partial \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_2}\right|_{\mathbf{y}}
\approx
\frac{\mathcal{D}(y_1,\, y_2 + \delta) -
      \mathcal{D}(y_1,\, y_2 - \delta)}{2\delta}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Second-order derivative (Hessian matrix)}

The Hessian matrix $\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}(\mathbf{y})\in\mathbb{R}^{2\times2}$
is the symmetric matrix of second-order partial derivatives of $\mathcal{D}$, as
formulated in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:2nd-deriv:H}, given that
$\frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_1 \partial y_2} = \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_2 \partial y_1}$.
This Hessian matrix represents also the Jacobian matrix's transpose of
the analytical gradient $\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y})$, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:2nd-deriv:H-Jgrad}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:2nd-deriv:H}
\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}(\mathbf{y}) =
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_1^2} &
\frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_1 \partial y_2} \\
\frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_2 \partial y_1} &
\frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_2^2}
\end{bmatrix}_{\mathbf{y}} =
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{(y_2 - p_y^*(\mathbf{y}))^2}{\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{p}^*(\mathbf{y})\|_2^3} &
-\frac{(y_1 - p_x^*(\mathbf{y}))(y_2 - p_y^*(\mathbf{y}))}{\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{p}^*(\mathbf{y})\|_2^3}\\
-\frac{(y_1 - p_x^*(\mathbf{y}))(y_2 - p_y^*(\mathbf{y}))}{\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{p}^*(\mathbf{y})\|_2^3} &
\frac{(y_1 - p_x^*(\mathbf{y}))^2}{\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{p}^*(\mathbf{y})\|_2^3}
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:2nd-deriv:H-Jgrad}
\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}(\mathbf{y}) =
\mathbf{J}_{\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y})}^T
\end{equation}

In practice, $\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}$ is computed numerically as the centered
finite difference of the normalized neighbor gradients
($\hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}$), as shown in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:2nd-deriv:H:numerical},
where $(\cdot)_{y_j}$ denotes the $j$-th component of the enclosed vector.
This numerical approximation is the discretization implemented in the proposed
approach for distance map-based 2D~point cloud registration
when requiring 2nd-order derivatives.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:2nd-deriv:H:numerical}
\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}(\mathbf{y}) \approx
\frac{1}{2\delta}
\begin{bmatrix}
\left(
    \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}(y_1{+}\delta,\,y_2) -
    \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}(y_1{-}\delta,\,y_2)
\right)_{y_1} &
\left(
    \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}(y_1,\,y_2{+}\delta) -
    \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}(y_1,\,y_2{-}\delta)
\right)_{y_1} \\[8pt]
\left(
    \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}(y_1{+}\delta,\,y_2) -
    \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}(y_1{-}\delta,\,y_2)
\right)_{y_2} &
\left(
    \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}(y_1,\,y_2{+}\delta) -
    \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}(y_1,\,y_2{-}\delta)
\right)_{y_2}
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Representation levels}
\label{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:representation}

As for the representation of the distance map required for the proposed
registration process, each cell of the discrete grid may store different amounts
of precomputed information about the distance field. Therefore, we consider
three representation levels of increasing information and memory cost.
The choice of the representation determines which quantities are available at
$O(1)$ query cost during registration, and which must be recomputed on the fly
from neighboring cells or analytical expressions.

\paragraph{Level~0 -- $\mathcal{M}_0$ (nearest-neighbor index only)}
Each cell stores only the integer index $k^*(\mathbf{y})$ of the
nearest reference point in $\mathcal{P}$
(see \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:representations:level0}).
The scalar distance $\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y})$, the gradient vector
$\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y})$, and the Hessian matrix
$\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}(\mathbf{y})$ are not precomputed nor stored explicitly.
These values may be recovered at query time through their analytical expressions
discussed in \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:derivation}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:representations:level0}
\mathcal{M}_0(\mathbf{y}) = k^*(\mathbf{y})
= \argmin_{i \in \{1,\dots,n\}}
\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{p}_i\|_2 \in \mathbb{Z}
\end{equation}

Furthermore, $\mathcal{M}_0$ provides the most memory-efficient data
structure for data association, and may serve as a drop-in replacement
for kd-tree search~\cite{bentley:cacm:1975}
used in classical \gls{icp} methods~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,
chen-medioni:icra:1991}. This modification reduces the per-query cost from
$O(\log n)$ to $O(1)$ at the expense of
the precomputation step of $\mathcal{M}_0$.

\paragraph{Level~1 -- $\mathcal{M}_1$ (distance and first-order derivatives)}
Each cell stores the scalar distance $\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y})$ together
with the two components of its spatial gradient
$\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y}) \in \mathbb{R}^2$, as shown in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:representations:level1}.
The integer index $k^*(\mathbf{y})$ of the nearest reference point in
$\mathcal{P}$
(see \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:representations:level0})
is optional for $\mathcal{M}_1$.
Hence, $\mathcal{M}_1$ requires three or four scalar values
per cell, depending on whether the $k^*(\mathbf{y})$ index is included into
$\mathcal{M}_1(\mathbf{y})$.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:representations:level1}
\mathcal{M}_1(\mathbf{y}) =
\left(
    \mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y}),
    \frac{\partial \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_1}(\mathbf{y}),
    \frac{\partial \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_2}(\mathbf{y})
\right)
\end{equation}

Subsequently, the scalar distance $\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y})$ and
the gradient vector $\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y})$ are precomputed
based on \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:definition} and using the
central finite difference scheme of
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:1st-deriv:numerical-derivation},
respectively.
The Hessian matrix $\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}(\mathbf{y})$ is not precomputed nor
stored explicitly. This value may be recovered at query time through its
analytical expression or through the central finite difference approximation
from neighbor cells of the normalized gradient discussed in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:derivation}.

\paragraph{Level~2 -- $\mathcal{M}_2$
(distance, first-, and second-order derivatives)}
Although the analysis in \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:derivation}
demonstrates that the analytical expression for $\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}$
leads to a symmetric matrix, its centered finite difference approximation
presented in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:2nd-deriv:H:numerical} does
not ensure that property.
Therefore, each cell of $\mathcal{M}_2$ additionally stores
all entries of the approximated Hessian matrix
$\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}(\mathbf{y})\in\mathbb{R}^{2\times2}$, as shown in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:representations:level2}.
Similarly to $\mathcal{M}_1$, the integer index $k^*(\mathbf{y})$
(see \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:representations:level0})
is optional for the $\mathcal{M}_2$ representation level.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:representations:level2}
\mathcal{M}_2(\mathbf{y}) =
\left(
    \mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y}),
    \frac{\partial \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_1}(\mathbf{y}),
    \frac{\partial \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_2}(\mathbf{y}),
    \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_1^2}(\mathbf{y}),
    \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_1 \partial y_2}(\mathbf{y}),
    \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_2 \partial y_1}(\mathbf{y}),
    \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{D}}{\partial y_2^2}(\mathbf{y})
\right)
\end{equation}

The representation level $\mathcal{M}_2$ makes available all values throughout
the registration process, thanks to the precomputation of the scalar distance
$\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y})$ stored in the discrete grid of the distance and based
on \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:definition}, while both the
gradient vector $\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y})$ and the Hessian matrix
$\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}(\mathbf{y})$ are computed by centered finite
differences from neighbor cells based on
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:1st-deriv:numerical-derivation} and
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:2nd-deriv:H:numerical}, respectively.

\subsubsection{Computation and data structures}
\label{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:computation}
Independently of the representation level, the computation of $\mathcal{M}_l$
is based on a wavefront propagation algorithm using a best-first priority queue,
analogous to Dijkstra's shortest-path algorithm~\cite{dijkstra:nm:1959}.
All variants considered in our approach share the same high-level pipeline:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item initialize the grid based on its resolution $\delta$ (m/px),
      the bounding box of $\mathcal{P}$, optional fixed map size $L$ (m), and
      optional truncation distance $d_{\max}$ (m);
\item seed the priority queue with the grid cells occupied by reference
      points, initializing their distance to zero;
\item propagate the integer index $k^*(\mathbf{y})$ of the nearest
      reference point and its corresponding distance value outward via
      best-first wavefront expansion through the $\mathcal{N}_8$ neighborhood
      (8-connectivity) of each cell in the priority queue for the whole map or
      until $d_{\max}$ is achieved from the nearest reference point;
\item depending on the required representation level
      $\mathcal{M}_l$, execute post-processing passes to compute the
      gradient $\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}$ and the Hessian
      $\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}$ through centered finite differences
      (see \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:derivation}).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Coordinates conversion}
All implemented variants in our approach for distance map-based representations
use the same affine mapping between metric coordinates
$\mathbf{y}\in\mathbb{R}^2$ \gls{wrt} the sensor coordinate frame and
integer grid indices $(r,c)\in\mathbb{Z}^2$,
and vice-versa. Given $\mathbf{y}_{\mathcal{M},\min}\in\mathbb{R}^2$,
the metric coordinates corresponding to the cell
$(r,c)_{\mathcal{M},\min}=(0,0)$ of the grid, determined during
the initialization procedure of the representation level $\mathcal{M}_l$,
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:conversion:global2local} defines
the mapping from the sensor coordinates to the grid indices.
Note that negative indices or values that exceed the grid size
represent invalid cells outside the data structure.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:conversion:global2local}
(r, c) =
\left\lfloor\frac{\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{y}_{\mathcal{M},\min}}{\delta}\right\rfloor
\in\mathbb{Z}^2
\end{equation}

Furthermore,
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:conversion:global2local:real}
formulates a sub-pixel (non-integer) version of
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:conversion:global2local}, omitting
the floor operator. We use this version for bilinear interpolation at
query time, enabling sub-pixel accuracy for the distance and derivatives
values returned to the registration optimizer.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:conversion:global2local:real}
(r, c)_{\mathbb{R}} =
\frac{\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{y}_{\mathcal{M},\min}}{\delta}
\in \mathbb{R}
\end{equation}

Lastly, the inverse mapping from grid indices to metric coordinates is obtained
by multiplying the indices by the resolution $\delta$ and offsetting by
$\mathbf{y}_{\mathcal{M},\min}$, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:conversion:local2global}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:conversion:local2global}
\mathbf{y} = \delta \cdot (r, c)^T + \mathbf{y}_{\mathcal{M},\min} \in \mathbb{R}^2
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Data structure storage}
Independently of the required
$\mathcal{M}_l$, we store the discrete grid in one of two memory layouts.
Under \emph{dense storage} ($\mathcal{M}_{l,\mathrm{dense}}$), the grid is
allocated as a contiguous array of cells (generic
Eigen\footnote{\label{note:eigen}\url{https://libeigen.gitlab.io/}} matrix in
C++), where every cell in the grid is populated during precomputation.
Under \emph{sparse storage}, only cells satisfying
$\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{y})\leq d_{\max}$ are stored, since moving points
projecting further than $d_{\max}$ distance from any reference point
are excluded from the registration cost. This truncated representation is
analogous to the \glslink{tsdf}{\glsentryfull{tsdf}} used in volumetric
reconstruction~\cite{curless-levoy:siggraph:1996},
where only a narrow band of the distance values is retained around a surface.

Moreover, we implement the sparse grid in C++ as a hash table using the container
\texttt{std::unordered\_map}\footnote{\label{note:hash-table}\url{https://en.cppreference.com/w/cpp/container/unordered_map.html}}.
The hash key for a cell $(r,c)$ is computed by encoding both integer indices
(\texttt{int32}) into a single 64-bit unsigned integer, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:computation:hash-key}, where
$\ll$ denotes a bit-wise left shift and $|$ denotes a bit-wise OR.
An important property of \texttt{std::unordered\_map} is that the container does
not invalidate existing element pointers upon rehashing, allowing the
priority queue to store raw cell pointers safely throughout the entire
wavefront propagation. After propagation, a rehash call consolidates
the bucket layout before the gradient and Hessian post-processing passes
iterate over all stored entries.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:computation:hash-key}
\mathrm{key}(r, c) =
\bigl(\texttt{uint64}(r) \ll 32\bigr)
\;\big|\;
\bigl(\texttt{uint64}(c) \;\&\; \texttt{0xFFFFFFFF}\bigr)
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Computation of the data structures}

First, \algref{alg:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:initialization} details the
initialization of the grid data structure.
This structure is parametrized by a map size ($L$) and a truncation distance
($d_{\max}$). If $L > 0$, the metric bounding box \gls{wrt} the local coordinate
frame of the sensor for the distance map is symmetrically defined as
$[-L, L]$. Otherwise, the bounding box is computed from
the reference point cloud $\mathcal{P}$ by iterating over all valid points and
accumulating the minimum and maximum coordinates.
Then, the grid dimensions in pixels are derived from the bounding box.
If $d_{\max} > 0$ is set and no explicit map size is
provided, the bounding box is expanded by $d_{\max}$ on both sides before
converting to pixel cells, ensuring that the truncation band is fully covered
around the point cloud boundary.
Otherwise, the bounding box is converted to pixel cell counts using
the resolution $\delta$ based on the original metric bounding box limits.
Finally, the grid is resized to the computed dimensions.

\algref{alg:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:nano} details
the full construction of the \emph{Nano} variant.
First, the entire grid $\mathcal{M}_{0,\mathrm{dense}}$ is filled with $-1$, representing
unvisited cells, and the priority queue $\mathcal{Q}$ is cleared.
Next, the reference point cloud $\mathcal{P}$ is projected onto the grid
using the coordinate conversion $\mathrm{global2local}(\cdot)$ of
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:conversion:global2local}.
Invalid points and points whose projected cell $(r, c)$ lies outside
the grid are skipped. For each remaining point $\mathbf{p}_i$,
the corresponding cell is assigned the index $i$.
A pointer to the cell is then pushed into $\mathcal{Q}$ with distance
$d = 0$, which sets the priority of the queue entry.
Since $\mathcal{M}_{0,\mathrm{dense}}$ does not store the distance value, the queue
entry must carry it explicitly alongside the cell pointer, so that
$\mathcal{Q}$ correctly orders cells during wavefront propagation.

At each iteration of the wavefront propagation, the element $q_k$ with
the smallest distance is popped from $\mathcal{Q}$. A negative stored nearest-neighbor
index ($k < 0$) indicates an entry overwritten by a closer parent that surpasses
the truncation distance $d_{\max}$ when set ($d_{\max} > 0$), and is therefore
discarded. For each cell $(r', c')$ in the $\mathcal{N}_8(r_k, c_k)$
neighborhood, the neighbor cell is updated to the closer parent provided
the truncation condition is satisfied, \ie,
\ $d_k < d_{\max}$ when truncation is enabled, or
unconditionally true when the full map is computed.
The first case is when the cell is unvisited ($o < 0$). The other case is
if already visited ($o \geq 0$) but with a stored distance greater than
the candidate ($d_o > d_k$). In both cases, the updated cell is pushed into
$\mathcal{Q}$, allowing the wavefront propagation to correct sub-optimal
assignments from earlier iterations.

The \emph{Standard} variant detailed in
\algref{alg:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:dense} follows a similar construction
pipeline as the \emph{Nano} variant
(see \algref{alg:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:nano}), with two key
differences arising from the more complete cell data structure.
Since the distance is now part of the cell itself in $\mathcal{M}_{1,\mathrm{dense}}$ and
$\mathcal{M}_{2,\mathrm{dense}}$ representation levels, the priority queue $\mathcal{Q}$
does not need to carry the distance separately, accessing directly from the
representation of the distance map.
The other difference is the distinction between unvisited and already-visited
neighbor cells, which required an explicit branch in
\algref{alg:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:nano}, is no longer necessary.
Since all cells are initialized with $+\inf$, the single condition
$\left(d_k < d_{\max} \textbf{ or } d_{\max} \leq 0\right) \textbf{ and }d_o > d_k$
handles both cases uniformly, where an unvisited cell holds a $+\inf$ distance,
while a previously visited cell is updated only if a closer parent is found.
After the wavefront terminates, the gradient
$\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}$ is computed over the entire dense grid via the
central finite difference scheme of
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:1st-deriv:numerical-derivation}.
If the $\mathcal{M}_{2,\mathrm{dense}}$ representation is requested, a second
post-processing pass computes the Hessian $\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}$
via \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:2nd-deriv:H:numerical}.
Both passes use one-sided (forward or backward) differences at grid borders.

As for the distance computation on the \emph{Sparse} variant described in
\algref{alg:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:sparse}, its construction differs
from the \emph{Standard} variant
(see \algref{alg:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:dense}) in three steps arising
from the \texttt{std::unordered\_map} storage layout. The
\texttt{std::unordered\_map} is not pre-allocated for the entire map, in order
to maintain a sparse storage layout when the truncation distance is set
($d_{\max} > 0$). Instead, the implemented wrapper around
\texttt{std::unordered\_map} defines a default cell whenever a queried index
$(r, c)$ is not yet present in the hash table.
Next, each entry pushed into the priority queue $\mathcal{Q}$ carries more
information than in the \emph{Standard} variant: a pointer to the
cell $\mathcal{M}_{1|2,\mathrm{sparse}}(r, c)$, the current cell index
$\{(r, c)\}$, and the parent nearest-neighbor index $\{(r_p, c_p)\}$.
This implementation avoids additional hash queries at every propagation step.
Lastly, once the wavefront terminates and before precomputing the derivatives,
a rehash call is issued to consolidate the internal bucket layout of the
\texttt{std::unordered\_map} to match the final number of populated
cells, minimizing the load factor and reducing hash collision overhead
for the subsequent read-intensive derivative passes.

\tabref{tab:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps} summarizes
the three distance map variants considered in this PhD~Thesis in
terms of storage layout, memory occupation, query lookup complexity, and
truncation requirements.
The \emph{Nano} variant stores a single integer index per cell, resulting
in the lowest memory footprint of $O(N_G)$.
Both the \emph{Standard} and \emph{Sparse} variants store between $3$
and $8$ scalar values per cell depending on the representation level
$\mathcal{M}_{1|2}$ and whether the optional nearest-neighbor index
$k^*(\mathbf{y})$ is included.
While the \emph{Standard} variant allocates the full grid as a dense
array with $O(N_G)$ memory, the \emph{Sparse} variant backed by
\texttt{std::unordered\_map} populates only the $N_d \leq N_G$ cells
within the truncation band, making it the most memory-efficient choice
when $d_{\max}$ is set, in general.
All three variants provide $O(1)$ query lookup, though the \emph{Sparse}
variant achieves this only on average, with worst-case $O(N_d)$ under
hash collision, mitigated by the post-propagation rehash discussed previously.

\subsection{Problem formulation}
\label{sec:distance-maps:alignment:problem}

Let $\mathbf{z}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$, $i = 1, \dots, M$, represent
the 2D~point measurements from the moving (current) point cloud.
Similarly to the reference cloud $\mathcal{P}$, the moving cloud is also
acquired by a 2D~laser scanner and expressed in the sensor coordinate frame $S$.
The goal of 2D~point cloud registration is to find the robot pose
$\mathbf{x} \in SE(2)$ that best aligns the moving cloud to the reference cloud.
The robot pose is represented as a homogeneous transformation on the $SE(2)$
manifold, as formulates in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:x:pose},
where $\mathbf{R}(\theta) \in SO(2)$ is the 2D orthonormal rotation matrix
(see \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:x:rotation}) and
$\mathbf{t} = (t_x, t_y)^T \in \mathbb{R}^2$ is the translation vector.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:x:pose}
\mathbf{x} = \left[\mathbf{R} \mid \mathbf{t}\right] \in SE(2)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:x:rotation}
\mathbf{R}(\theta) =
\begin{bmatrix} r_{1,1} & r_{1,2} \\ r_{2,1} & r_{2,2} \end{bmatrix} =
\begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) & -\sin(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} \in SO(2)
\end{equation}

Furthermore, let $\mathbf{T}_S^R \in SE(2)$ denote the known rigid-body
transformation of the sensor \gls{wrt} the robot frame (the extrinsic
calibration of the laser scanner), as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:sensor}. Then,
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:sensor:inv} formulates the inverse
transformation for the extrinsic parameters of the sensor ($\mathbf{T}_R^S$).
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:sensor}
\mathbf{T}_S^R = \left[\mathbf{R}_S^R \mid \mathbf{t}_S^R\right] \in SE(2)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:sensor:inv}
\mathbf{T}_R^S =
\left[\mathbf{R}_R^S \mid \mathbf{t}_R^S\right] =
\left[{\mathbf{R}_S^R}^T \mid -{\mathbf{R}_S^R}^T\mathbf{t}_S^R\right]
\in SE(2)
\end{equation}

As a result, the moving point $\mathbf{z}_i$ expressed in the robot frame is
formulated by \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:moving-pt:robot}, while
the same point transformed by the current pose estimate $\mathbf{x}$ is defined
by \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:moving-pt:projected}.
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:moving-pt:projected:robot}
presents the projected moving point $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_{i}$ expressed in the
sensor frame, considering the current pose estimate $\mathbf{x}$.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:moving-pt:robot}
\mathbf{z}_{R,i} = \mathbf{T}_S^R \mathbf{z}_i
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:moving-pt:projected}
\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_{R,i} = \mathbf{x} \mathbf{T}_S^R \mathbf{z}_i =
\mathbf{x} \mathbf{z}_{R,i}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:moving-pt:projected:robot}
\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_{i} = \mathbf{T}_R^S \mathbf{x} \mathbf{T}_S^R \mathbf{z}_i =
\mathbf{T}_R^S \tilde{\mathbf{z}}_{R,i}
\end{equation}

Since the optimization variable $\mathbf{x}$ lives on the $SE(2)$ manifold,
incremental updates cannot be performed by simple Euclidean addition,
as discussed in \secref{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold}.
A perturbation
$\mathbf{\Delta x} = (\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta\theta)^T \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is
defined in the tangent (Euclidean) space with the \emph{exponential} and
\emph{logarithmic mappings} defined as
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:x:exp} and
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:x:log}, respectively.
Consequently, the manifold update between Gauss-Newton iterations adopt a
right-sided perturbation update defined in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:gauss-newton:addition}, with the
corresponding inverse (distance) operation, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:gauss-newton:subtraction}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:x:exp}
\exp\!\left(\mathbf{\Delta x}\right) =
\left[\mathbf{R}(\Delta\theta) \mid (\Delta x, \Delta y)^T \right] \in SE(2)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:x:log}
\log(\mathbf{x})_{\mathbf{x} \in SE(2)} =
    \left(t_x,t_y,\atan2\!\left(r_{2,1}, r_{1,1}\right)\right)^T
    \in \mathbb{R}^3
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:gauss-newton:addition}
\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k \oplus \mathbf{\Delta x}
                 = \mathbf{x}_k \cdot \exp\!\left(\mathbf{\Delta x}\right)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:gauss-newton:subtraction}
\mathbf{\Delta x} = \mathbf{x}_1 \ominus \mathbf{x}_2
                 = \log\!\left(\mathbf{x}_1^{-1} \mathbf{x}_2\right)
\end{equation}

Lastly, the cross-product matrix (hat operator) in 3D maps a vector
$\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ to a $3\times 3$ skew-symmetric matrix and gives
the rotational sensitivity of a rotated vector. In 2D, an analogous operator
is defined for a vector $\mathbf{v} = (v_x, v_y)^T \in \mathbb{R}^2$, as shown
in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:skew}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:skew}
\lfloor \mathbf{v} \rfloor =
\left.\frac{\partial\mathbf{R}(\theta)\mathbf{v}}{\partial \theta}
\right|_{\theta=0} =
\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & \phantom{-}0 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mathbf{v} =
\begin{pmatrix} -v_y \\ v_x \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}

Intuitively, $\lfloor \mathbf{v} \rfloor$ is the 90\textdegree{}
counter-clockwise rotation of $\mathbf{v}$, \ie\ the instantaneous velocity of
$\mathbf{v}$ under an infinitesimal positive rotation. This skew operator
appears in all Jacobian matrices of the 2D~point cloud registration problem
derived in \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment}, corresponding to the column
associated with the rotational degree of freedom $\Delta\theta$ of
the perturbation $\mathbf{\Delta x}$.

\subsection{Point-to-point error formulation}
\label{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt}

In terms of defining the error, first,
we propose a point-to-point error defined as the scalar value returned by
the distance map when the transformed moving point $\mathbf{z}_i$ is projected
onto the reference sensor coordinate frame $S$. Let
$\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i = \mathbf{T}_R^S \mathbf{x} \mathbf{T}_S^R \mathbf{z}_i
\in \mathbb{R}^2$ (see
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:moving-pt:projected:robot})
denote the moving point transformed by the current pose
estimate $\mathbf{x}$ and expressed in the reference sensor frame $S$.
The point-to-point scalar residual $e_i^{\mathrm{p2p}}(\mathbf{x})\in\mathbb{R}$ is
then given by \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt:error}.
This scalar residual measures the Euclidean distance from the transformed moving
point $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i$ to its nearest reference point in $\mathcal{P}$,
as encoded by the precomputed distance map $\mathcal{D}$.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt:error}
e_i^{\mathrm{p2p}}(\mathbf{x}) =
\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right) =
\mathcal{D}\!\left(\mathbf{T}_R^S \cdot \mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{T}_S^R \mathbf{z}_i\right) \in \mathbb{R}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Jacobian matrix via right-sided perturbation}
\label{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt:jacobian}

Applying a right-sided perturbation
$\mathbf{x} \oplus \mathbf{\Delta x} = \mathbf{x} \cdot \exp(\mathbf{\Delta x})$
as defined in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:gauss-newton:addition},
the perturbed error for the proposed point-point error formulation based on
distance maps is given by \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt:perturbed}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt:perturbed}
e_i^{\mathrm{p2p}}\!\left(\mathbf{x} \oplus \mathbf{\Delta x}\right) =
\mathcal{D}\!\left(
  \mathbf{T}_R^S \cdot \mathbf{x} \cdot \exp\!\left(\mathbf{\Delta x}\right)
  \cdot \mathbf{T}_S^R\,\mathbf{z}_i
\right)
\end{equation}

Then, the $1 \times 3$ Jacobian matrix $\mathbf{J}_i^{\mathrm{p2p}}$ is obtained
by differentiating \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt:perturbed} \gls{wrt}
$\mathbf{\Delta x}$ via the chain rule, evaluating at
$\mathbf{\Delta x} = \mathbf{0}$, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt:jacobian}.
The chain rule decomposes the derivative into two factors.
The first is the spatial gradient
$\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right)^T \in
\mathbb{R}^{1 \times 2}$ over the distance map, evaluated at the projected point
$\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i$ (see \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt:error}).
The second is the Jacobian of $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i$ \gls{wrt}
$\mathbf{\Delta x}$ at $\mathbf{\Delta x} = \mathbf{0}$, denoted
$\mathbf{J}_i^{\mathrm{p2p},\mathrm{ICP}} \in \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 3}$
and defined by the underbrace in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt:jacobian}.
This second factor coincides with the Jacobian of the classical
point-to-point \gls{icp} error, where
$\mathbf{z}_{R,i}$ is the moving point
expressed in the robot frame (see
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:moving-pt:robot})
and $\lfloor\cdot\rfloor$ is the 2D skew operator
defined in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:skew}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt:jacobian}
\mathbf{J}_i^{\mathrm{p2p}} =
\left.\frac{\partial e_i^{\mathrm{p2p}}\!\left(\mathbf{x} \oplus \mathbf{\Delta x}\right)}
           {\partial \mathbf{\Delta x}}\right|_{\mathbf{\Delta x}=\mathbf{0}}
= \bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right)^T \cdot
  \underbrace{
    \mathbf{R}_R^S \mathbf{R}(\theta)
    \cdot \left(\mathbf{I}_{2\times2} \mid \lfloor\mathbf{z}_{R,i}\rfloor\right)
  }_{\mathbf{J}_i^{\mathrm{p2p},\mathrm{ICP}}}
\in \mathbb{R}^{1\times3}
\end{equation}

The translational columns of $\mathbf{J}_i^{\mathrm{p2p}}$, associated with
$\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$, arise from the identity block $\mathbf{I}_{2\times2}$,
while the rotational column, associated with $\Delta\theta$, arises from
$\lfloor\mathbf{z}_{R,i}\rfloor$.
The spatial gradient $\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right)$
is retrieved at $O(1)$ cost from the precomputed distance map at representation
level $\mathcal{M}_1$ or $\mathcal{M}_2$, as discussed in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:representation}.

\subsection{Point-to-plane error formulation}
\label{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl}

Furthermore, we propose a point-to-plane variant for the distance map-based
formulation of the 2D~point cloud registration problem.
This variant  incorporates surface normal information to improve
convergence in environments dominated by planar or locally smooth structures,
analogously to the classical formulation of
Chen and Medioni~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991}.
Here, normals $\mathbf{n}_{z_i} \in \mathbb{R}^2$ are estimated
on the moving point cloud in the sensor frame, as detailed in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals}.

Let $\hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right) =
\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right) /
\|\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right)\|_2$
denote the normalized spatial gradient of the distance map at the projected
moving point $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i$, obtained by normalizing the finite-difference
gradient stored in $\mathcal{M}_1$ or $\mathcal{M}_2$ at query time or from
the gradient's analytical expression when using the $\mathcal{M}_0$
representation level (see \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:derivation}).
Then, the product
$\hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right)
\cdot \mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right)$
recovers the displacement vector from the projected moving point
$\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i$ to its nearest reference point in $\mathcal{P}$.
The point-to-plane error $e_i^{\mathrm{p2pl}}(\mathbf{x})$ projects this displacement onto
the surface normal rotated to the reference sensor frame, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:error}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:error}
e_i^{\mathrm{p2pl}}(\mathbf{x}) =
\left(\mathbf{R}(\theta)\,\mathbf{n}_{z_i}\right)^T \cdot
\hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right) \cdot
\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right) \in \mathbb{R}
\end{equation}

The normal $\mathbf{n}_{z_i}$ is expressed in the sensor frame of
the moving cloud and must be rotated to the reference sensor frame before
the projection. Since all rotations in $SO(2)$ are planar and therefore
commutative, the extrinsic calibration cancels itself,
$\mathbf{R}_R^S\,\mathbf{R}(\theta)\,\mathbf{R}_S^R =
{\mathbf{R}_S^R}^T\,\mathbf{R}_S^R\,\mathbf{R}(\theta) = \mathbf{R}(\theta)$,
and \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:error} follows directly.

\subsubsection{Jacobian matrix via right-sided perturbation}
\label{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:jacobian}

Applying the right-sided perturbation
$\mathbf{x} \oplus \mathbf{\Delta x} = \mathbf{x} \cdot \exp(\mathbf{\Delta x})$
as defined in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:gauss-newton:addition},
the perturbed error on the proposed point-to-plane formulation is given by
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:perturbed},
where $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i' =
\mathbf{T}_R^S\,\mathbf{x}\,\exp(\mathbf{\Delta x})\,\mathbf{T}_S^R\,\mathbf{z}_i$
is the perturbed projected point.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:perturbed}
e_i^{\mathrm{p2pl}}\!\left(\mathbf{x} \oplus \mathbf{\Delta x}\right) =
\left(\mathbf{R}(\theta) \mathbf{R}(\Delta\theta)
\mathbf{n}_{z_i}\right)^T \cdot
\hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i'\right) \cdot
\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i'\right)
\end{equation}

When differentiating \gls{wrt} $\mathbf{\Delta x}$ at
$\mathbf{\Delta x} = \mathbf{0}$,
the product rule yields three additive contributions to the $1 \times 3$
Jacobian $\mathbf{J}_i^{\mathrm{p2pl}}$, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:jacobian}.
The first term accounts for the rotation of the normal under the perturbation
$\Delta\theta$.
The second and third terms account for the change in the gradient direction and
the change in the distance value, respectively, as the projected point moves
under $\mathbf{\Delta x}$. Both terms share the factor
$\mathbf{J}_i^{\mathrm{p2p,ICP}} \in \mathbb{R}^{2\times3}$ defined in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt:jacobian}.
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:jacobian}
\mathbf{J}_i^{\mathrm{p2pl}} =
\left.\frac{\partial e_i^{\mathrm{p2pl}}\!\left(\mathbf{x} \oplus \mathbf{\Delta x}\right)}
           {\partial \mathbf{\Delta x}}\right|_{\mathbf{\Delta x}=\mathbf{0}}
& =
\underbrace{
  \mathbf{n}_{z_i}^{T}
  \left(\mathbf{0}_{2\times2} \mid
    \left\lfloor -\mathbf{R}(\theta)^T \cdot
      \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right) \cdot
      \mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right)
    \right\rfloor
  \right)
}_{\text{rotation of the normal}}
\\
&\quad+
\underbrace{
  \left(\mathbf{R}(\theta)\,\mathbf{n}_{z_i}\right)^T \cdot
  \mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right) \cdot
  \mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right) \cdot
  \mathbf{J}_i^{\mathrm{p2p,ICP}}
}_{\text{change in gradient direction}}
\\
&\quad+
\underbrace{
  \left(\mathbf{R}(\theta)\,\mathbf{n}_{z_i}\right)^T \cdot
  \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right) \cdot
  \bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right)^T \cdot
  \mathbf{J}_i^{\mathrm{p2p,ICP}}
}_{\text{change in distance value}}
\in \mathbb{R}^{1\times3}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The first term in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:jacobian} is non-zero
only in the rotational column ($\Delta\theta$), since the translational
perturbation does not rotate the normal. The second and third terms contribute
to all three columns and require the precomputed Hessian
$\mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}}$ and gradient $\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}$, both
retrieved at $O(1)$ cost from the $\mathcal{M}_2$ representation discussed in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:representation}.

\subsubsection{Normals estimation}
\label{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals}

Surface normals $\mathbf{n}_{z_i} \in \mathbb{R}^2$ are estimated
on the moving point cloud prior to the registration optimization in the scope
of this PhD~Thesis. This precomputation is performed once per scan,
independently of the iterative Gauss-Newton solver.
Since a 2D laser scanner produces an angular-ordered sequence of range
measurements, the moving cloud $\{\mathbf{z}_i\}_{i=1}^{M}$ constitutes a
1D-organized array of range observations in the sensor frame, where the $i$-th
range encodes spatial adjacency along the scan arc.
The normal estimation exploits this ordering to collect a spatially coherent
local neighborhood for each point, as described below.

\paragraph{Radius neighborhood search}
For each point $\mathbf{z}_i$, the neighborhood $\mathcal{N}_r(\mathbf{z}_i)$
is formed by collecting all points $\mathbf{z}_j$ whose Euclidean distance to
$\mathbf{z}_i$ does not exceed the radius $r > 0$ (m), as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals:neighbourhood}.
Rather than a kd-tree search~\cite{bentley:cacm:1975} in the 2D~point cloud,
the 1D~organization of the scan is exploited:
indices are traversed in both directions from $i$ along the ordered
scan sequence, wrapping circularly at the scan boundaries to handle
360\textdegree{} sensors, and halted when the Euclidean distance to
$\mathbf{z}_i$ exceeds $r$.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals:neighbourhood}
\mathcal{N}_r(\mathbf{z}_i) =
\left\{\mathbf{z}_j \mid \| \mathbf{z}_j - \mathbf{z}_i\| _2 \leq r \right\}
\end{equation}

If the cardinality $|\mathcal{N}_r(\mathbf{z}_i)|$ falls below a minimum
threshold $N_{\min} \geq 3$, the neighborhood is deemed insufficient for a
reliable normal estimate. With fewer than three points, the $2\times 2$
covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}_i$ is at most rank-1, and its eigen-decomposition
does not yield a geometrically valid surface normal direction.
In such cases, the normal $\mathbf{n}_{z_i}$ is set to zero, marking the point
as invalid for the point-to-plane error term.

\paragraph{Covariance-based normal estimation via \glslink{pca}{\glsentryfull{pca}}}
For each valid neighborhood $\mathcal{N}_r(\mathbf{z}_i)$, the $2\times2$
covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}_i$ is computed as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals:covariance}, where
$N_i = |\mathcal{N}_r(\mathbf{z}_i)|$ and $\bar{\mathbf{z}}_i$ is the sample
mean. This formulation admits a single-pass accumulation of
$\sum \mathbf{z}_j \mathbf{z}_j^T$ and $\sum \mathbf{z}_j$ without storing the
neighborhood explicitly, and is equivalent to the standard population covariance.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals:covariance}
\bar{\mathbf{z}}_i =
\frac{1}{N_i}
\sum_{\mathbf{z}_j \in \mathcal{N}_r(\mathbf{z}_i)} \mathbf{z}_j,
\quad
\mathbf{C}_i =
% \left(
  \frac{1}{N_i}
  \sum_{\mathbf{z}_j \in \mathcal{N}_r(\mathbf{z}_i)}
  \mathbf{z}_j \mathbf{z}_j^T
% \right)
- \bar{\mathbf{z}}_i \bar{\mathbf{z}}_i^T
\in \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 2}
\end{equation}

Since $\mathbf{C}_i$ is real symmetric and positive semi-definite, its
eigen-decomposition yields two orthogonal eigenvectors. The eigenvector
$\mathbf{v}_{\min}$ associated with the smallest eigenvalue $\lambda_{\min}$
corresponds to the direction of least variance within
$\mathcal{N}_r(\mathbf{z}_i)$, which is the local surface normal direction,
as given by \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals:eigenvector}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals:eigenvector}
\mathbf{n}_{z_i} = \pm\mathbf{v}_{\min},
\quad
\lambda_{\min} = \min\!\left(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\right),
\quad
\mathbf{C}_i\,\mathbf{v}_{\min} = \lambda_{\min}\,\mathbf{v}_{\min}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Normal orientation}
The eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_{\min}$ is defined only up to a sign ambiguity.
To ensure a consistent orientation convention, the normal is flipped so that
it points toward the sensor origin, \ie\ so that
$\mathbf{n}_{z_i}^T \mathbf{z}_i \leq 0$, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals:orientation}.
This convention reflects the fact that, for a 2D laser scanner, surface normals
on obstacle boundaries consistently face the sensor.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals:orientation}
\mathbf{n}_{z_i} \leftarrow
\begin{cases}
-\mathbf{v}_{\min} & \text{if } \mathbf{v}_{\min}^T \mathbf{z}_i > 0, \\
\phantom{-}\mathbf{v}_{\min} & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\section{Experimental Results and Discussion}\label{sec:distance-maps:results}

This section evaluates the distance map-based registration formulations proposed
in this work (see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment}) with synthetic and
real-world data. First, a synthetic benchmark assesses registration accuracy and
uncertainty estimation across four planar scenes
(circle, square, corridor, and random) designed to expose different
geometric challenges of the 2D~point cloud registration problem.
Second, a real-world laser odometry evaluation on the IILABS~3D
dataset~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} assesses trajectory accuracy against
millimeter-precision ground-truth from an OptiTrack motion capture system.
Both experiments compare the proposed distance map point-to-point
(see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt}) and point-to-plane
(see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl}) formulations against
standard point-to-point~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} and
point-to-plane~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991} \gls{icp} error formulations,
with data association resolved via the distance map representations
proposed in this work for the \gls{icp} baselines.
While the synthetic benchmark focuses on
registration accuracy and uncertainty estimation, the real-world evaluation
extends the analysis to odometric drift across extended trajectories,
where cumulative alignment errors directly impact localization accuracy.

\subsection{Synthetic benchmark}
\label{sec:distance-maps:results:synthetic}

The use of synthetic data provides access to accurate ground-truth poses and
the ability to control testing conditions.
Thus, we evaluate the proposed distance map-based alignment formulations
discussed in \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment} against
point-to-point~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} and
point-to-plane~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991} \gls{icp} baselines,
guided by distance map data association, under consistent conditions with
2D point cloud synthetic data.

\subsubsection{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:distance-maps:results:synthetic:setup}

Synthetic data is generated from four planar scenes using
the 2D~laser scanner range model. The model parametrization is based on
a real 2D~laser, namely the Hokuyo~UST-10LX sensor, with a
$\SI{30}{\metre}$ maximum range, $\SI{0.25}{\degree}$ angular resolution,
and a repeatability of $\SI{0.03}{\metre}$, used here as
the standard deviation of the Gaussian range noise model.
These scenes are designed to expose the methods to
different geometric challenges of the 2D point cloud registration problem.
The four synthetic scenes considered in this work are defined as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Circle:} a single circular wall of \SI{10}{\metre} radius
      observed from its interior.
      The scene is rotationally symmetric.
      Translations are fully constrained, while rotational alignment is
      degenerate because any rotation of the resulting scan \gls{wrt}
      the circle center maps it to an identical observation;
\item \textbf{Square:} a square room with a \SI{20}{\metre} side length
      observed from its interior,
      providing four planar walls in two perpendicular directions ($x, y$).
      This geometry constrains all three \gls{dof} of the $SE(2)$ pose,
      up to the rotational symmetry of the square;
\item \textbf{Corridor:} two parallel walls of \SI{100}{\metre} length
      separated by \SI{10}{\metre} observed from the in-between the corridor
      walls, constituting a canonical degenerate scene.
      Since the \SI{30}{\metre} maximum sensor range does not reach
      the corridor endpoints, a translation of the sensor along the corridor
      axis leaves the observed point cloud invariant,
      making the translation along the $x$ axis unobservable;
\item \textbf{Random:} a set of random 2D~points sampled from a uniform real
      distribution along the full range of the sensor ($\SI{30}{\metre}$).
      This sampling follows the laser scanner range model, retrieving a random
      sample for each ray, providing an unstructured scene.
      Because the point cloud carries no local surface structure,
      the point-to-plane alignment formulations
      cannot extract surface normals from such data.
\end{itemize}

Each scene is evaluated over $N=100$ independent trials. In every
trial, a ground-truth perturbation $\mathbf{T}_\mathrm{gt}\in SE(2)$ is sampled
from a uniform distribution:
translations up to $\pm\SI{1.5}{\metre}$ in $x$ and $y$ coordinates
(reduced to $\pm\SI{0.5}{\metre}$ in $y$ and $\pm\SI{0.15}{\metre}$ in both
axes for the corridor and random scenes, respectively) and rotations up to
$\pm\SI{20}{\degree}$ ($\pm\SI{10}{\degree}$ and $\pm\SI{5}{\degree}$ for the
corridor and random scenes, respectively).
Both the reference and moving clouds
are generated from the 2D~laser scanner range model. Then, Gaussian noise with
zero mean and standard deviation $\sigma\in\{0,\,\SI{0.03}{\metre}\}$
(modelling the noise-free case and the Hokuyo~UST-10LX sensor, respectively) is
added independently to each measurement of both scans, as explained previously.

\subsubsection{Evaluation metrics}
\label{sec:distance-maps:results:synthetic:metrics}

Kümmerle~\etal~\cite{kummerle:ar:2009} refer to a straightforward error metric
in their work to evaluate \gls{slam} algorithms based on the \emph{logarithmic
mapping} of the pose estimate ($\mathbf{x}_t$) back to the tangent space at the
reference ground-truth pose ($\mathbf{x}_t^*$) over the robot trajectory ($1:T$),
as formulated in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:kummerle} and similar
to the inverse distance operation of two $SE(2)$ poses formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:gauss-newton:subtraction}.
However, this metric is focused on the robot trajectory, not on single pose
independent alignments, while also being sub-optimal in \gls{slam} evaluation.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:kummerle}
\varepsilon\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{1:T}\right) =
\sum_{t=1}^{T}\left(\mathbf{x}_t\ominus\mathbf{x}_t^*\right)^2
\end{equation}

Still, in our case, the goal of the synthetic benchmark for 2D~point cloud
registration presented in this work is to evaluate independent pairwise
alignments in the same scene, not throughout a robot trajectory.
Focusing on a single pairwise alignment,
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:pose-error} presents the
\emph{logarithmic mapping} of the pose error
$\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\!\left(\mathbf{x}\right)$ on
the manifold $SE(2)$ between the pose estimate ($\mathbf{x}$) and its true value
($\mathbf{x}^*$) \gls{wrt} the target frame,
where the rotation matrix ($\mathbf{R}_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\mathbf{x}}$)
represents the angle difference error between
the estimated orientation ($\theta$) and the ground-truth value ($\theta^*$),
and the translation vector ($\mathbf{t}_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\mathbf{x}}$)
represents the $x$ and $y$ error components.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:pose-error}
\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\!\left(\mathbf{x}\right) =
\log\!\left(\mathbf{x}\cdot{\mathbf{x}^{*}}^{-1}\right) =
\log\!\left(\left[\mathbf{R}|\mathbf{t}\right] \cdot
\left[{\mathbf{R}^*}^T|-{\mathbf{R}^*}^T\mathbf{t}^*\right]\right) =
\log\!\left(\left[
  \mathbf{R}(\theta-\theta^*) |
  \mathbf{t} - {\mathbf{R}^*}^T\mathbf{t}^*
\right]\right) =
\log\!\left(\left[ \mathbf{R}_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\mathbf{x}} |
       \mathbf{t}_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\mathbf{x}} \right]\right)
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Translation error}
The first metric considered in this work for the synthetic benchmark is
the translation error~($\varepsilon_t$) formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:trans-error}.
This translation error is considered for non-degenerate scenes on translation
(circle, square, random), representing the Euclidean norm of the
residual translation vector ($\mathbf{t}_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\mathbf{x}}$).
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:trans-error}
\varepsilon_t = \|\mathbf{t}_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\mathbf{x}}\|_2
\end{equation}

For the corridor scene, the translation along the corridor axis (in our case,
$x$) is unobservable. So, only the error along the perpendicular
$y$-axis~($\varepsilon_y$) is reported for that scene, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:y-error}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:y-error}
\varepsilon_y = |t_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\mathbf{x},y}|
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Rotation error}
Next, the rotation error is the absolute angular residual extracted from
$\mathbf{R}_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\mathbf{x}}$, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:rot-error}, where
$r_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\mathbf{x},i,j}$ represents the $(i,j)$ element
of the matrix $\mathbf{R}_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\mathbf{x}}$.
For the circle scene, $\varepsilon_\theta$ is not reported because
the rotational \gls{dof} is degenerate by the scene's symmetry.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:rot-error}
\varepsilon_\theta =
\left|\atan2\!\left(
  r_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\mathbf{x},2,1},
  r_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\mathbf{x},1,1}\right)\right|
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Mahalanobis-squared consistency metric}
Finally, alongside pose accuracy, the internal consistency of each solver's
uncertainty estimate based on the discussion presented in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares}
is assessed via the Mahalanobis-squared error formulated in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:chi2},
where $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\!\left(\mathbf{x}\right)$ is the pose error
(see \eqref{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:pose-error}) and
$\boldsymbol{\Omega} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$ is the information matrix
returned by the Gauss--Newton solver (see
\secref{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares})
for the estimated pose $\mathbf{x}$ of the moving \gls{wrt} the target
frame~\cite{kummerle:ar:2009}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-maps:results:synthetic:chi2}
\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 =
    \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\!\left(\mathbf{x}\right)^T \cdot
    \boldsymbol{\Omega} \cdot
    \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\!\left(\mathbf{x}\right)
\end{equation}

In the ideal case, $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 = 0$ corresponds to a
perfect alignment. Large values indicate either poor alignment accuracy,
an overconfident information matrix $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$, or both.
In degenerate scenes, $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$ becomes ill-conditioned along
unobservable directions, inflating $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ even when
observable components of the alignment are accurate.
Translation and rotation errors are aggregated over the $N=100$ independent
trials and reported as the \gls{rmse}, whilst
$\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ is reported as the mean over trials.

\subsubsection{Registration accuracy and uncertainty}
\label{sec:distance-maps:results:synthetic:registration}

Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.01m_noise-0.00m}--\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.05m_noise-0.03m}
present the synthetic benchmark results for point cloud alignment using
distance map-based error formulations and distance map-guided \gls{icp}
baselines~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991} across
the four synthetic scenes considered in this work
(circle, square, corridor, and random).
Each table corresponds to a specific combination of distance map resolution
$\delta \in \{\SI{0.01}{\metre},\,\SI{0.03}{\metre},\,\SI{0.05}{\metre}\}$
and sensor noise standard deviation
$\sigma \in \{\SI{0.00}{\metre},\,\SI{0.03}{\metre}\}$, where
$\sigma = \SI{0.00}{\metre}$ represents the noise-free ideal case and
$\sigma = \SI{0.03}{\metre}$ models the measurement noise of the
Hokuyo~UST-10LX sensor. The four registration formulations evaluated in
this benchmark are the following:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}$: distance map point-to-point formulation,
      using the scalar distance $\mathcal{D}$ as the residual
      (see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt}).
      Evaluated with representation levels $\mathcal{M}_0$ and $\mathcal{M}_1$;
\item $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}$: distance map point-to-plane formulation,
      projecting the displacement vector from the moving point to its
      nearest reference point onto the rotated surface normal
      (see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl}).
      Evaluated with $\mathcal{M}_0$ and $\mathcal{M}_2$;
\item $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2p}}$~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992}:
      standard point-to-point \gls{icp} error metric, with data association
      resolved via the representation level $\mathcal{M}_0$ instead of
      kd-tree searching~\cite{bentley:cacm:1975}.
\item $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2pl}}$~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991}:
      standard point-to-plane \gls{icp} error metric,
      with $\mathcal{M}_0$-based data association, similarly to the
      $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2p}}$~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992}
      implementation considered in this work.
\end{itemize}

\input{tables/distance-maps/synthetic-benchmark/final-test/results_synthetic_pc_res-0.01m_noise-0.03m.tex}
\input{tables/distance-maps/synthetic-benchmark/final-test/results_synthetic_pc_res-0.03m_noise-0.0m.tex}
\input{tables/distance-maps/synthetic-benchmark/final-test/results_synthetic_pc_res-0.03m_noise-0.03m.tex}

All methods are solved using the same Gauss--Newton optimizer within the
srrg2\_solver~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020} framework on the $SE(2)$ manifold,
with a maximum of 50~iterations and a step-norm convergence threshold of
$10^{-5}$. M-estimators are disabled for all formulations, given that the
dimension of the error vector differs across methods and the synthetic data
does not generate outliers. The usage of M-estimators would otherwise prevent
a fair comparison under a common robust kernel parametrization.
The distance map size parameter $L$ is disabled, making the map extent
determined automatically from the bounding box of points with a valid range
measurement ($\leq \SI{30}{\metre}$). A maximum truncation distance of
$d_{\max} = \SI{5.00}{\metre}$ is applied to the distance field, limiting data
association and distance map computation to points within $\SI{5}{\metre}$ of
the nearest reference surface, which facilitates convergence and
avoids computing the whole distance map.

Moreover, the surface normals required by point-to-plane formulations are
estimated via \gls{pca} (see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals})
on the local neighborhood of each point, with a minimum neighborhood size of
$N_{\min} = 3$ points and a search radius $=\SI{0.15}{\metre}$.
For each method and representation level, results are reported with
and without voxelization of the input point clouds at the distance map cell
resolution~$\delta$, and with and without bi-linear sub-pixel interpolation
(applicable only to $\mathcal{M}_1$ and $\mathcal{M}_2$ representations).
Notably, data association via $\mathcal{M}_0$ avoids the per-point
kd-tree search~\cite{bentley:cacm:1975} traditionally required at every
\gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991} iteration,
reducing the association step to an $O(1)$ lookup after a one-time map
construction. Next, the accuracy and uncertainty of the four registration
formulations are analyzed across the different synthetic configurations.

\paragraph{Registration accuracy}
First, across all six table configurations, point-to-plane formulations
($\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}$ and $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2pl}}$)
consistently achieve lower or equal translation and rotation errors compared to
their point-to-point counterparts. This result confirms the conclusion of
Rusinkiewicz and Levoy~\cite{rusinkiewicz-levoy:im:2001} for \gls{icp} variants,
while demonstrating the same conclusion of point-to-plane compared to
point-to-point distance map-based formulations for 2D~point cloud registration.
On all scenes with no noise
(Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.01m_noise-0.00m},
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.03m_noise-0.00m}, and
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.05m_noise-0.00m}),
the best accuracy results for $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2pl}}$ ($\mathcal{M}_0$ representation
level, no interpolation on the distance map, with negligible difference between
enabling voxelization or not) report $\varepsilon_t=\SI{0.000}{\metre}$ and
$\varepsilon_\theta=\SI{0.00}{\degree}$.
In contrast, point-to-point formulations have in most cases a non-zero error
for translation and/or orientation metrics for scenes with no noise.
When analyzing the results for scenes with added noise
$\sigma=\SI{0.03}{\metre}$
(Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.01m_noise-0.03m},
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.03m_noise-0.03m}, and
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.05m_noise-0.03m}),
the point-to-plane formulations still outperform the point-to-point methods.
This result is a consequence of point-to-point formulations depending on
explicit point-to-point associations. Since the synthetic benchmark considered
in this work employs a 2D~laser scanner model, the point clouds acquired from
two distinct poses sample the scene surfaces at different angular positions
and range values, producing point sets that do not coincide even in the absence
of additive noise. Point-to-point residuals are therefore sensitive to this
sampling discrepancy, as the association error between non-coincident points
contributes directly to the alignment error.
Meanwhile, point-to-plane residuals measure the orthogonal distance from
each source point to the target surface, a quantity that is invariant to
the specific point sampling along that surface, and are consequently less
affected by the inter-scan sampling discrepancy inherent to the laser
scanner model.

Next, the synthetic experimental results show a numerical equivalence between
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2pl}}$ across all scenes, resolutions, and
noise levels. This result is confirms the expected theoretical equivalence
between the original point-to-plane \gls{icp}~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991} and
the point-to-plane formulation based on distance maps proposed in this work
(see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl}).
When the $\mathcal{M}_0$ representation is used,
the point-to-plane residual of the proposed distance map formulation
(see~\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:error}) reduces to the dot product
of the rotated normal of the moving point $\mathbf{R}(\theta)\mathbf{n}_{z_i}$
with the analytical expression of the vector
$\hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right) \cdot
\mathcal{D}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i\right)$
(see Equations~\ref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:definition} and
\ref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:1st-deriv:analytical-derivation}),
equivalent to the vector $(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i - \mathbf{p}^*)$, which is
exactly the original point-to-plane residual~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991}.
The two formulations are therefore analytically identical when using
$\mathcal{M}_0$, and the numerical agreement in the tables confirms this
derivation. The same equivalence holds between
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2p}}$ in terms of data association, though
the two formulations use different residual functions (scalar distance versus
vector displacement), yielding slightly different pose estimates.
This result carries an important practical implication. The $\mathcal{M}_0$
grid can be used as an $O(1)$ drop-in replacement for the kd-tree nearest-neighbor
search~\cite{bentley:cacm:1975} traditionally required by \gls{icp} methods at
every iteration~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991,segal:rss:2009}.
Classical \gls{icp} methods incur $O(\log n)$ cost per point per iteration.
The $\mathcal{M}_0$ representation level reduces this cost to $O(1)$ after a
one-time precomputation, an advantage exploited by all four formulations
evaluated in this benchmark.

Regarding the point-to-point formulations, whilst registration accuracy at
representation level~$\mathcal{M}_0$ is comparable between the distance map and
\gls{icp} variants, a noticeable difference in $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ consistency emerges
between noise-free, noisy conditions, and between point-to-point alignment
approaches on degenerate scenes. For example, for the distance map
point-to-point formulation~$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}$ without voxelization,
the $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ values on the circle scene are
$6404.92$, $6234.68$, and $6198.44$ for resolutions
$\delta \in\{0.01, 0.03, 0.05\}$~m under noise-free conditions, reducing to
$2980.76$, $3100.86$, and $3913.39$, respectively, when
$\sigma = \SI{0.03}{\metre}$ Gaussian noise is added to the point cloud.
These results translate a reduction of approximately 46.54--63.14\% depending on
resolution. By contrast, the corresponding
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$
values are $6665.84$, $6432.74$, and $6431.42$ without noise, and
$6584.09$, $6479.07$, and $6541.08$ with noise, showing no meaningful
reduction in $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ under noisy conditions.
In the corridor scene, $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ remains approximately constant between
noise-free and noisy conditions for both formulations, with
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}$ consistently producing lower values
than~$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$.
This behavior is attributed to the difference in residual dimensionality
between the two formulations: $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$
constructs a two-dimensional coordinate error per correspondence, whereas
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}$ produces a scalar distance residual.
In structured scenes, where point-wise geometric information reduces to a
point-to-surface distance rather than a full two-dimensional displacement,
the scalar residual of the distance map formulation is more consistent with
the effective information content of the measurement.
Consequently, $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$ tends to produce
an overconfident pose estimate relative to~$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}$.
This observation is examined further in the context of
\emph{Uncertainty estimation} below.

The comparison between the precomputed representation
levels~$\mathcal{M}_1$ and~$\mathcal{M}_2$ and the \gls{icp} baselines
indicates slightly worse registration accuracy for the distance map-based
formulations relative to the \gls{icp} baselines at~$\mathcal{M}_0$.
In both point-to-point and point-to-plane formulations,
the accuracy degradation relative to the \gls{icp} baselines
is attributed to quantization errors arising from the grid-based layout of
the precomputed representation levels and to the finite-difference
approximation of the distance field gradient, whose discretization error
scales with cell resolution and introduces a systematic bias in the residual
direction. Still, both precomputed formulations
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ and
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_2)$ achieve competitive registration
accuracy compared to the \gls{icp} baselines when the bi-linear interpolation
is disabled and for $\delta = \{\SI{0.01}{\metre}, \SI{0.03}{\metre}\}$
cell resolutions on noisy data.

\paragraph{Distance map parametrization}
The choice of the representation level ($\mathcal{M}$) and parametrization
(cell resolution, sub-pixelization, voxelization) has an effect on the results
from the point-to-point and point-to-plane distance map-based formulations.
For~$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$, accuracy degrades as cell resolution ($\delta$)
increases, with~$\mathcal{M}_1$ variants consistently producing higher
translation errors than~$\mathcal{M}_0$ across all scenes and noise conditions.
In contrast, $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_2)$ is not affected
significantly by cell resolution ($\delta$) when bi-linear interpolation is
disabled, independently of performing voxelization pre-processing on the point
clouds. Within the~$\mathcal{M}_1$ representation, enabling bi-linear sub-pixel
interpolation does not improve registration accuracy. In several conditions,
the sub-pixelization introduces a degradation relative to the non-interpolated
variant, specially on translation errors, despite bi-linear interpolation being
expected to yield smoother and more accurate distance and gradient values
between cell centres. An example of this degradation is shown in the results
from $\delta = \SI{0.05}{\metre}$ with added noise $\sigma = \SI{0.03}{\metre}$
(see~\tabref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.05m_noise-0.03m}).
With no voxelization, $\varepsilon_t$ raise from $\SI{0.003}{\metre}$,
$\SI{0.004}{\metre}$, $\SI{0.008}{\metre}$, and $\SI{0.034}{\metre}$ to
$\SI{0.039}{\metre}$, $\SI{0.047}{\metre}$, $\SI{0.036}{\metre}$, and
$\SI{0.063}{\metre}$ for the circle, square, corridor, and random scenes,
respectively. A similar trend is noted for
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_2)$ results.

The root cause of this counterintuitive behavior lies in the distance map
construction: hit cells are initialized with a zero distance,
with a single point assigned to each cell, even when multiple source points
may fall within the same cell. Then, bilinear interpolation operates on
a distance field that carries this initialization bias, resulting in residuals
that are smoother in magnitude but less accurately directed than those from
the non-interpolated field. Still, the synthetic results validate
the design choice of the distance map computation algorithm adopted in
this work, considering the disabling of sub-pixelization
(see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps}). By initializing hit cells with
a zero distance value and assigning a single point per cell, the algorithm
avoids the complexity of handling multiple-point cell collisions while remaining
sufficiently accurate for point cloud registration.

As for voxelization of the 2D~point clouds, this pre-processing step has a
negligible effect on registration accuracy in the synthetic benchmark,
with differences between voxelized and non-voxelized variants not exceeding the
numerical precision of the reported metrics in the majority of conditions.
The noticeable differences are present on the translation error results from
the point-to-point
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} and
the $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ variant of the distance map-based
formulation, specially in $\delta \in\{0.03, 0.05\}$~m
(see Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.03m_noise-0.00m},
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.03m_noise-0.03m},
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.05m_noise-0.00m}, and
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.05m_noise-0.03m}),
where the probability of multiple points on the same voxel being averaged is
higher due to a higher voxel scale resolution, assuming the voxelization step
with the distance map resolution value.
With $\delta = \SI{0.03}{\metre}$ with no noise (see
\tabref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.03m_noise-0.00m}),
$\varepsilon_t$ increases from $\SI{0.001}{\metre}$
to $\SI{0.009}{\metre}$ and $\SI{0.012}{\metre}$ for the circle and square
scenes, respectively, with the $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$
alignment when enabling voxelization.
Similarly, the results for $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$ on the
same settings raise from $\SI{0.000}{\metre}$ and $\SI{0.001}{\metre}$ to
$\SI{0.009}{\metre}$ and $\SI{0.013}{\metre}$ for the circle and square tests
with voxelization enabled, respectively.
This trend is even more noticeable with noise and higher cell resolution, and thus,
voxelization scale.
With $\delta = \SI{0.05}{\metre}$ with added noise $\sigma = \SI{0.03}{\metre}$
(see \tabref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.05m_noise-0.03m}),
$\varepsilon_t$ increases from $\SI{0.003}{\metre}$ to
$\SI{0.026}{\metre}$ and $\SI{0.019}{\metre}$ with the
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$, and from
from $\SI{0.003}{\metre}$ to $\SI{0.026}{\metre}$ and
$\SI{0.020}{\metre}$ with $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$ alignment
for the circle and square scenes, respectively, when enabling voxelization.
This behavior is consistent with the expectation that voxelization retains
the same or less information than the original point cloud. Since multiple
points falling into the same cell are replaced by their centroid in the
pre-processing step, the effect is more severe when the voxel scale is large
relative to the scene geometry.

By contrast, precomputed distance map variants, in particular
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ with voxelization, are
substantially less affected and achieve accuracy comparable to
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$ without voxelization.
This result suggests that the coarser point-to-cell residual of these variants
is already insensitive to the centroid averaging introduced by voxelization.
Moreover, orientation error is similarly unaffected across all conditions
for all formulations. The random scene results are largely invariant to
voxelization owing to the uniform spatial distribution of the synthetic points,
which reduces the probability of intra-voxel collisions.
Despite its neutral-to-negative effect in the synthetic benchmark results,
voxelization remains a recommended pre-processing step for deployment with
physical 2D laser scanners. In practical operation, the radial scanning geometry
induces a non-uniform point density, where surfaces at close range are sampled
at higher angular density than distant ones,
concentrating measurements near the sensor origin.
Without voxelization, the optimizer implicitly weights these denser regions
more heavily, potentially skewing the estimated transformation towards
close-range observations at the expense of more distant, geometrically
informative surfaces.
Although this effect is not observable under the controlled conditions of the
synthetic benchmark, it is expected to have a measurable impact in real-world
continuous tracking scenarios, where successive scans are acquired at varying
sensor-to-surface distances and density non-uniformity accumulates over time.

\paragraph{Uncertainty estimation}
The Mahalanobis-squared $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ statistic exposes systematic differences in
information matrix estimation across formulations and scenes considered in
the synthetic benchmark. The $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2pl}}$ formulations produce identical
$\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ values throughout the tables. Similarly to registration accuracy, this
result is expected given that both formulations are analytically equivalent.
For the non-degenerate square scene, these methods achieve $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 = 0.00$
under noise-free conditions (see
Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.01m_noise-0.00m},
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.03m_noise-0.00m}, and
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.05m_noise-0.00m}),
only rising to $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 = 0.01$ when adding $\sigma = \SI{0.03}{\metre}$ Gaussian
noise (see
Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.01m_noise-0.03m},
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.03m_noise-0.03m}, and
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:synthetic:accuracy:res-0.05m_noise-0.03m}).
In the corridor scene, $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2pl}}$ obtain $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \leq 5.72$
and $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \leq 14.07$ under noise-free conditions and with
$\sigma = \SI{0.03}{\metre}$ noise, respectively.
While for the circle scene the $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ is an order of magnitude higher,
possibly due to the unobservable heading component contributing a large positive
term to the quadratic form compared to other formulations, both
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2pl}}$ still achieve much lower values for
the Mahalanobis-squared $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ statistic.
Overall, $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2pl}}$ formulations obtained the best
$\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ statistic results across all conditions in the synthetic benchmark,
indicating that the two formulations are the best for estimating the uncertainty
of the pose estimation in 2D~point cloud registration.

All analytical-based point-to-point formulations evaluated in this work
exhibit severe overconfidence on the circle scene.
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2p}}$ at $\mathcal{M}_0$
yields $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \approx 5755$--$6666$ across all resolutions without noise,
and $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \approx 6249$--$6587$ with noise. These results are two to
three orders of magnitude above those of the point-to-plane counterpart.
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}$ at $\mathcal{M}_0$ is similarly affected to a lesser
extent, with $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \approx 4827$--$6405$ without noise, decreasing to
$\approx 2981$--$3913$ with noise.
For the corridor scene, $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2p}}$ yields
$\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \approx 636$--$834$ without noise and $\approx 766$--$858$ under noise,
compared to $\approx 380$--$543$ and $\approx 435$--$522$ for
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}$ at $\mathcal{M}_0$.
The reduction relative to $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2p}}$ is
attributable to the difference in residual dimensionality: the distance-map
point-to-point formulation produces a scalar residual with a
$1 \times 3$ Jacobian, whereas the standard point-to-point \gls{icp} constructs
a two-dimensional point coordinate residual with a $2 \times 3$ Jacobian. The
latter accumulates a larger information matrix per correspondence, leading to
greater overconfidence.
In the non-degenerate square scene, all point-to-point variants yield
$\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \leq 2.15$ across conditions,
with $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \leq 0.04$ in the random scene.
The overdetermined constraint structure of these geometries
compensates for the overconfidence detected in the circle and corridor scenes,
producing well-calibrated consistency statistics regardless of
which point-to-point variant is employed.

The $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ variants, which use a
finite-difference approximation of the distance map gradient, yield
systematically lower $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ values than both
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2p}}$ formulations on the circle and
corridor scenes. The reduction in $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ grows
with cell resolution~$\delta$ and is more pronounced under noise-free
conditions. On the circle scene without noise,
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ yields
$\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \approx 1684$--$2742$,
$\approx 257$--$586$, and $\approx 162$--$481$ for
$\delta \in \{0.01, 0.03, 0.05\}$~m, respectively.
These represent improvements of 52\%--74\%, 88\%--96\%, and 90\%--97\% over
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$
($\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \approx 5727$--$6405$,
$\approx 4827$--$6235$, and $\approx 4954$--$6198$), and of
59\%--74\%, 91\%--96\%, and 93\%--97\% over
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2p}}$
($\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \approx 6568$--$6666$,
$\approx 6433$--$6579$, and $\approx 5755$--$6431$).
Under noise ($\sigma = \SI{0.03}{\metre}$), the improvements are smaller but
remain substantial, with
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ yielding
$\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \approx 1810$--$2118$,
$\approx 536$--$938$, and $\approx 304$--$704$, compared to
$\approx 2981$--$2991$, $\approx 3101$--$3124$, and $\approx 3201$--$3913$
for $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$
(29\%--39\%, 70\%--83\%, and 78\%--92\% improvement), and to
$\approx 6584$--$6587$, $\approx 6479$--$6580$, and $\approx 6249$--$6541$
for $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2p}}$
(68\%--73\%, 86\%--92\%, and 89\%--95\% improvement).
A consistent trend is observed on the corridor scene.
Without noise, $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ achieves
$\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \approx 155$--$191$,
$\approx 71$--$98$, and $\approx 44$--$77$ for
$\delta \in \{0.01, 0.03, 0.05\}$~m, representing improvements of
64\%--71\%, 79\%--87\%, and 80\%--90\% over
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$
($\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \approx 531$--$543$,
$\approx 463$--$540$, and $\approx 380$--$455$), and of
77\%--81\%, 87\%--91\%, and 88\%--95\% over
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2p}}$
($\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \approx 825$--$833$,
$\approx 776$--$834$, and $\approx 636$--$828$).
Under noise, $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ yields
$\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2 \approx 263$--$303$,
$\approx 112$--$159$, and $\approx 69$--$110$, compared to
$\approx 435$--$436$, $\approx 454$--$461$, and $\approx 492$--$522$
for $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$
(30\%--40\%, 65\%--76\%, and 78\%--87\% improvement), and to
$\approx 856$--$858$, $\approx 817$--$855$, and $\approx 766$--$844$
for $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\mathrm{p2p}}$
(65\%--69\%, 81\%--87\%, and 86\%--92\% improvement).

Although the finite-difference gradient used in by the
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ variants is approximately unit-norm by
the Eikonal property of Euclidean distance field~\cite{jones:tvcg:2006}, it
differs from the analytical gradient in direction. These differences are present
near surface boundaries and at coarser resolutions,
where cell-level averaging introduces a smoothing effect that alters the
Jacobian structure relative to the one used in
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$.
Whether this constitutes an improvement in calibration or merely a systematic
bias in the information matrix that happens to reduce the quadratic form in
degenerate scenes cannot be determined from the
$\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ statistic alone, and a rigorous analysis
of the Jacobian structure of finite-difference distance-map gradients is beyond
the scope of this work.
Still, the experimental results in the synthetic benchmark suggest that
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ variants yield better-calibrated
uncertainty estimates than the analytical point-to-point
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ variants and the original
point-to-point \gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992},
making it more suitable for uncertainty-aware applications such as information
matrix weighting in pose-graph optimization.

$\approx 5$--$11$, and $\approx 13$--$21$ for
$\delta \in \{0.01, 0.03, 0.05\}$~m, respectively,
under noise-free conditions. While the $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$
consistency degrades under noise ($\approx 337--445$, $\approx 82--129$, and
$\approx 32--56$ for $\delta \in \{0.01, 0.03, 0.05\}$~m), the results for
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_2)$ are lower than all point-to-point
formulations considered in the benchmark.
This sensitivity to noise is attributable to the use of finite-difference
approximations for both the gradient and the Hessian of the distance field in
the $\mathcal{M}_2$ representation. The information matrix construction in
the point-to-plane formulation requires the second-order spatial derivatives of
the distance field, which are obtained by applying finite differences to
the already finite-difference-approximated gradient.
This successive two-step numerical differentiation compounds the amplification
of measurement noise, making the Hessian estimate substantially more
sensitive to noise than the gradient used in $\mathcal{M}_1$.
As a consequence, the uncertainty estimates produced by $\mathcal{M}_2$ are
less reliable than those of the $\mathcal{M}_0$ point-to-plane variants under
noisy conditions, suggesting that the analytical distance map representation
is preferable for uncertainty-aware applications.

away from the nearest surface boundary into free space.

the rotational degeneracy of the circle scene.

$\partial\mathcal{D}/\partial x$ is near-zero throughout the free space,
directly visualizing the degenerate $x$~direction and confirming that only
translation perpendicular to the walls and rotation are observable by the
alignment formulations.
In the square and random scenes, both derivative components exhibit rich
spatial variation, consistent with the well-constrained alignment geometry
and near-zero $\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ values observed in those scenes.

A notable feature of the 2nd-order derivative fields is their sparsity and
spatial discontinuity compared to the smooth 1st-order fields: non-zero values
are concentrated near surface boundaries where the nearest-neighbor
association changes, and their magnitude varies sharply across cell boundaries.
This structure is directly relevant to the degraded uncertainty estimation
of the $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}$ variant at $\mathcal{M}_2$ under noise:
the information matrix construction in that formulation requires evaluating
these 2nd-order derivatives at query point locations, which are themselves
subject to measurement noise.
Because the 2nd-order derivatives are obtained by applying finite differences
to the already finite-difference-approximated gradient, their sensitivity to
noise is compounded relative to the 1st-order case, producing the large
$\chi_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^2$ values observed under noisy conditions.
The 1st-order derivative fields, by contrast, are spatially smooth and
vary gradually between cells, which explains the comparatively stable
behavior of the $\mathcal{M}_1$-based point-to-point formulations under noise.

Although the point-to-plane formulation for the unstructured random scene is
not evaluated in this work given its unstructured nature,
\figref{fig:distance-maps:results:synthetic:scenes:2nd-deriv:random} presents
its 2nd-order partial derivatives for completeness.
Non-zero values appear at cell boundaries where the nearest-neighbor
association changes, as well as in the vicinity of the observed points,
confirming that the Hessian field is well-defined for structured scenes but
reflects the irregular geometry of unstructured point distributions.

\subsection{Real-world laser odometry evaluation}
\label{sec:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d}

The synthetic benchmark evaluates pairwise registration accuracy under
controlled, repeatable conditions with known ground-truth poses.
Real-world sequences expose the formulations for 2D~point cloud registration to
cumulative integration of small alignment errors across extended trajectories,
sensor noise under real-world conditions, and geometric diversity that
the synthetic scenes considered in this work cannot fully replicate. Therefore,
we evaluate the proposed distance map-based registration formulations as a
2D~laser odometry tracker on the IILABS~3D dataset~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025},
a complementary contribution within the scope of this PhD~Thesis
(see~\appxref{appx:iilabs3d}).
The tracker uses the srrg2\_solver~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020} framework to
implement the distance map point-to-point and point-to-plane formulations
introduced in \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt}
and~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl}, and
the standard point-to-point~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} and
point-to-plane~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991} \gls{icp} baselines,
with data association resolved via the same distance map representations.
This implementation approach allows the evaluation of different registration
formulations in a unified manner, ensuring consistent conditions across them.

Unlike the synthetic benchmark, which constitutes an ablation study of
individual registration configurations under controlled perturbations,
our real-world evaluation focuses on determining which error formulations and
distance map representation levels are best suited to laser odometry in
indoor environments, compared with the \gls{icp} baselines.
We focus on laser odometry drift, \ie, the accumulation of translational and
rotational alignment errors along trajectories. This drift
conditions localization and mapping accuracy in a \gls{slam} front-end.

Furthermore, the data source for the laser odometry tracker implemented in this
work is the Hokuyo~UST-10LX 2D~laser scanner sensor used on the
IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} dataset.
The tracker runs in offline mode, processing all \gls{ros} messages
from the recorded bag files without dropping frames.
The estimated robot trajectory is logged into a TUM file~\cite{sturm:iros:2012},
consistent with the ground-truth format provided by the
dataset~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025}.
In terms of the tracker configuration, the distance map resolution ($\delta$)
is fixed at \SI{0.03}{\metre} per cell with a maximum truncation distance
($d_{\max}$) of \SI{0.50}{\metre}. The map size ($L$) is computed automatically
from the bounding box of the reference point cloud expanded by $d_{\max}$
(see \algref{alg:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:initialization}).
Following the discussion from the synthetic benchmark results
(see \secref{sec:distance-maps:results:synthetic:registration}), we disable the
bi-linear interpolation on precomputed distance map formulations, while
leaving point cloud voxelization enabled to reduce the radial density effect on
2D~laser data. Outlier rejection is handled by a Cauchy robustifier
(see \tabref{tab:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-losses})
operating in two sequential stages:
a coarse one of 5~iterations with $c^2=0.250$ (\SI{0.50}{\metre} threshold);
followed by a fine stage up to 15~iterations with $c^2 = 0.005$ (approximately
$2\sigma = \SI{0.06}{\metre}$ of the Hokuyo~UST-10LX range repeatability).
The solver terminates when the step norm falls below \num{1e-5} or when reaching
the maximum of 20~iterations.
Surface normals, required by the point-to-plane formulations,
are estimated via \gls{pca} over a neighborhood of radius
\SI{0.15}{\metre}, with a minimum of 5~points required for
a valid estimate (see \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals}).
Whenever the inlier ratio falls below 20\%, the tracker resets its current
keyframe, preventing tracking failures.

The experimental evaluation of the distance map-based and
\gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991} registration
methods on the IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} dataset considers three
values of the maximum sensor range
$r_\mathrm{max} \in \{\SI{10.0}{\metre},\,\SI{20.0}{\metre},\,\SI{30.0}{\metre}\}$.
This maximum range determines which scan points are considered for the
pre-processing voxelization step and the subsequent registration process,
assessing how $r_\mathrm{max}$ influences the laser odometry drift on the
different formulations.
Similarly, the following two keyframe splitting criteria are evaluated for all
formulations across a range of thresholds to assess their influence on the
odometric drift:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Translation threshold:} the keyframe is replaced when the
      estimated displacement from the current keyframe exceeds
      a fixed translation distance
      ($\SI{0.50}{\metre}$, $\SI{1.00}{\metre}$, $\SI{1.50}{\metre}$,
      $\SI{2.00}{\metre}$, $\SI{2.50}{\metre}$, and $\SI{3.00}{\metre}$);
\item \textbf{Inlier ratio threshold:} the keyframe is replaced when
      the ratio of inlier correspondences
      % according to the robustifier threshold $c^2 = 0.005$ from the fine stage
      drops below a given ratio
      (40\%, 50\%, 60\%, 70\%, 80\%, and 90\%).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Evaluation metrics}
\label{sec:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:metrics}

The real-world evaluation of the tracker with the 2D~point cloud registration
formulations discussed in this work adopts the same metrics and toolchain as
the IILABS~3D benchmark study~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025}.
Accuracy metrics are computed using the open-source evo
framework~\cite{grupp:evo} from the estimated and ground-truth
TUM trajectory files~\cite{sturm:iros:2012}, with a maximum
time synchronization difference of \SI{0.01}{\second} between matched poses.
Although we align the trajectories using the Umeyama
method~\cite{umeyama:tpami:1991} as implemented in the evo
framework~\cite{grupp:evo}, this alignment is not mandatory for
the odometry drift evaluation based on relative metrics.

The evaluation focuses on laser odometry drift arising from the different
2D~point cloud registration formulations. This evaluation considers
drift-oriented metrics computed over fixed trajectory segments of
$\Delta d = \SI{10}{\metre}$, consistent with the IILABS~3D
benchmark study~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025}.
Let $\mathbf{x}_t$ and $\mathbf{x}_t^*$ denote the estimated and
ground-truth poses at time $t$, respectively. Moreover, let
$\mathbf{x}_{t,t+\Delta d}$ and $\mathbf{x}^*_{t,t+\Delta d}$ denote
the relative transformations over a segment of length $\Delta d$ for the
estimated and ground-truth trajectories, respectively, defined as
$\mathbf{x}_{t,t+\Delta d} = \mathbf{x}_t^{-1} \mathbf{x}_{t+\Delta d}$
and
$\mathbf{x}^*_{t,t+\Delta d} = {\mathbf{x}_t^*}^{-1} \mathbf{x}^*_{t+\Delta d}$.
The segment error transformation is then
$\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{t,t+\Delta d} = {\mathbf{x}^*_{t,t+\Delta d}}^{-1}\mathbf{x}_{t,t+\Delta d}$,
from which the translational and rotational error components are extracted
as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{\gls{rte}:} introduced in the KITTI Vision Benchmark
      Suite~\cite{geiger:cvpr:2012}, this metric quantifies translational
      drift as a percentage of the traversed segment length $\delta$.
      For a set of $N_s$ segments, \gls{rte} is computed as in
      \eqref{eq:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:rte}, where
      $\mathbf{t}_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{t,t+\Delta d}} \in \mathbb{R}^2$
      is the translational part of the segment relative error
      $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{t,t+\Delta d}$;
      \begin{equation}
      \label{eq:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:rte}
      \mathrm{RTE} =
      \frac{1}{N_s} \sum_{t=1}^{N_s}
      \frac{\left\| \mathbf{t}_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{t,t+\Delta d}} \right\|_2}{\Delta d}
      \times 100\,\%
      \end{equation}
\item \textbf{\gls{rre}:} the rotational counterpart of \gls{rte},
      measuring angular drift per unit distance traversed.
      For the same set of $N_s$ segments, \gls{rre} is computed as in
      \eqref{eq:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:rre},
      where $\theta_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{t,t+\Delta d}}$ is the rotation
      angle extracted from the rotational part of
      $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{t,t+\Delta d}$.
      \begin{equation}
      \label{eq:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:rre}
      \mathrm{RRE} =
      \frac{1}{N_s} \sum_{t=1}^{N_s}
      \frac{\left| \theta_{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{t,t+\Delta d}} \right|}{\Delta d}
      \quad \left(\si{\degree\per\metre}\right)
      \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

Both \gls{rte} and \gls{rre} metrics are aggregated as the mean over all
segments across the evaluated sequences.
These relative metrics are preferred over the \gls{ate} for evaluating
the laser odometry front-end because they characterize the local
consistency of successive pose estimates independently of global
trajectory alignment.

\subsubsection{Laser odometry drift}
\label{sec:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:drift}

Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-10.0m:m0}--\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-30.0m:m0-1-2}
present the real-world laser odometry drift results on the
IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} dataset for all four registration
formulations discussed in this work across three maximum ranges
($r_\mathrm{max} \in \{\SI{10}{\metre},\, \SI{20}{\metre},\, \SI{30}{\metre}\}$),
two keyframe splitting criteria (translation and inliers ratio), and
four benchmark sequences (Nav~A~Diff, Nav~A~Omni, Loop, and Slippage).
Only the 2D~laser scanner data from the Hokuyo~UST-10LX sensor available in the
dataset is considered in this evaluation.

\paragraph{Theoretical equivalence of point-to-plane formulations}
The analytical distance map point-to-plane formulation
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and the \gls{icp} point-to-plane
baseline $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991}
produce similar \gls{rte} and \gls{rre} values across all sequences,
maximum sensor ranges, and splitting criteria
(see Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-10.0m:m0},
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-20.0m:m0}, and
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-30.0m:m0}).
At $r_\mathrm{max} = \SI{10}{\metre}$, 35~and 33~out of 48~test cases
(4~sequences, 12~splitting configurations)
are within the \SI{0.05}{\percent} \gls{rte} and \SI{0.005}{\degree\per\metre}
\gls{rre} tolerance, respectively. Similarly,
at $r_\mathrm{max} = \SI{20}{\metre}$, this agreement improves to 38 out of 48
for both metrics, while at $r_\mathrm{max} = \SI{30}{\metre}$,
34 and 38 configurations out of 48 are within tolerance.
Across all 144~configurations, mean absolute differences between
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$ remain small at
\SI{0.04}{\percent} for \gls{rte} and \SI{0.003}{\degree\per\metre} for
\gls{rre} with a standard deviation of \SI{0.05}{\percent} and
\SI{0.004}{\degree\per\metre}, respectively, confirming that the two
formulations produce numerically equivalent trajectories in general.
This result is consistent with both the theoretical derivation presented in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl} and the synthetic benchmark findings
(see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:results:synthetic:registration}), confirming the
theoretical equivalence between both formulations under real-world tracking
conditions.

\paragraph{Point-to-plane versus point-to-point}
Similarly to the synthetic benchmark results, at the $\mathcal{M}_0$
representation level, the proposed distance map-based and \gls{icp}
point-to-plane~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991} formulations
($\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$) outperform their point-to-point
counterparts ($\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$
and $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$) across the majority of
configurations in terms of rotational and, specially, on translational drift.
This result is also in conformity with the finding of Rusinkiewicz and
Levoy~\cite{rusinkiewicz-levoy:im:2001}, where the original point-to-plane
\gls{icp}~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991} metric demonstrated significantly
better performance than the point-to-point~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992}
formulation in \gls{icp}, while extending this conclusion to analytical
distance-based formulations for 2D~point cloud registration proposed in this
work. Specifically, $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ outperforms
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ in
37, 30, and 34 out of 48~configurations
(median improvement of \SI{20.10}{\percent}, \SI{15.60}{\percent}, and
\SI{16.75}{\percent}) on \gls{rte}
and in 28, 30, and 31~cases
(median improvement of \SI{7.45}{\percent}, \SI{4.65}{\percent}, and
\SI{4.55}{\percent}) on \gls{rre}
at $r_\mathrm{max} \in \{\SI{10}{\metre},\,\SI{20}{\metre},\,\SI{30}{\metre}\}$,
respectively.
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$ also outperforms
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$, improving on \gls{rte} in
34, 33, and 32~out of 48~cases
(median improvement of \SI{15.28}{\percent}, \SI{12.25}{\percent}, and
\SI{13.05}{\percent}) and on \gls{rre} in 24, 28, and 27~configurations
(median improvement of \SI{1.05}{\percent}, \SI{4.6}{\percent}, and
\SI{6.95}{\percent}) for the three maximum range values.

However, the advantage of point-to-plane over point-to-point formulations does
not hold across all splitting configurations.
At \SI{90}{\percent} inlier thresholds, the proposed distance map-based and the
original point-to-plane \gls{icp}~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991} formulations are
consistently worse than their point-to-point counterparts on Nav~A~Diff,
Nav~A~Omni, and Slippage sequences, across all three maximum sensor ranges and
for both distance-map and \gls{icp} variants.
For example, at the 90\% inlier threshold,
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ reaches \SI{9.23}{\percent} \gls{rte}
on Nav~A~Omni and \SI{7.12}{\percent} on Slippage at
$r_\mathrm{max} = \SI{10}{\metre}$, compared to \SI{4.12}{\percent} and
\SI{2.61}{\percent} for $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$.
The same pattern holds for $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$,
indicating that the degradation is related to the point-to-plane error
formulation rather than of the distance-map correspondence mechanism.
The exception is on the Loop sequence, where the robot traverses in and out of
the Nav~A space through its external corridor. Indeed, even at the 90\%
threshold, the point-to-plane formulations consistently outperform their
point-to-point counterparts on that sequence. These results indicate that
point-to-plane metrics retaining its consistency better than point-to-point
counterparts when traversing different spaces of the same environment through
doors.

\paragraph{Precomputed point-to-point formulation}
Another observation from the evaluation on the
IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} dataset is that the precomputed
point-to-point formulation $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ proposed in
this work outperforms both its analytical
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ variant
(compare Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-10.0m:m0-1-2},
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-20.0m:m0-1-2}, and
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-30.0m:m0-1-2} with
Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-10.0m:m0},
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-20.0m:m0}, and
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-30.0m:m0}, respectively)
and the point-to-point \gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} baseline
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$
(see Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-10.0m:m0-1-2},
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-20.0m:m0-1-2}, and
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-30.0m:m0-1-2}) across the
majority of configurations. Against $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$,
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ achieves lower \gls{rte} in
42, 36, and 43 (median reduction of \SI{22.20}{\percent}, \SI{28.10}{\percent},
and \SI{28.60}{\percent}) and lower \gls{rre} in 30, 35, and 36
(median reduction of \SI{12.55}{\percent}, \SI{17.10}{\percent}, and
\SI{21.85}{\percent}) out of 48~configurations at
$r_\mathrm{max} \in \{\SI{10}{\metre},\,\SI{20}{\metre},\,\SI{30}{\metre}\}$,
respectively. Against $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$,
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ outperforms in 37, 37, and 39
out of 48~configurations on \gls{rte} (median improvement of
\SI{21.05}{\percent}, \SI{21.35}{\percent}, and \SI{22.40}{\percent}) and
31, 34, and 35 on \gls{rre} (median improvement of \SI{8.2}{\percent},
\SI{22.85}{\percent}, and \SI{20.35}{\percent}) for the three maximum ranges
considered in the evaluation.
When considering the best formulation between
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$,
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$, and
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ across all sequences and
configurations, the precomputed variant
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ obtains the best overall results
on \gls{rte} and \gls{rre} with a \SI{30}{\metre} maximum range on the
Nav~A~Diff (\SI{0.95}{\percent} and \SI{0.078}{\degree\per\metre}) and
Nav~A~Omni (\SI{0.81}{\percent} and \SI{0.065}{\degree\per\metre}) with an
inliers splitting criterion of 50\%, and on the Loop sequence with  the 40\%
inliers threshold (\SI{2.14}{\percent} and \SI{0.134}{\degree\per\metre}).
The only exception is on the Slippage sequence,
where $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$ obtains the best results
with a \SI{2.00}{\metre} translation criterion
(\SI{0.45}{\percent} and \SI{0.054}{\degree\per\metre}).

This behavior emphasizes the results from the synthetic benchmark, where
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ achieved competitive accuracy with
the analytical point-to-point and point-to-plane formulations
($\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$,
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$,
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$, and
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$) regardless of distance map
resolution and of voxelization being enabled,
considering the bi-linear interpolation not enabled. Indeed,
the precomputed finite-difference gradient and distance map resolution
regularizes the influence of points that lie close to surfaces, where the
precomputed distance field is nearly flat.
In contrast, perturbations from sensor noise produce variations on
the gradient from point-to-point correspondence under the analytical
$\mathcal{M}_0$-based formulations for analytical-based point-to-point
formulations ($\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$ and
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$), contributing to worse results on
laser odometry drift compared to $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$.

In many configurations, the improvement of the precomputed point-to-point
formulation $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ proposed in this work over
the point-to-point baselines extends to competitive performance against
the point-to-plane formulations $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$
and $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991}.
At $r_\mathrm{max} \in \{\SI{10}{\metre},\,\SI{20}{\metre},\,\SI{30}{\metre}\}$,
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ outperforms
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ in 26, 29, and 30 out of
48~configurations on \gls{rte} (median improvement of \SI{5.20}{\percent},
\SI{5.00}{\percent}, and \SI{9.50}{\percent}) and in 31, 34, and 39 cases on
\gls{rre} (median improvement of \SI{4.85}{\percent}, \SI{9.25}{\percent}, and
\SI{20.00}{\percent}). Similarly, $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$
achieves lower \gls{rte} in 28, 27, and 30 cases (median reduction of
\SI{2.85}{\percent}, \SI{4.55}{\percent}, and \SI{6.75}{\percent}) and lower
\gls{rre} in 31, 35, and 36 out of the 48~configurations (median reduction of
\SI{4.70}{\percent}, \SI{8.25}{\percent}, and \SI{18.55}{\percent}), when
compared to $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991}.
This competitive performance of $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$
against point-to-plane formulations is most pronounced on the Nav~A~Diff,
Nav~A~Omni, and Slippage sequences. The best results of
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ across all configurations
and ranges reach \SI{0.95}{\percent} \gls{rte} and
\SI{0.078}{\degree\per\metre} \gls{rre} on Nav~A~Diff,
\SI{0.81}{\percent} and \SI{0.063}{\degree\per\metre} on Nav~A~Omni, and
\SI{0.55}{\percent} and \SI{0.056}{\degree\per\metre} on the Slippage sequence.
In comparison, the best results of
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991} are
\SI{0.88}{\percent} and \SI{0.082}{\degree\per\metre},
\SI{0.82}{\percent} and \SI{0.070}{\degree\per\metre}, and
\SI{0.64}{\percent} and \SI{0.056}{\degree\per\metre} on the Nav~A~Diff,
Nav~A~Omni, and Slippage sequences.

The competitive performance on laser odometry drift of
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ against
point-to-plane baselines is possibly due to the relationship between the
distance map gradient and the point-to-plane error metric. Within planar regions
away from surface boundaries, the finite-difference approximation of
$\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}$ implicitly encodes a similar geometric constraint to
the point-to-plane formulation, without requiring explicit normal
estimation. The discretization of the gradient at cell resolution further
regularizes this direction against sensor noise, attenuating the effect of
measurement perturbations that would otherwise introduce variation in
point-to-plane normals estimated from nearest-neighbor sets. Still, near surface
endpoints and at geometric discontinuities, the gradient direction
$\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}$ diverges from the surface normal,
and the two formulations differ. In those regions,
point-to-plane formulations retains their advantage, which accounts
for the residual gap observed on the Loop sequence
that requires traversing between two distinct areas
(Nav~A and its external corridor). $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$
achieves a best \gls{rte} of \SI{2.14}{\percent} against \SI{1.37}{\percent} for
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$.

As for the precomputed variant of the proposed distance-based point-to-plane
formulation $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_2)$, it exhibits different
behaviors depending on the splitting criteria.
Under the translation-based criterion,
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_2)$ outperforms
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ in 9--13 out of 24~configurations
(4~sequences, 6~threshold values) and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$ in 9--11 configurations on
\gls{rte} and \gls{rre} across all three maximum ranges considered in the
evaluation. This result indicates that distance fields could provide an
advantage over direct point-to-point correspondences approaches
even on point-to-plane-based formulations.
Still, under the inliers ratio criterion at thresholds of
80\% and 90\%, $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_2)$ is consistently worse
than $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$. This behavior,
absent from the corresponding $\mathcal{M}_0$ and $\mathcal{M}_1$
formulations and in the translation-based criterion,
mirrors the sensitivity of $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_2)$ to noise
identified in the synthetic benchmark results. Indeed, the point-to-plane
residual at $\mathcal{M}_2$ requires evaluating second-order finite-difference
derivatives of the distance field, which may introduce discretization errors
and amplification of the measurement noise.

\paragraph{Effect of the keyframe splitting criterion}
The selection of the keyframe splitting criterion impacts the odometric
drift. For the translation-based criterion, performance generally improves from
$\SI{0.5}{\metre}$ to $\SI{2.0}{\metre}$--$\SI{2.5}{\metre}$ across 2D point
cloud registration approaches and sequences, before degrading at
$\SI{3.0}{\metre}$. Higher translation thresholds reduce the frequency of
keyframe splits, limiting the accumulation of registration and numerical errors
across transitions. As the distance between the current pose and the centre of
the keyframe's coordinate frame increases, the inlier ratio decreases while also
changing the point density due to the 2D laser scanner radial effect.
Therefore, the best threshold for the translation splitting criterion on
the data sequences considered in this work seems to be between
$\SI{2.0}{\metre}$ and $\SI{2.5}{\metre}$.
Similarly, the parametrization of the inlier ratio criterion seems to impact
the performance of registration formulations on laser odometry drift.
The best results are obtained in general at 40\%--60\% thresholds across
formulations. Performance degrades at thresholds of 80\% and above,
which is attributed to the increased frequency of keyframe splits,
causing registration and numerical errors to accumulate
across a larger number of transitions.
Also, in general, the 40\% inlier threshold provides lower or equal
odometric drift compared to the best translation-based threshold results across
most formulations and sequences. This result suggests that a keyframe splitting
criterion based on the information that the tracker is perceiving
leads to more stable tracking compared to the translation criterion,
only changing keyframe when needed with the decrease on the inliers ratio.

\paragraph{Effect of maximum sensor range}
When increasing the maximum admissible range of the data for registration from
$\SI{10}{\metre}$
(see Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-10.0m:m0} and
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-10.0m:m0-1-2})
to $\SI{20}{\metre}$
(see Tables~\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-20.0m:m0} and
\ref{tab:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:accuracy:rmax-20.0m:m0-1-2}), this
results in a consistent reduction in odometric drift across all formulations.
Among point-to-point formulations, $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$
reduces in 38 and 34~out of the 48~configurations on \gls{rte} and \gls{rre}
(median improvement of \SI{7.55}{\percent} and \SI{8.25}{\percent}),
respectively. Also, $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$ reduces in 32
and 26~out of the 48~test cases on the \gls{rte} and \gls{rre} metrics
(median reduction of \SI{5.80}{\percent} and \SI{3.95}{\percent}).
The greater improvement is noted with the proposed precomputed variant
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$, where the \gls{rte} and \gls{rre}
reduces in 40 and 42~out of the 48~configurations with a median improvement of
\SI{7.40}{\percent} and \SI{13.25}{\percent}, respectively.
While the median improvement for point-to-plane is lower than in point-to-point
formulations, there is still a consistent reduction on the laser odometry
drift from $\SI{10}{\metre}$ to $\SI{20}{\metre}$.
The results from $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ improve
in 33~and 32 cases on the \gls{rte} and \gls{rre} metrics
(median reduction of \SI{5.65}{\percent} and \SI{7.75}{\percent}),
in 32~and 31 configurations for $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$
(median reduction of \SI{5.25}{\percent} and \SI{5.65}{\percent}), and
in 35~and 34 cases for $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_2)$
(median improvement of \SI{6.25}{\percent} and \SI{7.05}{\percent}).

The improvements from $\SI{20}{\metre}$ to $\SI{30}{\metre}$ are less noticeable
across all six formulations considered in the evaluation on
the IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} dataset.
The results from $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ improve only
in 23~and 17~out of 48~configurations on \gls{rte} and \gls{rre}, leading to a
median increase on the metrics of \SI{0.15}{\percent} and \SI{1.45}{\percent}.
As for $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$, the results improve in
23~and 33~cases, with a \SI{0.00}{\percent} median improvement on \gls{rte},
while a median reduction of \SI{2.00}{\percent} on \gls{rre}, while
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ improves in 26~and 29~configurations
(median reduction of \SI{1.80}{\percent} and \SI{2.80}{\percent}).
Similarly, for the point-to-plane formulation, the results when increasing from
$\SI{20}{\metre}$ to $\SI{30}{\metre}$ maximum range in 20 and 19 configurations
on the \gls{rte} and \gls{rre} metrics for
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$,
in 21 and 24~cases for $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$, and
in 18 configurations on both metrics for
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_2)$.

The lesser noticeable returns from increasing $\SI{20}{\metre}$ to $\SI{30}{\metre}$
compared to from $\SI{10}{\metre}$ to $\SI{20}{\metre}$ on the maximum range are
possibly due to the space dimensions of the sequences from the
IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} dataset considered in this work
(see Figures~\ref{fig:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:nav-a} and
\ref{fig:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:sequences}).
Nav~A has approximately $\SI{28.4}{\metre}$ length and $\SI{8.5}{\metre}$ width,
while its external corridor has a $\SI{32.5}{\metre}$ total length and a
$\SI{4.0}{\metre}$ width. As a result, given that the mobile platform does not
reach the extremities of the room or corridor during the dataset,
the $\SI{20}{\metre}$ maximum range already allows the robot to observe
structural limits of the environment. Thus, increasing
$\SI{20}{\metre}$ to $\SI{30}{\metre}$ on the maximum range brings less new
information for the 2D point cloud registration process compared to
changing from $\SI{10}{\metre}$ to $\SI{20}{\metre}$.

Still, when considering the results on the \gls{rte} and \gls{rre} metrics
across all ranges, the best ones on the Nav~A~Diff, Nav~A~Omni, and Loop
sequences are obtained with the $\SI{30}{\metre}$ maximum range.
On the Nav~A~Diff, $\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$ obtains a
\SI{0.88}{\percent} \gls{rte} with a 40\% inliers threshold, while
the precomputed $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ variant with a 50\%
inliers threshold has the best \gls{rre} of \SI{0.078}{\degree\per\metre}.
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ obtains a best of
\SI{0.81}{\percent} and \SI{0.063}{\degree\per\metre} with 50\% and 70\%
inlier ratio thresholds on Nav~Omni, respectively. As for the Loop sequence,
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$ obtains the best results
(\SI{1.37}{\percent} and \SI{0.106}{\degree\per\metre}) with the
$\SI{30}{\metre}$ maximum range and a 40\% threshold on the inliers criterion.
While the increase on new information from $\SI{20}{\metre}$ to
$\SI{30}{\metre}$ is less pronounce than from $\SI{10}{\metre}$ to
$\SI{20}{\metre}$, this result indicates that the higher range still contributes
for the best overall results on the Nav~A~Diff, Nav~A~Omni, and Loop sequences.
The only exception is on the Slippage sequence, that the best results are
obtained with a $\SI{10}{\metre}$ maximum range by
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$ with a
$\SI{2.00}{\metre}$ splitting criterion on \gls{rte} (\SI{0.45}{\percent})
and by the $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ with a
$\SI{3.00}{\metre}$ splitting criterion on \gls{rre}
(\SI{0.033}{\degree\per\metre}).
As illustrated in \figref{fig:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:sequences},
the fact the the Slippage sequence is executed on the area with more close
information within the Nav~A space is a possible reason why the lowest
maximum range considered in this evaluation leads to the best results
on this sequence.

\section{Summary and Conclusions}\label{sec:distance-maps:conclusions}

This chapter presents a novel framework for 2D~point cloud registration using
unsigned distance maps, developed in the scope of this PhD~Thesis in
collaboration with Prof.~Dr.~Giorgio~Grisetti from Sapienza Università di Roma.
The proposed approach decouples data association from the iterative optimization
procedure by precomputing distance map-based
representations over the reference point cloud,
replacing the explicit nearest-neighbor search required at each iteration of
classical \gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991}
with up to $O(1)$ grid lookups. We derive two error formulations within
the Gauss-Newton framework on the $SE(2)$ manifold:
a point-to-point formulation whose residual is the scalar distance
returned by the distance map representation, and a point-to-plane formulation
that projects the distance gradient multiplied by the distance to the nearest
reference point onto the surface normal rotated to the reference frame. Three
distance map representation levels of increasing information content are defined
($\mathcal{M}_0$, $\mathcal{M}_1$, $\mathcal{M}_2$) and implemented with dense
matrices (\emph{Nano}, \emph{Standard}) or hash tables (\emph{Sparse}) data
structures, each offering a different trade-off between memory requirements,
precomputation cost, and query-time information availability.
Then, we derive the spatial derivatives of distance fields analytically and
approximate them using centered finite differences for precomputed variants,
providing the gradient vector and Hessian matrix of distance fields required by
the Jacobians of the proposed distance map-based
point-to-point and point-to-plane formulations.
Additionally, we use the nearest-neighbor index-only representation level
$\mathcal{M}_0$ as a drop-in replacement for kd-tree
search~\cite{bentley:cacm:1975} for data association with classical
\gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991} methods.

The experimental evaluation presented in this chapter comprises a synthetic
benchmark on four scenes (circle, square, corridor, and random) and
a real-world laser odometry evaluation on
the IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} dataset within four planar sequences
(Nav~A~Diff, Nav~A~Omni, Loop, and Slippage). This evaluation compared
the proposed point-to-point ($\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}$) and point-to-plane
($\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}$) distance map-based formulations against
the classical point-to-point~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992}
($\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$) and
point-to-plane~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991}
($\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$) \gls{icp} baselines using
distance-map-guided data association, all within the same
srrg2\_solver~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020} framework.
First, the analytical point-to-plane formulations
($\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$)
consistently outperform their point-to-point counterparts
($\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$) in terms of registration accuracy,
uncertainty estimation in degenerate scenes (circle, corridor), and
odometric drift across the majority of configurations.
This result is consistent with
Rusinkiewicz and Levoy~\cite{rusinkiewicz-levoy:im:2001} comparative study of
\gls{icp} variants, while extending that conclusion to the distance map-based
formulations for 2D~point cloud registration proposed in this work.
Next, the analytical distance map point-to-plane formulation
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and the classical point-to-plane
\gls{icp}~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991} baseline
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$ were shown to be analytically
identical. This equivalence was confirmed by both the derivation in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl} and the numerical agreement
observed throughout the synthetic and real-world experiments.

Furthermore, the precomputed point-to-point distance map formulation
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ proposed in this work achieved
lower odometric drift than both its analytical counterparts
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$ across the majority of
configurations in the real-world evaluation. Indeed, the proposed formulation
even achieved competitive performance against point-to-plane formulations on
the Nav~A~Diff, Nav~A~Omni, and Slippage sequences across all maximum ranges and
configurations considered in the evaluation. This behavior is attributed to
the regularization effect of the finite-difference gradient
$\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}$ precomputed at a discrete cell resolution: within
planar regions away from surface boundaries, the precomputed gradient implicitly
encodes a geometric constraint similar to the point-to-plane metric without
requiring explicit normal estimation, while attenuating the influence of
measurement noise that affects analytical gradient-based formulations near
surfaces. Still, the residual gap of $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$
relative to point-to-plane methods is most evident in the Loop sequence,
which involves transitions between distinct areas of the environment
through doorways (between Nav~A and its external corridor), where gradient
directions deviate from surface normals near geometric discontinuities.
While the proposed precomputed point-to-plane distance map formulation
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_2)$
achieves competitive registration accuracy compared to \gls{icp} baselines,
this formulation is more affected by measurement noise than
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$, attributed to the finite-difference
approximation for both the gradient vector and Hessian matrices.

As for uncertainty estimation evaluation on the synthetic benchmark,
the precomputed variant $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ produces
better-calibrated uncertainty estimates than both
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2p}}$ in degenerate scenes
(circle, corridor), suggesting that the finite-difference Jacobian structure and
the cell-resolution regularization introduce also a beneficial smoothing effect
on the information matrix. As expected, the analytical point-to-plane
formulations $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2pl}(\mathcal{M}_0)$ and
$\mathrm{ICP}_{\mathcal{D},\,\mathrm{p2pl}}$ produce the best uncertainty
estimates across all configurations, especially for degenerate geometries,
given the use of surface information in their error formulations.
Still, these variants may be limited by the structure of the environment and may
not be suitable for completely unstructured scenes, given the requirement of
surface normals estimation. Lastly, the inlier-ratio criterion at a 40\%--60\%
threshold provides lower or equivalent laser odometry drift relative to the best
translation-based threshold in most configurations. This result indicates that
a data-driven splitting policy adapts better to varying
tracking conditions than a fixed translation criterion,
based on the real-world experiments.

In summary, the distance map framework proposed in this work provides
a unified formulation for 2D~point cloud registration that supports both
point-to-point and point-to-plane error metrics,
multiple representation levels, and sparse or dense storage layouts.
The $\mathcal{M}_0$ representation enables a drop-in $O(1)$ replacement for
kd-tree~\cite{bentley:cacm:1975}-based data association within
classical \gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991}.
Also, the proposed precomputed distance map-based formulation
$\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$ improves over its point-to-point
counterparts while achieving competitive performance over analytical
point-to-plane formulations.
The unified distance map data structure developed in this chapter is compatible
with both the proposed distance map-based registration formulations and the
\gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991} baselines,
providing a common correspondence mechanism across all four evaluated variants.
Moreover, the unified distance map approach discussed in this work allows
to formulate point-to-point and point-to-plane registration formulations
independently of the representation level $\mathcal{M}_l$,
its implementation through dense or sparse data structures, or even if using
distance map or classical \gls{icp} error formulations.
This unified framework is carried forward into \chapref{chap:slam}, where
the tracker is integrated as the front-end of a graph \gls{slam} system,
generating odometry and loop closure constraints subsequently
refined by srrg2\_solver~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020}.

Future work will focus on improving the results and limitations of this work.
While Sobreira~\etal~\cite{sobreira:jint:2019} showed in their simulation
results that precomputed lookup tables that does not require
an explicit data association step brings computational benefits over
the traditional two-step optimization procedure on classical
\gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992,chen-medioni:icra:1991} approaches,
the 2D~point cloud formulations discussed in this chapter will be evaluated
in terms of their computational performance compared to
\gls{icp} with kd-tree search~\cite{bentley:cacm:1975}.
Also, we will explore alternative strategies to obtain the Hessian of the
distance field to avoid the compounding of finite-difference errors.
Finally, a more thorough evaluation of uncertainty estimation with more complex
scenes within other simulation environments will be performed to study its
impact on incorporating the estimated information matrices as edge weights
within a pose-graph optimization back-end.
