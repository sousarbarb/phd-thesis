\subsection{Problem introduction}\label{sec:literature:slam:problem}
\gls{slam} is one of the most fundamental problems in robotics.
This problem, also previously known as \gls{cml},
may be formulated by \defref{def:literature:slam}.
When the robot does not have access to a representation of the environment
nor know its pose, \gls{slam} enables an \gls{amr} to build a map of
its surroundings while simultaneously localizing itself.
\gls{slam} may also be used to build a map in applications
assuming a static scene, even if the map is only continuously updated
upon the mapping process~\cite{thrun:book:2005}.

\begin{definition}[{\cite[p.~349]{siegwart:book:2004}}]\label{def:literature:slam}
``...the robot must not only create a map,
but it must also do this while moving and localizing to explore the environment.
In the robotics community, this is often called the
\glslink{slam}{\glsentryfull{slam}} problem.''
\end{definition}

Two main formulations define \gls{slam} from a probabilistic perspective,
also illustrated in \figref{fig:literature:slam}.
The first is online \gls{slam}, where the algorithm estimates
the posterior over the momentary pose ($x_t$ at a time instant~$t$)
along with the map ($m$), as defined in \eqref{eq:literature:slam:online},
provided the sensor measurements ($z_{1:t}$)
and the control variables ($u_{1:t}$).
The online term is due to only involving the estimation of variables
that persist at the current time instant $t$.
Several algorithms incrementally implement online \gls{slam}, \ie,
discarding past measurements and controls once they have been processed.
The other probabilistic formulation is called full \gls{slam},
given the goal to compute a posterior over the entire path ($x_{1:t}$)
along with the map, as formulated in \ref{eq:literature:slam:full},
instead of just the current pose ($x_t$).
The difference between online and full \gls{slam} is
that the former results in an integration of the past poses
from the full \gls{slam} problem~\cite{thrun:book:2005}.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:literature:slam:online}
p\left(x_t,m \;\vert\; z_{1:t},u_{1:t}\right)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:literature:slam:full}
p\left(x_{1:t},m \;\vert\; z_{1:t},u_{1:t}\right)
\end{equation}

In the case of the algorithm also estimating correspondences
between measurements and map landmarks,
the explicit inclusion of the correspondence variables ($c_t$ or $c_{1:t}$)
in the estimation problem may be useful for reasoning
if the perceived feature is the same one as previously detected
and already represented in the map or if it is a new landmark.
Consequently, \eqref{eq:literature:slam:online:with-association} and
\eqref{eq:literature:slam:full:with-association} formulate
the posterior estimation for online and full \gls{slam},
respectively~\cite{thrun:book:2005}.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:literature:slam:online:with-association}
p\left(x_t,m,c_t \;\vert\; z_{1:t},u_{1:t}\right)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:literature:slam:full:with-association}
p\left(x_{1:t},m,c_{1:t} \;\vert\; z_{1:t},u_{1:t}\right)
\end{equation}

Although computing a full posterior captures all the information
to be known about the map and the pose or the path,
this estimation is practically unfeasible due to
the high dimensionality of the continuous parameter space
and the large number of discrete correspondence
variables~\cite{thrun:book:2005}.
So, next, the discussion on scan matching, particle filter,
and pose graph implementations of \gls{slam} focuses on
the practical aspects of performing continuous localization and mapping.

\subsection{Scan matching}\label{sec:literature:slam:matching}

A basic approach for solving the \gls{slam} problem is continuously
matching the sensor scans to the map learned until the current instant.
An example is HectorSLAM~\cite{kohlbrecher:ssr:2011} algorithm, where the
block diagram describing that system is shown in
\figref{fig:literature:slam:hector-slam}.
This algorithm is considered to be online \gls{slam} due to
only estimating the current pose and the map.
Also, the method integrates incrementally the sensor readings
to allow discarding those in subsequent iterations.
Furthermore, HectorSLAM~\cite{kohlbrecher:ssr:2011} aligns the beam endpoints
of a 2D~\gls{lidar} with the current map using a matcher inspired by an
alignment algorithm based on computer
vision~\cite{lucas-kanade:ijcai:1981}.
The matcher estimates the homogeneous transformation between
the sensor scan and the map (\ie, scan-to-map alignment) to update the latter
with the latest scan, while also updating
the current estimation of the robot pose.
Although the matcher is specific to the HectorSLAM~\cite{kohlbrecher:ssr:2011}
algorithm, it does not invalidate using other matchers,
such as the PerfectMatch~\cite{lauer:robocup:2006},
\gls{icp}~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992},
\gls{nicp}~\cite{serafin-grisetti:iros:2015},
\gls{ndt}~\cite{biber-strasser:iros:2003}, or others,
to obtain the transformation between the 2D~\gls{lidar} scan and the map,
allowing to perform \gls{slam} with a scan matching methodology.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{literature/slam-hector.pdf}
  \caption[Block diagram of the mapping and navigation systems of HectorSLAM]{%
  Block diagram of the mapping and navigation systems of HectorSLAM~\cite{kohlbrecher:ssr:2011}}
  \label{fig:literature:slam:hector-slam}
\end{figure}

The main advantage of scan matching \gls{slam} algorithms
is the lower use of computational resources compared to more complex approaches.
These algorithms do not require an exhaustive pose search
or data association search with previous scans
(the matching with all preceding scans is implicit in matching the current one
with the map)~\cite{kohlbrecher:ssr:2011}.
However, as in the case of HectorSLAM~\cite{kohlbrecher:ssr:2011},
a pure scan matching approach does not implement loop closure
(see \defref{def:literature:slam:loop-closure}).
This limitation prevents correcting the map when recognizing a previously
mapped location, which means there is no reduction in the uncertainty of the
map estimate, nor any correction for map distortions
caused by incremental alignment errors.

\begin{definition}[{\cite[p.~188]{siegwart:book:2004}}]\label{def:literature:slam:loop-closure}
``The problem of location recognition is also called loop-detection,
because a loop is a closed trajectory of a vehicle that
returns to a previously-visited point.
The problem of minimizing the error at the loop closure
is instead called loop-closing.''
\end{definition}

\subsection{Particle filter}\label{sec:literature:slam:pf}
The graphical model of the \gls{slam} problem shown in
\figref{fig:literature:slam} is equivalent to a discrete \gls{dbn}
(see \defref{def:literature:slam:dbn}). A sampling-based inference algorithm for
\glspl{dbn} is the particle filter.
However, these filters suffer from the problem of dimensionality,
scaling exponentially~\cite{doucet:uai:2000}.
The assumption of conditional independence between map measurements
allows using \glspl{rbpf} to solve the \gls{slam} problem.
These filters use particles to represent the posterior probability
over some variables (\eg, the robot path, correspondence variables)
and Gaussians (or other parametric probabilistic distribution functions)
to represent all other variables
(\eg, the feature positions)~\cite{thrun:book:2005}.
This marginalization of some variables implemented by \glspl{rbpf}
improves the computational efficiency compared to standard particle filters,
reducing the space size for the sampling step~\cite{doucet:uai:2000}.

\begin{definition}[{\cite{grisetti:itsm:2010}}]\label{def:literature:slam:dbn}
``A convenient way to describe this structure is via the
\glslink{dbn}{\glsentryfull{dbn}}...
A Bayesian network is a graphical model that
describes a stochastic process as a directed graph.
The graph has one node for each random variable in the process,
and a directed edge (or arrow) between two nodes
models a conditional dependence between them''
\end{definition}

An example of a \gls{rbpf}-based \gls{slam} is
FastSLAM~\cite{montemerlo:aaai:2002,montemerlo:ijcai:2003}.
This algorithm decomposes the \gls{slam} problem into a
robot localization one and the estimation of map landmarks
conditioned on the robot pose estimate.
The decomposition and factoring of the posterior probability
($p\left(x_t,m \;\vert\; z_{1:t},u_{1:t},c_{1:t}\right)$)
followed by the FastSLAM~\cite{montemerlo:aaai:2002,montemerlo:ijcai:2003}
algorithm is shown in \eqref{eq:literature:slam:fast-slam:factoring}.
The algorithm assumes the conditional independence of the features ($m_n$)
given the robot path ($x_{1:t}^{\left[N\right]}$), \ie,
knowing the location of one feature does not add any additional information
about the location of other features if the robot path is known.
Moreover, \figref{fig:literature:slam:fastslam} represents the composition of
particles implemented in FastSLAM. Each particle $k$ contains an estimate of
the robot path until the current time instant $t$ and
a set of \glspl{ekf} with a mean ($\mu_j^{\left[k\right]}$) and
covariance ($\sum_j^{\left[k\right]}$) of the estimation,
one for each feature $j=1..N$ represented in the map $m$.
The basic steps of the FastSLAM~\cite{montemerlo:aaai:2002,montemerlo:ijcai:2003}
algorithm are the following ones~\cite{thrun:book:2005}:
\begin{itemize}
\item for each particle $k=1..M$:
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{retrieval:} retrieve a pose $x_{t-1}^{\left[k\right]}$
  from the particle set;
  \item \textbf{prediction:} sample a new pose
  $x_{t}^{\left[k\right]} \sim p\left(x_t \;\vert\; x_{t-1}^{\left[k\right]},u_t\right)$
  using the probabilistic motion model of the robot
  (the improved version, FastSLAM~2.0~\cite{montemerlo:ijcai:2003},
  incorporates also the sensor measurements $z_t^i$ in the prediction step);
  \item \textbf{measurement update:} for each observed feature obtained from a
  measurement $z_t^i$, identify the correspondence $j$ in the particle map,
  and incorporate $z_t^i$ into the corresponding \gls{ekf},
  updating the mean ($\mu_j^{\left[k\right]}$) and
  covariance ($\sum_j^{\left[k\right]}$) of the
  corresponding map feature accordingly;
  \item \textbf{importance weight:} compute the importance weight
  $w^{\left[k\right]}$ for the new particle.
  \end{enumerate}
\item \textbf{resampling:} sample, with replacement, the $M$ particles,
where the probability of sampling being proportional to $w^{\left[k\right]}$, to
incorporate the measurements $z_t^i$ into the robot pose $x_{t}^{\left[k\right]}$
(due to only considering the most recent control $u_t$ in the previous steps,
only in FastSLAM~1.0~\cite{montemerlo:aaai:2002}).
\end{itemize}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:literature:slam:fast-slam:factoring}
\begin{aligned}
p\left(x_t,m \;\vert\; z_{1:t},u_{1:t},c_{1:t}\right) & =
p\left(x_t \;\vert\; z_{1:t},u_{1:t},c_{1:t}\right)
p\left(m \;\vert\; x_t,z_{1:t},c_{1:t}\right) =\\
& = p\left(x_t \;\vert\; z_{1:t},u_{1:t},c_{1:t}\right)
\prod_{n=1}^{N} p\left(m_n \;\vert\; x_t,z_{1:t},c_{1:t}\right)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The adaptation of FastSLAM~\cite{montemerlo:aaai:2002,montemerlo:ijcai:2003}
to occupancy grid-based \gls{slam} is implemented by the
GMapping~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007} algorithm.
This adaptation requires the modification of three modules relative to
FastSLAM~\cite{montemerlo:aaai:2002,montemerlo:ijcai:2003}.
First, GMapping~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007} considers the occupancy grid as
the map representation for the \gls{slam} problem,
where each particle contains a grid map.
Next, the computation of a measurement likelihood
($p\left(z_t \;\vert\; x_{t}^{\left[k\right]}, m_{t-1}^{\left[k\right]}\right)$)
uses the beam endpoint sensor model,
characteristic of range-bearing sensors such as 2D~lasers,
instead of integrating the measures into an \gls{ekf} as in
FastSLAM~\cite{montemerlo:aaai:2002,montemerlo:ijcai:2003}.
This model considers the individual scan beams independent from each other,
using the distance between the beam endpoint and the closest obstacle in
the map to that point to compute the beam's individual likelihood.
Lastly, the update of each particle's map
($p\left(m_t^{\left[k\right]} \;\vert\; z_t x_{t}^{\left[k\right]}, m_{t-1}^{\left[k\right]}\right)$)
computes a new occupancy grid map.
This update estimates the probability of each individual cell,
assuming the independence of each other,
using a binary Bayes filter and the inverse model
of the sensor measurements~\cite{thrun:book:2005}.

A key advantage of particle filter-based \gls{slam} is the decisions on
data associations being made on a per-particle basis,
instead of the most likely one as in scan matching or \gls{ekf} approaches.
This characteristic represents inherently multi-hypotheses associations
in the filter, making it more robust to data association problems.
Another advantage is particle filters being capable to model
non-linear robot motions without requiring model approximations. However,
a disadvantage of particle filters is relative to loop closure detection.
Even though these filters are able to detect previously visited places,
unlike scan matching approaches such as HectorSLAM~\cite{kohlbrecher:ssr:2011},
this place recognition ability is only maintained through the diversity
in the particle sets, possibly maintaining larger sets and
requiring more computational resources~\cite{thrun:book:2005}.

\subsection{Pose graph}\label{sec:literature:slam:graph}
Another solution for the \gls{slam} problem leveraging its graphical model
(illustrated in \figref{fig:literature:slam}) is pose graph \gls{slam},
also known as GraphSLAM. This optimization-based solution consists of two
subsystems, as in filter-based approaches such as particle filters and \gls{ekf}.
In filter-based \gls{slam}, the filters implement a prediction stage
usually based on the vehicle's motion model, and then,
the correction phase updates the predicted state
by matching the sensor observation to the map.
As for optimization-based solutions such as the posse graph formulation,
a first module also known as the front-end is responsible to identify
constraints (\eg, odometry, loop closure, landmark observations)
of the problem based on sensor data by finding correspondences
between new observations and the current map.
Then, a second subsystem known as the back-end computes or refines
the poses of the graph and the map minimizing the coherence errors as a whole.
This second module usually requires optimization algorithms such as
Gauss-Newton or Levenberg-Marquardt for minimizing the objective
function~\cite{bresson:tiv:2017}. However, there are available in the literature
optimization frameworks more specific for the pose graph formulation, namely,
\gls{toro}~\cite{grisetti:tits:2009},
\gls{isam}~\cite{kaess:tro:2008,kaess:icra:2011},
\gls{gtsam}~\cite{dellaert:tech:2012,dellaert-kaess:ftr:2017},
$g^2o$~\cite{kummerle:icra:2011}, or
\texttt{srrg2\_solver}~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020}.

Furthermore, the edge connection in GraphSLAM between vertexes $x_i$ and $x_j$
is illustrated in \figref{fig:literature:slam:graph-slam}.
Considering $z_{ij}$ as a virtual measurement obtained from raw sensor data
between those two vertexes, let $\overline{z}_{ij}$ and $\Omega_{ij}$
be the mean and the information matrix of the virtual data, respectively.
This virtual measurement actually represents the transformation that maximizes
the overlap between the observations acquired from the pose nodes $i$ and $j$.
The log-likelihood $l_{ij}$ (see \defref{def:literature:slam:log-likelihood})
of the measurement $z_{ij}$ can be defined as in
\eqref{eq:literature:slam:slam:graph:log-like},
where $\hat{z}_{ij}\left(x_i,x_j\right)$ is the prediction of the
virtual measurement given the configuration of the nodes $x_i$ and $x_j$.
Then, the configuration of nodes $x^*=\left(x_1,\dots,x_T\right)^T$ can be found
formulating a \gls{mle} problem that minimizes the negative log-likelihood
function $F\left(x\right)$ (see \eqref{eq:literature:slam:slam:graph:function})
of all the observations, seeking to solve the
\eqref{eq:literature:slam:slam:graph:solution}~\cite{grisetti:itsm:2010}.
For exemplification purposes, two possible examples of virtual measurements
are wheeled odometry and 2D~laser scan matching data. Both cases would define
the prediction of the measurement as $\hat{z}_{ij}=x_j-x_i$.
The virtual measurement would be $z_{ij}=x_j - g\left(x_i,u_{ij}\right)$
for wheeled odometry, where $g\left(x_i,u_{ij}\right)$ represents
the relative transformation between $x_i$ and $x_j$ estimated by
the odometry estimator, while using the wheeled odometry data $u_{ij}$
between those two nodes. As for the scan matching case,
the measurement would be $z_{ij}=h\left(z_i,z_j\right)$ representing the
matching between the laser scans $z_i$ and $z_j$ acquired from
the nodes $x_i$ and $x_j$, respectively.

\begin{definition}[{\cite[p.~224]{rossi:book:2018}}]\label{def:literature:slam:log-likelihood}
``...since the natural logarithm is a one-to-one function on $\mathbb{R}^+$,
no information about $\theta$ is lost when the log-likelihood function
$l\left(\theta\right)=\ln\left[p\left(\theta\right)\right]$ is used in place of
$p\left(\theta\right)$. Moreover, for many probability models,
it is easier to work with the log-likelihood function
than with the likelihood function...''
\end{definition}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:literature:slam:slam:graph:log-like}
l_{ij} \propto \left[z_{ij}-\hat{z}_{ij}\left(x_i,x_j\right)\right]^T \Omega_{ij} \left[z_{ij}-\hat{z}_{ij}\left(x_i,x_j\right)\right]
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:literature:slam:slam:graph:function}
F\left(x\right) = \sum_{i,j} e_{ij}^T \Omega_{ij} e_{ij}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:literature:slam:slam:graph:solution}
x^* = \argmin_x F\left(x\right)
\end{equation}

  Edge connection in the graph formulation of the \gls{slam} problem between

A pose graph \gls{slam} implementation is the \gls{slam}~Toolbox
framework~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021}, also available in
the official navigation stack of the \gls{ros}~\cite{ros} (in version 2).
This toolbox is intended for 2D~\gls{slam} using a 2D~laser scanner
in conjunction with wheeled odometry obtained from
the wheel encoders of the robot's motors.
The optimization framework used for the pose graph back-end
is the Ceres Solver~\cite{ceres:sw:2023}.
Although \gls{slam}~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021}
states having lifelong mapping capabilities,
these are only relative to being able to update existing maps and serialize
the data for use in other mapping sessions,
not for dynamic environments or appearance invariance.

Overall, a key difference between pose graph \gls{slam} compared to
other solutions for the \gls{slam} problem,
such as filter and scan matching approaches,
is that the former has access to the full data when building the map,
being able to apply improved linearization and data association techniques.
Indeed, in GraphSLAM, all data can be used to linearize and calculate
correspondences, \ie, can revise past data association and linearize
more than once~\cite{thrun:book:2005}.
This key difference is pointed as a possible reason to graph \gls{slam}
tending to give better results than filters,
where the latter is more subject to linearization
issues~\cite{bresson:tiv:2017}.
However, the pose graph formulation shows a linear growth over time in the
graph's size requiring techniques for constraining the graph size, and thus,
limit the computational resources required for continuously performing
GraphSLAM~\cite{thrun:book:2005}.

\subsection{Purpose of the study}\label{sec:literature:systematic:purpose}
The main studies reviewing the \gls{slam} literature are presented in
\tabref{tab:literature:systematic:purpose:current-literature}.
In terms of introducing the problem formulation,
the studies focus on explaining different frameworks for performing \gls{slam}.
Durrant-Whyte~and~Bailey~\cite{durrant-whyte-bailey:ram:2006,bailey-durrant-whyte:ram:2006}
discuss Bayesian-based probabilistic formulations,
namely, \gls{ekf} and the Rao-Blackwellized particle filter frameworks,
also categorized as filtering approaches.
In contrast, Grisetti~\etal~\cite{grisetti:itsm:2010} explain in detail
a smoothing formulation, the graph-based \gls{slam},
which estimates the full trajectory of the robot
from a set of sensor measurements.
Thrun~\cite{thrun:inbook:2008} introduces both probabilistic
(\gls{ekf} and particle filter) and
smoothing (graph) formulations.
Focusing on vision sensorization,
Scaramuzza~and~Fraundorfer~\cite{scaramuzza-fraundorfer:ram:2011,fraundorfer-scaramuzza:ram:2012}
present an extensive tutorial on visual odometry for
estimating relative motion from visual data,
where the study discusses camera modeling and calibration,
motion estimation, and feature matching.
Yousif~\etal~\cite{yousif:iis:2015} extend
the previous visual odometry tutorial
to include methodologies for the vision-based \gls{slam} problem.
Although the introductions mentioned here
provide comprehensive explanations of the problem formulation itself, none of
them focus the discussion on the possible long-term challenges of \gls{slam}.

Moreover, other studies focus on theoretical aspects
of the \gls{slam} formulation.
Dissanayake~\etal~\cite{dissanayake:iciinfs:2011} discuss the
fundamental properties of \gls{slam}:
observability (whether the problem is solvable),
convergence (whether the state uncertainty converges to a finite value),
and consistency (whether the estimated state is unbiased).
Huang~and~Dissanayake~\cite{huang-dissanayake:ijars:2016}
extend the previous work by providing an in-depth explanation of the
fundamental properties and defining criteria for the
performance evaluation of \gls{slam} algorithms in terms of
consistency, accuracy, and computational efficiency.
In contrast to \cite{dissanayake:iciinfs:2011},
which focuses mainly on filtering-based \gls{slam},
Huang~and~Dissanayake~\cite{huang-dissanayake:ijars:2016} also discuss the
properties for smoothing formulations.
Both studies focus only on theoretical aspects,
without addressing the problem of long-term localization and mapping.

In terms of surveys on \gls{slam} trends,
Cadena~\etal~\cite{cadena:tro:2016} provide a broad overview
of metric and semantic \gls{slam} works.
The work briefly discusses localization and mapping robustness
in terms of loop closure validation and handling dynamic environments,
and \gls{slam} scalability in terms of pose graph sparsification
and parallel and distributed computing.
By contrast, Lowry~\etal~\cite{lowry:tro:2016} focus on topological
\gls{slam}, providing a comprehensive review of visual place recognition.
Although the study discusses the challenges of navigation
across varying conditions, it is limited to vision sensors.
Bresson~\etal~\cite{bresson:tiv:2017} survey trends in single- and
multi-vehicle \gls{slam} and in large-scale experiments for autonomous vehicles.
Although the study compares the methods on accuracy, scalability, availability,
recovery, map updateability, and scene dynamics,
their study only refers to approaches composed of at least odometry and a
mapping module, not discussing localization-only algorithms.
Also, the discussion is more focused on loop closure and relocalization modules
and on leveraging existing data, rather than on methodologies for
addressing the long-term challenges of continuous \gls{slam}.

Similar to \cite{bresson:tiv:2017},
Kuutti~\etal~\cite{kuutti:jiot:2018} and Badue~\etal~\cite{badue:eswa:2021}
analyze trends for self-driving vehicles.
While Kuutti~\etal~\cite{kuutti:jiot:2018} focus on sensorization and
cooperative localization between vehicles,
Badue~\etal~\cite{badue:eswa:2021} concentrate on the architecture of
autonomous driving systems. However, neither of those studies discuss the
challenges of achieving long-term \gls{slam}.
Saeedi~\etal~\cite{saeedi:jfr:2016} present a review of multi-robot \gls{slam}.
Even though the authors identify large-scale, dynamic environments,
multi-session operations, and agent scalability as challenges for
multi-robot \gls{slam}, their study does not provide an overview of
existing methodologies to address these problems.
Paull~\etal~\cite{paull:joe:2014} and Ebadi~\etal~\cite{ebadi:tro:2024}
also present overviews of the \gls{slam} literature,
but are more specific in their domains:
the former focuses on autonomous underwater navigation,
and the latter on \gls{slam} in extreme underground environments.
However, those two studies do not discuss long-term \gls{slam}.

The surveys by Kunze~\etal~\cite{kunze:ral:2018} and
Zaffar~\etal~\cite{zaffar:ahs:2018} focus on
some challenges of long-term autonomy.
Kunze~\etal~\cite{kunze:ral:2018} provides a brief overview of how \gls{ai}
can enable the long-term operation of autonomous systems.
Although the survey discusses challenges such as environments with
varying appearance and the dynamics of moving elements,
the discussion is limited to \gls{ai}-related work.
The work only briefly analyzes localization and mapping tasks,
while also focusing on reasoning, human-robot interaction, and planning.
As for Zaffar~\etal~\cite{zaffar:ahs:2018},
the study focuses solely on reviewing, discussing, and comparing sensors
with respect to sensor lifetime, field operability, ease of replacement,
and suitability for various environments.

Nevertheless, none of the studies presented in
\tabref{tab:literature:systematic:purpose:current-literature} describe
their methodology for selecting the works for discussion.
This limitation prevents other researchers from repeating the review process,
\eg, to update existing studies and maintain an up-to-date understanding
of the current state of the art in \gls{slam}.

As a result, \textit{A systematic literature review on long-term localization
and mapping for mobile robots}~\cite{sousa:jfr:2023}
(see \appxref{appx:slr}) was written in the scope of
this PhD~thesis. The review follows the \gls{prisma}~\cite{page:bmj:2021}
guidelines, defining a systematic methodology to ensure the repeatability of the
selection and data extraction processes while allowing future updates
over the discussion and the review's methodology.
Furthermore, our systematic review does not focus on any specific
strategy or publication time interval.
These considerations improve the study's coverage of
the long-term localization and mapping topic,
compared to the existent \gls{slam} surveys.

In summary, the following questions are the focus of our systematic review on
long-term localization and mapping literature~\cite{sousa:jfr:2023}:
\begin{itemize}
\item main challenges inherent to lifelong \gls{slam};
\item main strategies for accomplishing long-term operations with mobile robots;
\item public datasets commonly used for evaluating long-term localization and
mapping algorithms;
\item how the researchers evaluate the performance of autonomous systems in
long-term operations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Review methodology}\label{sec:literature:systematic:method}
A systematic literature review uses explicit, rigorous, and
reproducible systematic methods to synthesize the findings of studies related
to a particular research question, topic area, or phenomenon of interest.
This type of review ensures the quality and trustworthiness of its findings
by presenting a complete, organized, and summarized analysis of all works
considered, while allowing others to replicate or update the review.
The most common standard for performing a systematic review is the \gls{prisma}
statement~\cite{page:bmj:2021}.
Although \gls{prisma} was initially designed to evaluate the effects of health
interventions, its methodology checklist items are general and applicable
to other subject areas~\cite{benos:sensors:2021,chukwu-garg:access:2020,moayedi:nca:2020,qureshi:ijim:2020,qureshi:ijim:2020}.
Thus, the methodology used in our systematic review on long-term localization
and mapping, extensively discussed in \appxref{appx:slr}, follows the
\gls{prisma} guidelines. This methodology is composed of the following steps:
\begin{enumerate}
\item definition of eligibility criteria;
\item conceptualization of the search strategy;
\item selection process:
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item identification;
  \item screening;
  \item quality assessment.
  \end{enumerate}
\item data extraction;
\item synthesis and analysis of the included works.
\end{enumerate}

First, we excluded eligible studies according to the exclusion criteria
presented in \tabref{tab:literature:systematic:method:exclusion-criteria}.
These criteria focus on the type of paper, availability, and relevance to the
research topic of long-term localization and mapping.
Studies must be published in scientific venues to ensure peer review.
Only primary literature and English-language papers available in digital
libraries are considered to ensure accessibility and reproducibility.
Additionally, studies not categorized in the scientific databases under
\textit{Computer Science}, \textit{Engineering}, \textit{Mathematics}, or
\textit{Multidisciplinary} subject areas
are excluded, as these fields are closely related to the research topic.
We also excluded works that focus solely on datasets, as these are not
considered original research, as well as odometry-only approaches,
which are not viable for long-term operations.
Lastly, we excluded papers that are not directly related to
long-term localization and mapping.

Next, our search strategy for the systematic literature review aimed
to identify relevant data sources and define the search fields and base string
to consider during the process of obtaining the included records.
The review considered multiple databases to increase the overall scientific
coverage, namely, ACM~\cite{acm}, Dimensions~\cite{dimensions},
IEEE~Xplore~\cite{ieeexplore}, INSPEC~\cite{inspec},
Scopus~\cite{scopus}, and Web~of~Science~\cite{wos}.
Furthermore, the \textit{Title}, \textit{Abstract}, and \textit{Keywords}
were the fields used to obtain search results and focus the search on the most
relevant summary information. The \textit{Keywords} field includes the author
keywords, database-indexed terms, and, if available, uncontrolled keywords.
Also, we did not impose any date restrictions to avoid missing important studies
and to provide a more comprehensive discussion on the topic.
Finally, we last performed the search on May 17, 2022, for the review.
The search query used the following base string, combining terms related to
robots, vehicles, localization, mapping, \gls{slam}, and long-term operations,
leveraging the asterisk (\texttt{*}) wildcard to consider multiple synonyms.

{\centering
\texttt{(robot* OR vehicle*) AND ((locali* AND map*) OR "slam") AND}\\
\texttt{("long term" OR "life long" OR lifelong)}
\par}

the search strategy was applied to all data sources,

requiring us to read their titles and abstracts to determine

Initially rejected papers were subject to a further assessment
to validate the exclusion. This assessment included analyzing
the experimental results and conclusions presented in the original works
to either confirm or reverse the exclusion decision.
Overall, we rejected 178~studies in the screening phase,
leaving 411~eligible works for quality assessment.

Finally, the data extraction process involved analyzing the eligible records
from the quality assessment phase and extracting relevant information.
In the scope of the systematic review on long-term localization and mapping,
we considered the following Data Extraction~(DE) items required for each record:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\left[DE1\right]$ Long-term considerations -- long-term factors the works
consider in their proposed approach and experiments. Considering the knowledge
obtained in the previous phases of the review's methodology,
we considered the following factors for categorizing the included works:
  \begin{itemize}
  \item appearance: varying conditions, appearance changes;
  \item dynamics: environment dynamics, dynamic elements;
  \item sparsity: map pruning, redundant data removal;
  \item multi-session: map management;
  \item computational: memory management, efficiency.
  \end{itemize}
\item $\left[DE2\right]$ Localization -- how the robot localizes itself and the
type of localizer;
\item $\left[DE3\right]$ Mapping -- type of the map;
\item $\left[DE4\right]$ Multi-robot -- if the proposed methodologies consider
multi-robot systems;
\item $\left[DE5\right]$ Execution mode -- offline, online, if requires both,
or if no information on this item;
\item $\left[DE6\right]$ Environment and domain -- type of environment (indoor,
outdoor) and domains (air, ground, water) tested with the proposed methodologies;
\item $\left[DE7\right]$ Sensory setup -- which sensors considered in the
methodologies;
\item $\left[DE8\right]$ Non-public experiments -- if the authors performed
experiments or tests with non-public data;
\item $\left[DE9\right]$ Ground-truth -- how ground-truth for non-public data is
obtained or its type, if available;
\item $\left[DE10\right]$ Distance and time characteristics -- relative to the
non-public experiments if available, as follows:
  \begin{itemize}
  \item total distance (km) of the non-public experiments;
  \item path (km), in the case of repetitive paths;
  \item total time (h) in terms of continuous operation;
  \item time interval (day/week/month/year, or d/w/m/y) between the first and
  the last run.
  \end{itemize}
\item $\left[DE11\right]$ Datasets -- if and which public datasets are used
in the experiments;
\item $\left[DE12\right]$ Evaluation metrics -- which metrics are used
for evaluation.
\end{itemize}

Although the data extraction phase typically does not exclude records,
33~of the 175~works analyzed had extended versions of the proposed
methodologies, which we excluded to maintain the originality of the review.
As a result, we included 142~original works for analysis and discussion
in the systematic literature review study,
representing 34.55\% of the eligible records.
The extracted data for these works is available in \appxref{appx:slr}.

\subsection{Map representation}

Inspired by the human memory, Dayoub~\etal~\cite{dayoub:ras:2011} and
Bacca~\etal~\cite{bacca:ras:2013} adapt the multi-store model of
Atkinson~and~Shiffrin~\cite{atkinson-shiffrin:inbook:1968} for mapping.
This model divides memory into three stores: \gls{sm}, which stores the most
recent information from the sensors; \gls{stm}; and \gls{ltm}.
Three mechanisms move information between memories:
selective attention from \gls{sm} to \gls{stm},
rehearsal from \gls{stm} to \gls{ltm}, and the retrieval mechanism to move
unused information from \gls{ltm} back to \gls{stm}.
Dayoub~\etal~\cite{dayoub:ras:2011} implement two types of state machines
for rehearsal and retrieval mechanisms of the \gls{stm} and \gls{ltm}.
In rehearsal, an \gls{stm} feature moves closer to \gls{ltm}
or moves back to the initial state (or is forgotten if already in that state),
depending on whether it is observed consecutively or not.
Similar for retrieval, where a feature in \gls{ltm} moves to the initial state
or closer to being forgotten if it is not observed in the current view.
Consequently, \gls{ltm} and \gls{stm} retain
the most static and dynamic features, respectively,
based on their observability in the current view.
In a changing environment, the method reduced localization failure rates
compared to a static view. Instead of using a state machine,
Bacca~\etal~\cite{bacca:ras:2013} implement a \gls{fsh}
depending on the feature observability to distinguish between
\gls{stm} and \gls{ltm} features using k-means clustering.
This modification allows an input feature in \gls{sm} to bypass \gls{stm}
and become part of \gls{ltm}, depending on the feature strength.
The method was able to filter out pedestrians from 2D~laser and camera data
and achieved a more accurate representation of the environment
compared to a static approach.

Although Biber and Duckett~\cite{biber-duckett:ijrr:2009}
do not adopt specific memory mechanisms,
they implement \gls{stm} and \gls{ltm} maps.
The method implements a dynamic map as a set of local maps,
each containing submaps that represent different timescales.
The timescale parameter for each submap determines when to add samples
from 2D~laser scans probabilistically.
The dynamics of the environment are represented by using 5~different timescales,
where the smallest one ($\sim$3.1 s) represents an \gls{stm} map
updated at every instant, and the other 4~timescales
($\sim$0.43 runs, 0.43 days, 3.1 days, and 13.5 days) are \gls{ltm} submaps
updated after each session or daily.
Instead of focusing only on the \gls{ltm} maps as in
Dayoub~\etal~\cite{dayoub:ras:2011} and Bacca~\etal~\cite{bacca:ras:2013},
Biber and Duckett~\cite{biber-duckett:ijrr:2009} select the best representation
of both \gls{stm} and \gls{ltm} maps that best explains the sensor data.
In a 5-week experiment, localization improved with a dynamic map,
in contrast to the degradation observed when only considering
a static representation of the environment.
Indeed, the usage of timescales led to static parts emerging as walls
in \gls{ltm} and dynamic elements disappearing from \gls{stm} maps.

Similar to the works \cite{dayoub:ras:2011} and \cite{bacca:ras:2013}, two maps
can provide a more stable and dynamic representation of the environment.
In Walcott-Bryant~\etal~\cite{walcott-bryant:iros:2012},
an active map represents the most current state of the environment,
including parts that did not change from previous passes
and objects added to the environment.
A dynamic map only saves the points of a 2D~laser scan that changed over time.
Wang~\etal~\cite{wang:icra:2019} use a tracking map with
short-term static points and a long-term map containing
only long-term static points, identified by a semantic segmentation module,
with ORB-SLAM2~\cite{mur-artal-tardos:tro:2017}.
Also, Zhu~\etal~\cite{zhu:icra:2021} create offline a semi-dynamic map
and a static one, where the former has semi-dynamic objects
(parked cars in a parking lot environment),
and the latter has both static and semi-dynamic objects.
The main goal of representing two different dynamics is to favor
the more stable one in the long term.
Walcott-Bryant~\etal~\cite{walcott-bryant:iros:2012} and
Wang~\etal~\cite{wang:icra:2019} use only static parts of the current
environment representation (active and long-term maps, respectively)
for localization. In the experiments,
the approach \cite{walcott-bryant:iros:2012} showed that
it could identify static parts, though it was affected by false positives
and negatives and by the blur effect in the 2D~grid map.
The method \cite{wang:icra:2019} improved the \gls{ate} in a dynamic environment
over ORB-SLAM2~\cite{mur-artal-tardos:tro:2017} and
DynaSLAM~\cite{bescos:ral:2018}.
As for Zhu~\etal~\cite{zhu:icra:2021}, their \gls{mcl} framework
(equivalent to particle-filter-based localization)
reduces the weight assigned to observations of moved semi-dynamic objects.
The method improved the localization in a parking lot
compared to a standard \gls{mcl}.

As for a multi-hypothesis representation,
Nielsen and Hendeby~\cite{nielsen-hendeby:tiv:2023} propose a framework to track
landmark positions using Markov chains to model transitions between positions.
The method~\cite{nielsen-hendeby:tiv:2023} groups position hypotheses
by landmark signature, and selects, according to incoming observations,
the position within each group with the highest probability as the active one.
The hypothesis likelihood depends on the match between the landmark measurements
and the landmark in the map, as well as on the number of missed detections, \ie,
landmarks with a probability of being seen in the current pose
but not represented in the observations.
Although the work~\cite{nielsen-hendeby:tiv:2023} maintained an up-to-date
representation of the actual landmark positions in simulated and real
experiments, the proposed approach always assume
uniquely identifiable landmarks.

Li~\etal~\cite{li:tim:2025} leverage a map representation based on
voxelized maps and octree structures to handle environmental dynamics.
The work~\cite{li:tim:2025} organizes prior, temporary, and static map points
within hashed voxel blocks, each containing a
Dynamic~i-Octree~\cite{zhu:icra:2024} for efficient local map loading,
radius-neighbor search, and incremental map updates.
The octree representation is not only a storage/indexing structure
but also a mechanism that allows the system to adapt when the robot
moves between mapped and unmapped areas or when the environment changes.
This mechanism promotes temporary observations into the static map
only when sufficient evidence accumulates.
Experiments demonstrated the effectiveness of the method,
in which a prior map was dynamically updated to reflect changes
caused by parked vehicles and growing trees,
while minimizing the inclusion of dynamic objects,
such as passing vehicles and pedestrians.

\subsection{Map matching}

Another trend in the literature is the identification of the scene dynamics
by comparing the current observation to the map.
Assuming a prior vector map as a permanent map,
Biswas and Veloso~\cite{biswas-veloso:ras:2017} determine
the probability of long-term observed features
by the 2D laser scan-to-map matching distance.
Short-term features are identified by the scan-to-scan matching distance, while
the remaining features are considered dynamic and not used for localization.
Compared to \gls{mcl} with a static map
and to Tipaldi~\etal~\cite{tipaldi:ijrr:2013},
Biswas and Veloso~\cite{biswas-veloso:ras:2017} obtained lower localization
error in a parking-lot environment. However, the method would not handle
semi-static changes. Instead of using a permanent map,
Zhang~\etal~\cite{zhang:acc:2019} maintain an \gls{sdf} representation
based on a prior occupancy map. The proposed approach rejects
dynamic points identified by range flow and updates the \gls{sdf}-based map
with semi-static changes observed in the scan-to-map difference.
Compared to \gls{mcl} in a semi-static environment,
Zhang~\etal~\cite{zhang:acc:2019} had lower pose errors
and an improved representation of the environment.

Similarly, Fu~\etal~\cite{fu:ral:2022} use \glspl{sdf} in mapping,
proposing the PlaneSDF representation, in which a scene is a set of planes,
each with an associated \gls{sdf} volume.
This volume describes geometric details of the objects
attached to their associated plane. Then, a change detection algorithm compares
2D~height maps and performs 3D~geometric validation between
two PlaneSDF-based maps to compute a change mask.
However, the method~\cite{fu:ral:2022} failed to detect changes in ambiguous
situations, such as object occlusions.
Boniardi~\etal~\cite{boniardi:ras:2019} detect semi-static changes
by leveraging \gls{icp} scan-to-map consistency and a \gls{cad} model
of the environment, and update the map accordingly.
The proposed approach~\cite{boniardi:ras:2019} was capable of maintaining
a consistent map even with substantial environmental reconfiguration.
Du~\etal~\cite{du:tvcg:2022} minimize the Gibbs energy defined on
the proposed \gls{lc-crf} to detect dynamic points,
assuming that these points often exhibit large reprojection errors
in frame-to-map matching and that points tend to share dynamic properties
with their neighbors. In a dynamic scene, \gls{lc-crf}~\cite{du:tvcg:2022}
achieved lower \gls{ate} than ORB-SLAM~\cite{mur-artal:tro:2015}.
Unlike \gls{lc-crf}~\cite{du:tvcg:2022}, the \gls{dfr-slam} proposed by
Liu~\etal~\cite{liu:scic:2022} does not look for similar dynamic properties
in the features' neighbors. Instead, the method~\cite{liu:scic:2022}
assumes that static features exhibit consistent motion over a long time
and that dynamic features exhibit distinct motion patterns,
labeling them using the proposed motion-consensus filtering algorithm.
\gls{dfr-slam}~\cite{liu:scic:2022} formulates a binary classification problem
using a graph-cut optimization framework, assigning penalties
to connected visual features across instances that do not share the same label.
The method~\cite{liu:scic:2022} had a lower \gls{ate} than
ORB-SLAM2~\cite{mur-artal-tardos:tro:2017} in dynamic scenarios.

Furthermore, Pan~\etal~\cite{pan:sensors:2019} and
Ding~\etal~\cite{ding:tits:2020} leverage clustering properties
of the observations, evaluating the observation count
when matching the scan with the map. The proposed approach segments
the points of a 3D~\gls{lidar} point cloud into clusters, assuming that
dynamic points do not appear frequently at the same location. The map only
considers clusters that appear in the same location more than 10 times.
As for Ding~\etal~\cite{ding:tits:2020}, the method builds on the assumption
that the dynamic and static parts of the environment exhibit
clustering properties relative to their neighbors
(similar to Du~\etal~\cite{du:tvcg:2022}).
The number of observations across multiple mapping sessions,
combined with their consistency relative to neighbors,
determines whether a map point is static throughout the sessions.
Both representations of the environment in
Pan~\etal~\cite{pan:sensors:2019} and Ding~\etal~\cite{ding:tits:2020}
were stable to structural changes in the environment.

Building on multi-session consistency,
Kim and Kim~\cite{kim:icra:2022} classify dynamic points into
high- and low-dynamic categories. An initial processing step
removes high-dynamic range points using a range-image-based discrepancy.
This step is followed by a multi-session consistency check
in which aligned sessions are compared via kd-tree searches
to identify low-dynamic points. By parsing negative
(removed data between consecutive sessions) and positive differences
(new data appearing in the environment between sessions)
instead of saving the whole mapping sessions,
their representation achieves a 60\% reduction in memory usage
compared to maintaining full session histories.
Similarly, Yang~\etal~\cite{yang:ral:2024} utilize a clean initial base map and
identify changes in incoming sessions using a k-\gls{nn}-based spatial filter.
The proposed approach segments points into base-difference, coexist,
and session-difference categories, while implementing a map version-control
system based on positive and negative differences
to maintain the environment representation throughout time.
To accelerate this process, Yang~\etal~\cite{yang:ral:2024} employ
2D~\gls{bev} descriptors to pinpoint intensity differences in 3D~data.
A similar memory reduction compared to Kim and Kim~\cite{kim:icra:2022}
was observed in the experiments, increasing memory efficiency
by 40.1\%--94.2\% across datasets, while retaining
at least 5--7\% more static points compared to \cite{kim:icra:2022}.

The concept of ray tracing is also used by the included works
to handle dynamic changes. Lázaro~\etal~\cite{lazaro:iros:2018}
use ray tracing to exploit free space information.
When comparing two 2D~point clouds from a viewpoint,
ray tracing identifies new objects added to the scene
(observed points closer to the viewpoint than the old ones)
and outdated information (observed points farther away), allowing dynamic
change detection and providing an up-to-date representation for localization.
Also, Lázaro~\etal~\cite{lazaro:iros:2018} merge points on the same ray
that are close to each other into a new point based on
the weighted average of their covariances. Experiments showed
improvements in map definition and removal of non-static obstacles.
Stefanini~\etal~\cite{stefanini:sensors:2023} adopt a 1-to-N ray-casting
strategy to improve map merging in the face of environmental dynamics.
Rather than a standard point-to-point comparison,
the method~\cite{stefanini:sensors:2023} compares measurement points against
a set of expected hit points along virtual neighboring range measurements
to detect environmental changes more robustly.

Given that 3D~ray tracing is memory-intensive and requires
dense map representations, Pomerleau~\etal~\cite{pomerleau:icra:2014}
use the sparse point cloud from a 3D~laser directly.
The map points are associated with every single reading
via small conical apertures in spherical coordinates,
updating the observed points closer than the mapped ones,
and leaving the farther ones untouched.
The approximation of ray-tracing results is used to update
the probability that points in the map are dynamic,
using a Bayesian approach. The probability of being dynamic can be used
in \gls{icp} to avoid trusting dynamic points.
In the experiments, the method~\cite{pomerleau:icra:2014} produced a more
precise and cleaner map than standard \gls{icp} matching.
Instead of weighting the map points, An~\etal~\cite{an:as:2016} propose the
\gls{del} to model the dynamics of a pose graph's edges
rather than the data itself. The observation of moving obstacles
between two poses gradually decreases the weight of the respective edge
until it no longer detects the obstacle.
Integrating \gls{del} in an exploration scheme,
nodes with an edge weight average lower than a certain threshold,
meaning frequent moving obstacles or changed structure near that node,
are not considered for exploration because the robot may be
unable to move to that position.

Although the standard \gls{ndt} representation does not model free space,
the works of
Einhorn and Gross~\cite{einhorn-gross:ecmr:2013,einhorn-gross:ras:2015} and
Saarinen~\etal~\cite{saarinen:ijrr:2013} use \gls{ndt} with occupancy maps
to explicitly model free space and adopt
an exponential weighted moving average and covariance for new measurements,
giving them stronger influence than old ones.
Einhorn and Gross~\cite{einhorn-gross:ecmr:2013} propose
a generic 2D/3D mapping using \gls{ndt} and occupancy maps.
The hit cells from scan-to-map alignment are updated incrementally
with exponential weighting, based on the current observation.
The other cells along the sensor beam, potentially empty,
are updated using the standard update rule for occupancy maps, based on
the log-odds of the occupancy value and the inverse range sensor model.
Instead of using the standard occupancy map update,
the sensor model in Saarinen~\etal~\cite{saarinen:ijrr:2013} relies on
the inconsistency between observations and the map.
Also, the occupancy value describes the confidence of the \gls{ndt}
based on past observations.
As for Einhorn and Gross~\cite{einhorn-gross:ras:2015},
the proposed approach defines two occupancy map probabilities:
occupancy and statically occupied. The former is updated based on
the sensor model (2D/3D generic beam sensor) and scan-to-map alignment, and the
latter slowly adapts to high-probability static objects in the environment.
The static occupancy probability follows the proposed ad-hoc model,
which is parameterized to control how quickly the static occupancy
probabilities are adapted, depending also on the occupancy probability itself.
The works \cite{einhorn-gross:ecmr:2013} and \cite{einhorn-gross:ras:2015}
handled both semi-static and dynamic changes, maintaining a consistent,
up-to-date representation of the environment. In contrast,
the method~\cite{saarinen:ijrr:2013} favored
long-term static structures in dynamic environments.

In terms of detecting dynamic objects, Yue~\etal~\cite{yue:icra:2020} proposes a collaborative dynamic mapping for detecting humans using visual and thermal images and a 3D \gls{lidar}. The YOLOv3 algorithm extracts the bounding boxes from the images relative to humans. The 3D point cloud projection onto the images allows the creation of a static point cloud for localization and mapping of each robot by filtering out the points corresponding to humans. In the experiments, the dynamic objects removal generated a more accurate relative transformation of the collaborative maps compared to not removing those objects.
Zhu~\etal~\cite{zhu:icra:2021} also uses YOLOv3 to extract bounding boxes of dynamic classes (parked cars) from a RGB image, and the projection of 3D \gls{lidar} points allows the creation of the static and semi-dynamic maps required for localization. Even though the method \cite{zhu:icra:2021} does not discard the dynamic objects, the localization module reduce the importance of weight of the corresponding observations.
Instead of using visual data, Sun~\etal~\cite{sun:ral:2018} adapts the PointNet network for object recognition (pedestrian, cyclist, car, or background) to classify the scan points of a 3D \gls{lidar}. The proposed Recurrent-OctoMap maintains the occupancy and semantic information, where the latter information specifies the cell semantic state and the probability of the prediction. The transition between states is learned by a \gls{ltm} \gls{rnn}. In a long-term experiment, the method \cite{sun:ral:2018} was able to improve its 3D semantic map compared to a standard Bayes update.

Moreover, pixel-wise semantic segmentation is another way to identify dynamic objects.
Additionally to the semantic-descriptor proposed by Singh~\etal~\cite{singh:itsc:2021} describing the spatial distribution of semantic entities in the scene, the method sets lower weights to features detected on sky and dynamic classes (person, car, \etc) from the semantic segmentation of images using EdgeNet. The proposed approach \cite{singh:itsc:2021} obtained higher accuracy when compared to SeqSLAM~\cite{milford-wyeth:icra:2012}, FAB-MAP~\cite{cummins-newman:ijrr:2008}, and \gls{bow}~\cite{sivic-zisserman:iccv:2003}-based place recognition methods in a highly dynamic outdoor experiment.
Instead of identifying object classes, Song~\etal~\cite{song:iros:2019} proposes the MD-Net \gls{cnn} to segment a grayscale image into unstable, static, and moving pixel points, only using static points for localization. The localization error was reduced compared to not estimated the pixel dynamic attribute.
Ganti and Waslander~\cite{ganti-waslander:crv:2019} proposes the \gls{sivo} to improve the performance of ORB-SLAM2~\cite{mur-artal-tardos:tro:2017} by using the Bayesian neural network SegNet for segmentation and computation of the network uncertainty. SegNet is trained to distinguish different object classes for identifying dynamic objects (sky, car, truck/bus, person/rider, motorcycle/bicycle, and void) from static ones (road, traffic sign, building, wall/fence, pole, vegetation, sidewalk, traffic light, and terrain). Only static keypoints that reduce the most of the state's uncertainty (considering the network uncertainty) are considered as input for ORB-SLAM2~\cite{mur-artal-tardos:tro:2017}.
\gls{sivo}~\cite{ganti-waslander:crv:2019} was able to remove uninformative and dynamic keypoints from the current frame. However, the rejection of potential dynamic objects without verifying if they are moving reduced the localization performance of the module in certain scenarios.

Dynamic objects identification can be improved by verifying geometric constraints.
Bescos~\etal~\cite{bescos:ral:2018} proposes DynaSLAM, using multi-view geometry verification for improving semantic labeling of potential dynamic classes obtained from a Mask \gls{rnn}. Although DynaSLAM~\cite{bescos:ral:2018} outperformed ORB-SLAM2~\cite{mur-artal-tardos:tro:2017} in highly dynamic scenarios, its performance reduces in slower dynamics.
Similarly, Wang~\etal~\cite{wang:icra:2019} also uses a geometric verification for improving labeling of dynamic objects, the latter obtained by a ResNet-based network. The verification is based on the reprojection error. The method \cite{wang:icra:2019} improved the \gls{ate} over DynaSLAM~\cite{bescos:ral:2018} and ORB-SLAM2~\cite{mur-artal-tardos:tro:2017} in a scenario with movable objects.
Guo and Liu~\cite{guo-liu:robio:2021} also uses a Mask \gls{rnn} network for pixel-wise segmentation and object instance labels, similar to DynaSLAM~\cite{bescos:ral:2018}. Rather than using multi-view geometry verification just for improving semantic labeling, this verification is used in \cite{guo-liu:robio:2021} also for detecting untrained moving objects. The approached \cite{guo-liu:robio:2021} outperformed DynaSLAM~\cite{bescos:ral:2018} in terms of \gls{ate} in dynamic scenes.
Instead of using semantic segmentation, the \gls{sgc-vslam}~\cite{yang:sensors:2020} proposed by Yang~\etal\  uses YOLOv3 to extract bounding boxes of dynamic objects for also improving ORB-SLAM2~\cite{mur-artal-tardos:tro:2017}. A constraint based on epipolar geometry improves the labeling. \gls{sgc-vslam} decrease the \gls{rmse} of the \gls{ate} by 96\% compared to ORB-SLAM2~\cite{mur-artal-tardos:tro:2017} in highly dynamic environments. However, similar to DynaSLAM~\cite{bescos:ral:2018}, its performance decreased in lower dynamics.
Finally, Xing~\etal~\cite{xing:jfr:2022} proposes the DE-SLAM to deal with \gls{std} and \gls{ltd} at the same time. A MobileNetv2 identifies bounding boxes of movable objects (cars, persons, \etc) classified as \gls{std}. A motion check of \gls{std} elements recognizes all moving objects in the current keyframe. As for \gls{ltd}, DE-SLAM uses \gls{hog} features extracted from ORB keypoints to improve its invariance to illumination changes. In the experiments, DE-SLAM~\cite{xing:jfr:2022} improved the localization over ORB-SLAM2~\cite{mur-artal-tardos:tro:2017} in a changing environment.
All of these methods using geometric constraints to improve semantic labeling only use static features for localization and mapping.

In the included works, Markov processes are used to predict the dynamics of the environment.
Tipaldi~\etal~\cite{tipaldi:ijrr:2013} uses a dynamic occupancy grid and exploits the stationary distribution and the state holding time associated with \glspl{hmm} on a 2D grid. The method uses past observations for each run to learn the state transition probabilities iteratively to estimate the \gls{hmm} parameters. Then, the localization can infer how often is expected to see a dynamic object in the environment and for how long. Comparing the proposed \gls{hmm}-based localization to \gls{mcl} using a standard grid, the former had a lower localization failure rate than \gls{mcl}, capable of dealing with high dynamics (moving cars) and lower ones (parked cars).
Rapp~\etal~\cite{rapp:itsc:2015} implements a semi-Markov process extended by a Levy process to model a time dependency on the state holding time of Markov processes, also predicting as Tipaldi~\etal~\cite{tipaldi:ijrr:2013} the expected retention time for each cell being in a specific state. In the experiments, the proposed model \cite{rapp:itsc:2015} integrated in \gls{mcl} improved the classic \gls{mcl} in a dynamic environment.

The environment dynamics can have periodic patterns associated with them.
Assuming periodic changing patterns, Krajník~\etal~\cite{krajnik:tro:2017} proposes \gls{fremen} to model the probability of occupancy or feature visibility in a grid as a combination of harmonic functions related to periodic processes. \gls{fremen}~\cite{krajnik:tro:2017} uses spectral analysis (Fourier transform) to compute the harmonic functions and predict future state with a given confidence. In a changing environment, \gls{fremen}~\cite{krajnik:tro:2017} outperformed a static map in terms of localization error by selecting the most likely visible features at each location for localization.
Santos~\etal~\cite{santos:ral:2016} adopts \gls{fremen}~\cite{krajnik:tro:2017} within an exploration scheme, where the planner predicts which areas are more likely to change at a certain time and generate the subsequent locations to explore. The experimental results showed that considering the environment dynamics increases the amount of information gathered compared to static models.
Unlike \gls{fremen}~\cite{krajnik:tro:2017}, Wang~\etal~\cite{wang:iros:2020} models both aperiodic and periodic changes by an \gls{arma} model. This model describes time series as stationary stochastic processes in terms of polynomials. While \gls{fremen}~\cite{krajnik:tro:2017} is able to update recursively its model online, \gls{arma}~\cite{wang:iros:2020} only is updated once a day based on past observations. However, the model \cite{wang:iros:2020} achieved a higher prediction accuracy than \gls{fremen}~\cite{krajnik:tro:2017} and a lower localization failure rate than both \gls{fremen}~\cite{krajnik:tro:2017} and Tipaldi~\etal~\cite{tipaldi:ijrr:2013}.

Instead of modeling the dynamics in the map, Thomas~\etal~\cite{thomas:icra:2022} uses a KPConv network to predict online dynamic motion labels of points with single 3D laser scans as input. The method \cite{thomas:icra:2022} is a self-supervised learning approach with two main modules: PointMap and PointRay. PointMap is an \gls{icp}-based \gls{slam} algorithm to provide a point cloud map for the annotation process. PointRay uses a similar approach to Pomerleau~\etal~\cite{pomerleau:icra:2014} to approximate ray tracing using spherical coordinates for obtaining the training annotation of dynamic labels: permanent (static points over all sessions), ground (to avoid ray tracing ground samples), and long-term (still objects in single sessions but relocated between sessions) and short-term (dynamic objects) movables, with the localization not considering the latter two. In the simulation experiments, PointMap with the proposed prediction module led to lower localization pose errors than the standard \gls{mcl} algorithm.
