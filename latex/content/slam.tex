\chapter{Simultaneous Localization and Mapping~(SLAM) in Dynamic Environments}\label{chap:slam}

This chapter presents RicoSLAM, a graph-based 2D~\gls{slam} framework for dynamic
environments developed within the scope of this PhD~Thesis, building on the 2D
point cloud registration framework introduced in \chapref{chap:distance-maps}.
RicoSLAM integrates the distance map-based tracker from
\chapref{chap:distance-maps} as its front-end with the
srrg2\_solver~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020} optimization back-end to construct
an $SE(2)$ pose graph, detecting loop closures by combining nodes' proximity
in the world frame with graph-distance of the loop chain.
The proposed \gls{slam} framework filters dynamic observations
online during local map maintenance using a 2D laser scanner ray model,
while providing an offline 2D occupancy grid map generation step in which
the same ray model is combined with
Bresenham-like~\cite{bresenham:ibm:1965,zingl:uas:2016} line rasterization to
ignore dynamic observations when projecting all scans considered during online
\gls{slam} onto the final grid map. Additionally, this chapter presents
novel formulations for \gls{gba} based on distance map-based per-point factors,
building on the point-to-point and point-to-plane distance map-based
formulations presented in \chapref{chap:distance-maps}.
Experimental results indicate that RicoSLAM can operate in highly dynamic,
noisy environments while filtering dynamic observations from
the 2D~occupancy grid representation of the environment.

This chapter is organized as follows.
First, \secref{sec:slam:ricoslam} presents a high-level overview of the RicoSLAM
framework, positioning the contributions of this chapter in relation to
the components inherited from \chapref{chap:distance-maps}.
Next, \secref{sec:slam:online} describes the online \gls{slam} pipeline,
covering the $SE(2)$ pose-pose geodesic error factor and its Jacobians via
right-sided perturbation available in the
srrg2\_solver~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020} framework, the local map splitting
and merging criteria, the loop closure detection procedure, the relocalization
mechanism, and the pose history streams (laser odometry, online and offline
corrected poses) logged for trajectory evaluation.
\secref{sec:slam:dynamics} describes the dynamics removal strategy proposed in
this work, both online (during local map maintenance) and offline
(during 2D~occupancy grid map generation).
Moreover, \secref{sec:slam:gba} introduces the distance map-based \gls{gba}
per-point formulations proposed in this work, deriving the
point-to-point and point-to-plane error factors together with their Jacobians
via right-sided perturbation on the $SE(2)$ manifold.
Then, \secref{sec:slam:results} presents the experimental evaluation, including
a benchmark on the IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} dataset in terms of
accuracy and scalability, the impact of dynamics removal from the 2D occupancy
grid representation, and a synthetic benchmark to evaluate the distance
map-based \gls{gba} proposed in this work.
Lastly, \secref{sec:slam:conclusions} summarizes the main conclusions and
directions for future work.

\section{Framework Overview}\label{sec:slam:ricoslam}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{slam/system/system-overview.drawio.pdf}
  \caption[Block diagram of the RicoSLAM framework]{%
  Block diagram of the RicoSLAM framework.}
  \label{fig:slam:ricoslam}
\end{figure}

RicoSLAM is a 2D~graph \gls{slam} framework compatible with 2D~laser scanners
and wheeled odometry for dynamic environments in industrial \gls{amr} applications.
This framework represents the robot trajectory and the environment map as
a sparse factor graph~\cite{grisetti:itsm:2010,dellaert-kaess:ftr:2017}.
Each $SE(2)$ variable in the graph encodes the pose of a keyframe in
a common world frame, together with its measurement:
the locally built point cloud, the precomputed distance map representation
introduced in \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps}, and
the 2D~laser scanner messages aggregated within that keyframe. Each factor
encodes a relative-pose measurement between two keyframes, generated either by
odometric chaining of the front-end tracker or by loop closure detection.

\figref{fig:slam:ricoslam} presents a block diagram of RicoSLAM.
Internally, the system is organized around two top-level containers -- a tracker
front-end and a factor graph back-end -- with two additional offline
post-processing stages, a distance map-based \gls{gba} procedure and a
2D~occupancy grid mapper, that operate on the optimized pose graph once the
online run has completed. All the blocks in the diagram are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item The tracker is built on top of the distance map-based aligner from
      \chapref{chap:distance-maps} and is extended in this chapter with a
      dynamics-aware local mapping stage. Preprocessing
      (conversion of laser scan messages into a point cloud representation,
      voxelization, and normal computation) and the distance map-based
      point-to-point and point-to-plane formulations
      (see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pt} and
      \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl}) are inherited from
      \chapref{chap:distance-maps}. We also added to the framework both
      point-to-point~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} and
      point-to-plane~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991} \gls{icp} formulations for
      2D~point cloud registration with the tracker's aligner,
      using distance map-guided data association.
      New to this chapter are the split and merge triggering criteria,
      which monitor the aligner output stream
      (translation, orientation, viewpoint, inlier ratio, and the
      timestamp from the latest laser scan message processed by the tracker).
      The split criterion determines when to commit the current keyframe to
      the graph and start tracking on a new keyframe.
      The merge criterion determines when to merge new returns into
      the tracker's local map, using a merger container that performs
      online ray-based dynamics removal on that local map by treating
      each laser beam as a sensor model that may see through a previously
      mapped point or add new observations to the local representation.
      The tracker maintains the current keyframe --
      local point cloud, precomputed distance map, and array of raw laser scans
      aggregated since the last split -- and exposes its
      laser odometry estimate and alignment statistics to the back-end;

\item The factor graph back-end uses srrg2\_solver~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020}
      to solve the optimization problem and host all the components that operate
      on the graph. Its core data structure is the pose graph,
      populated by the tracker through the update-on-split action that
      promotes the current keyframe to a graph variable and adds a geodesic
      factor against the previously committed keyframe.
      The loop finder and graph visitor containers traverse the graph
      in search of loop closure candidates by combining world-frame proximity
      around the current robot pose with a graph-distance constraint that
      ensures non-trivial loop geometry. Each candidate is validated by
      the loop aligner, aligning the latest point cloud processed by the tracker
      against the candidate keyframe's local map, using two complementary
      initial guesses (pose graph-based and same-place-based). This loop aligner
      may be the same as the tracker's front-end or a different one.
      After a successful loop closure, a relative-pose geosedic factor is added
      between the current keyframe and the loop variables. Also, if
      the relocalization policy is enabled, it updates the tracker's keyframe
      from an existing one on the graph, relocalizing the robot in
      the existing back-end graph. The Gauss-Newton solver, provided by
      srrg2\_solver~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020} and the only third-party
      component of the framework, performs iterative least-squares optimization
      on the $SE(2)$ manifold with a sparse-block Cholesky linear solver,
      and is invoked incrementally whenever a loop closure factor is added to
      the graph. Furthermore, a pose history container makes available the three
      pose streams produced over time: the raw laser odometry from the tracker,
      the online \gls{slam} pose estimate, and the corrected pose based on
      the online estimate while considering the loop closures throughout time.
      RicoSLAM generates TUM~\cite{sturm:iros:2012} files for these three pose
      streams through external \gls{ros} services upon user request.
      Additionally, RicoSLAM saves the factor graph through the serialization
      framework made available through the
      srrg2\_solver~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020} into a human-readable JSON
      file, including keyframe variables and their global poses,
      the $SE(2)$ geosedic pose-pose factors, 2D~point clouds, and
      the arrays of 2D~laser scan messages associated with each keyframe;

\item Finally, two offline post-processing stages consume the optimized pose
      graph from the serialized JSON file after the online run completes.
      The \gls{gba} replaces the pose-pose constraints obtained from
      the tracker and the loop aligner by per-scan-point distance map residuals
      between different laser scan messages, thereby refining all scans that
      contributed to the online \gls{slam} map through novel distance map-based
      \gls{gba} formulations proposed in this chapter.
      The 2D~occupancy grid mapper generates a \gls{ros}-compatible occupancy grid map
      by Bresenham-based~\cite{bresenham:ibm:1965,zingl:uas:2016} rasterization
      of each retained scan, using the same laser ray model as
      the online merger to ignore the contributions of dynamic observations.
\end{itemize}

Throughout this chapter, the notation introduced in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:problem} is kept whenever possible.
Consistent with the convention adopted in \chapref{chap:distance-maps}
for $SE(2)$ optimization variables, a keyframe variable in the pose graph
is denoted by $\mathbf{x} \in SE(2)$ when it refers to a generic graph node,
and is given subscripts \emph{from} or \emph{to} when both endpoints of
a pose-pose geosedic factor must be distinguished.
The current pose \gls{wrt} the world frame is denoted
$\mathbf{x}_{R,W} = \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{kf}} \cdot \mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$,
where $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{kf}}$ is the current keyframe estimate and
$\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$ is the local laser odometry estimate of
the robot \gls{wrt} that keyframe. The right-sided perturbation update
$\mathbf{x} \oplus \mathbf{\Delta x} = \mathbf{x}\cdot\exp(\mathbf{\Delta x})$
adopted in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:manifold} and
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:problem} is used in all error and
Jacobian derivations of this chapter.

\section{Online SLAM}\label{sec:slam:online}

This section describes the online operation of RicoSLAM.
First, \secref{sec:slam:online:overview} presents the runtime pipeline that
processes each incoming 2D~laser scan message.
Next, \secref{sec:slam:online:splitting-merging} explains the merging and
splitting criteria used by the tracker to decide when
the local map of the current keyframe should be updated or when
a new node should be added to the back-end graph.
\secref{sec:slam:online:factor} derives the $SE(2)$ pose-pose
geodesic factor and its Jacobians via right-sided perturbation implemented in
srrg2\_solver~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020} that encodes pairwise constraints
between keyframes.
Furthermore, \secref{sec:slam:online:loop-closure} details the loop closure
detection procedure in RicoSLAM.
\secref{sec:slam:online:relocalization} describes the relocalization policy that
allows the system to track the robot pose in keyframes already within the graph,
mitigating the unbounded graph growth problem.
Finally, \secref{sec:slam:online:pose-history} discusses the two robot pose
history streams (online and offline corrected) logged by RicoSLAM
for trajectory evaluation.

\subsection{Processing pipeline}\label{sec:slam:online:overview}

The tracker front-end of RicoSLAM processes the 2D~laser scan message stream
from the sensor and, optionally, wheel odometry data for initialization during
the point cloud registration step. \algref{alg:slam:online:tracker} summarizes
the processing pipeline for the latest scan message ($\mathcal{S}$), updating
the local robot pose \gls{wrt} the current keyframe
($\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$) and providing a laser odometry estimate to
the RicoSLAM back-end. First, the tracker converts the range vector of the
incoming laser scan ($\mathcal{S}$) into a 2D~point cloud ($\mathcal{P}$) in the
sensor frame using the scanner's angular parametrization, preserving the data ordering.
This conversion marks points whose ranges fall outside the configured admissible
range interval or outside the sensor's minimum and maximum ranges as invalid,
so the aligner ignores them. Two additional preprocessing steps include
computing the point cloud normals
(see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals}) and voxelizing
the point cloud using a given voxelization scale ($r_v$), or assuming
the same resolution as the distance map representation.
Next, the tracker updates $\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$ using wheel odometry data
if the user enables it. If not, the tracker assumes the robot's pose from
the previous scan message for aligner initialization. In the case that it is
the first laser scan message processed by the tracker, the latter initializes
the local map ($\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{kf}}$), building its distance map
representation and appending the first laser scan message, and initializes the
local robot pose as an identity $SE(2)$ pose, changing the state of
the tracker to \emph{Initialized}.

\input{algorithms/slam/tracker.tex}

Then, the tracker's aligner estimates the local robot pose $\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$
that best aligns the moving cloud ($\mathcal{P}$) from the latest scan message ($\mathcal{S}$)
to the reference cloud of the current keyframe. This aligner implements the
distance map-based formulations proposed in \chapref{chap:distance-maps}, with
the option also to use \gls{icp} point-to-point~\cite{besl-mckay:tpami:1992} and
point-to-plane~\cite{chen-medioni:icra:1991} formulations.
If the aligner fails the registration process, the tracker resets its
local map ($\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{kf}}$) and local robot pose
($\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$), setting the internal state to \emph{Error}.
After updating $\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$ and its information matrix
($\boldsymbol{\Omega}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$) upon a successful registration,
the tracker changes its internal state to \emph{Tracking}.
Afterward, the tracker first evaluates whether the split criterion is triggered
based on the aligner stats ($\chi_{\mathrm{inlier}}^2$, inlier ratio),
$\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$, and the timestamp of the latest scan message.
If the split criterion is triggered, the tracker changes its current keyframe
and sets an $SE(2)$ identity pose, changing the internal state to
\emph{TrackingMeasurementSplit}. Second, if the split criterion is not triggered
and the merge criterion is triggered based on the same evaluation as for
the former, the tracker's merger fuses the moving cloud ($\mathcal{P}$) into
the local map ($\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{kf}}$) using the online ray-based dynamics
removal model (see~\secref{sec:slam:dynamics:online}).
The raw scan is appended to the keyframe's history of aggregated measurements,
changing the internal state to \emph{TrackingMeasurementMerged}.
Testing splitting before merging ensures that a scan satisfying both criteria is
committed as a new keyframe rather than merged into an outgoing one.
The five states admissible by the tracker --
\emph{Initialized}, \emph{Error}, \emph{Tracking},
\emph{TrackingMeasurementSplit}, and \emph{TrackingMeasurementMerged} --
are then used by the back-end of RicoSLAM to decide whether to
insert a fixed keyframe, an odometric pose-pose factor, or simply
update the corrected-pose stream.

Next, \algref{alg:slam:online} summarizes the online processing pipeline
triggered when a new scan message ($\mathcal{S}$) arrives at
the RicoSLAM system. First, as already explained, the tracker processes
the message stream and the wheel odometry data
(see~\algref{alg:slam:online:tracker}). Depending on the tracker's status --
\emph{Initialized} (first scan after the tracker initialized its current
keyframe), \emph{Error} (registration failure), or
\emph{TrackingMeasurementSplit} (the splitting criterion fired making
the tracker changing its current keyframe) -- a new graph node may be inserted
with either a fixed status (reserved for the first keyframe variable of
the back-end factor graph) or an active status with an odometric pose-pose
factor linking it to the previous keyframe. The distinction between
fixed and active variables is what makes the pose graph optimization problem
well-posed. The cost minimized by the Gauss-Newton solver is a sum of
geodesic errors of the form
$\left(\mathbf{x}_i^{-1}\cdot\mathbf{x}_j\right)\ominus\mathbf{z}_{ij}$,
which depend only on the relative pose between the two endpoints
(see~\secref{sec:literature:slam:graph}). Applying any transformation
$\mathbf{g}\in SE(2)$ to every variable in the graph leaves unchanged
these relative quantities their the cost. Consequently,
the Gauss-Newton approximate Hessian
$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J}^{T}\boldsymbol{\Omega}\mathbf{J}$ is
rank-deficient and any trajectory would be determined only up to
a global rigid-body transformation. Marking the first keyframe as
fixed eliminates its three columns from the normal equations, makes the reduced
Hessian full-rank, and defines the world frame the same as that
keyframe~\cite{grisetti:itsm:2010,grisetti:robotics:2020}.

\input{algorithms/slam/slam.tex}

After the keyframe-management step, the back-end of RicoSLAM updates
the robot pose \gls{wrt} the world frame ($\mathbf{x}_{R,W}$)
by composing the current keyframe estimate in the factor graph
($\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{kf}}$) with the local laser odometry of the robot
\gls{wrt} that keyframe ($\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$), as formulated in
\eqref{eq:slam:online:robot-in-world}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:online:robot-in-world}
\mathbf{x}_{R,W} = \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{kf}}\cdot\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}
\end{equation}

If loop closure detection is enabled, the back-end of RicoSLAM invokes the loop
finder to compute the set of candidate hints
(see~\secref{sec:slam:online:loop-closure}).
Each hint is then validated by running the loop aligner (either the same
distance map-based aligner used by the tracker or a dedicated instance)
on the current moving point cloud against the candidate keyframe's local
map. Successfully aligned hints generate loop closure factors that are added
to the graph, based on the loop aligner estimation of the robot \gls{wrt} the
loop keyframe ($\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{R,\ell}$) and the robot \gls{wrt} the current
one ($\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$).
If at least one loop factor is added,
the solver runs a single optimization pass and the world-frame robot pose
is updated accordingly (see \eqref{eq:slam:online:robot-in-world}).
Finally, if relocalization is enabled and at least one
short-loop candidate is available, the relocalization policy described in
\secref{sec:slam:online:relocalization} may transfer the tracker into an
existing graph node instead of growing the graph further.

\subsection{Splitting and merging criteria}
\label{sec:slam:online:splitting-merging}

The tracker is responsible for maintaining the local map attached to
the current keyframe, serving as the reference cloud for 2D~point cloud
registration (see \chapref{chap:distance-maps} and
\secref{sec:slam:online:overview}). Two criteria control
the update on the local map: a splitting criterion that decides when
the local map should be replaced by a new keyframe and inserted into
the back-end pose graph, and a merging criterion that triggers
the integration of the moving cloud from the latest 2D~laser scan message into
the active local map by the ray-based merger
(see~\secref{sec:slam:dynamics:online}), updating both
the reference point cloud and its precomputed distance map, while adding
the latest laser message to the keyframe's history of aggregated scans.

Architecturally, both criteria types are implemented through the same interface,
differing only in their parametrization and in the cadence at which
the tracker resets the criteria's internal state. Each criterion type exposes
a common compute method, evaluated for every successfully registered scan
(see \algref{alg:slam:online:tracker}), considering the scan timestamp ($t$),
the current robot pose \gls{wrt} the local frame ($\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$),
and the inlier ratio reported by the aligner ($\eta_\mathrm{inliers}$). All
criteria store an internal state composed by an $SE(2)$ reference pose
($\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{ref}}$) and a reference timestamp ($t_\mathrm{ref}$),
updated upon triggering events or when the tracker changes its local map.
As a result, all criteria types based on $SE(2)$ displacement use
\eqref{eq:slam:online:splitting-merging:delta} to evaluate triggering events,
based on the logarithmic mapping defined in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:x:log}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:online:splitting-merging:delta}
\boldsymbol{\Delta} =
\left(\boldsymbol{\Delta}_{\mathbf{t}}^{T},\ \Delta_{\theta}\right)^{T}
=
\log\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{ref}}^{-1}\cdot
\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}\right) \in \mathbb{R}^{3}, \;
% \mathrm{where} \;
\boldsymbol{\Delta}_{\mathbf{t}} = \left(\Delta_x, \Delta_y\right)
\end{equation}

Moreover, the splitting criterion is reset once per keyframe lifecycle,
at the moment the local map is created, with the reference pose always set to
the identity $\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{ref}} =
\left[\mathbf{I}_{2\times 2}\,|\,\mathbf{0}_{2\times 1}\right]$.
Thus, its displacement $\boldsymbol{\Delta}_{\mathrm{split}}$
measures the total displacement accumulated since the creation of the current
keyframe. In contrast, the merging criterion is reset not only at keyframe
creation but also after each successful merge event, with the reference pose
updated to the robot pose at the last merge event
$\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{ref}} = \mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$.
As a result, its displacement $\boldsymbol{\Delta}_{\mathrm{merge}}$ measures
the incremental displacement since the last update of the local map.

RicoSLAM implements several criteria types as follows, all built on
\eqref{eq:slam:online:splitting-merging:delta} unless noted otherwise:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{translation:} $\|\boldsymbol{\Delta}_{\mathbf{t}}\|_{2} > d_{\min}$;
\item \textbf{rotation:} $|\Delta_{\theta}| > \theta_{\min}$;
\item \textbf{viewpoint:} combination of the previous two;
\item \textbf{grid cell:} $|\Delta_{x}| > s/2$ or $|\Delta_{y}| > s/2$ for a
cell of side length $s$ centered on the frame $\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{ref}}$;
\item \textbf{inlier ratio:} $\eta_{\mathrm{inliers}} < \eta_{\max}$, while
providing configurable minimum displacement $(d_{\min},\theta_{\min})$
requirements before triggering, or an unconditional maximum displacement rule
$(d_{\max},\theta_{\max})$;
\item \textbf{time:} $t-t_{\mathrm{ref}} > \Delta t_{\max}$
\end{itemize}
Additionally, the framework provides a force trigger interface through external
\gls{ros}~\cite{ros} services for all the criteria types mentioned before,
while also providing an empty type option to turn off the triggering mechanisms.
The latter only allows trigger events through the forcing interface.

In typical configurations, RicoSLAM uses the same type for both roles,
with the merging thresholds set tighter than the splitting thresholds so that
multiple merges accumulate within a keyframe's lifecycle before
the splitting criterion triggers. Assigning the no-op variant to either role
gives rise to two practical operating modes: a scan-based graph \gls{slam} mode
and a local maps variant. The former has the merging criterion disabled and
every registered scan becomes its own keyframe. The latter has both criteria
active and multiple scans are aggregated per keyframe before a split.

\subsection{$SE(2)$ pose-pose geodesic error factor}
\label{sec:slam:online:factor}

Pairwise pose constraints between keyframes are encoded in the back-end graph by
an $SE(2)$ pose-pose geodesic error factor available in the
srrg2\_solver~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020} framework. RicoSLAM only adapts
the original constraint to be compatible with the custom distance map-based
$SE(2)$ variables implemented in the scope of this PhD~Thesis.
Let $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}, \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}} \in SE(2)$
be the two keyframe variables connected by the factor, and let
$\mathbf{z}\in SE(2)$ be the measured relative pose of $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}$
\gls{wrt} $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}$, provided either by the tracker
(odometric edges) or by the loop aligner (loop closure edges).
The prediction $\hat{\mathbf{z}}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}},\,\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\right)$
is given by \eqref{eq:slam:online:factor:prediction}, and the residual
$\mathbf{e}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}},\,\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\right)$
is the \emph{logarithmic mapping} of the discrepancy between the prediction and
the measurement on the manifold, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:slam:online:factor:error}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:online:factor:prediction}
\hat{\mathbf{z}}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}},\,\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\right)
= \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}^{-1}\cdot\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}} \in SE(2)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:online:factor:error}
\mathbf{e}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}},\,\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\right) =
\log\!\left(\mathbf{z}^{-1}\cdot
            \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}^{-1}\cdot\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\right)
\in \mathbb{R}^3
\end{equation}

Following the convention of
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:problem}, the
\emph{logarithmic mapping} from $SE(2)$ to its tangent space is the vector
$(t_x, t_y, \atan2(r_{2,1}, r_{2,2}))^T$ that stacks the translational
components with the angular residual extracted from the rotation matrix.
Let $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{z}^{-1}\cdot\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}^{-1}\cdot
\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\in SE(2)$ denote the residual $SE(2)$
transformation, with rotation matrix $\mathbf{R}_E$ and translation
$\mathbf{t}_E$. Then
$\mathbf{e} = \left(\mathbf{t}_E^T,\;\atan2(r_{E,21}, r_{E,22})\right)^T$.

\subsubsection{Jacobian matrices via right-sided perturbation}
\label{sec:slam:online:factor:jacobian}

Applying the right-sided perturbation
$\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}\oplus\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}}
= \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}\cdot\exp(\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}})$
and $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\oplus\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}}
= \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\cdot\exp(\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}})$, with
$\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}},\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}}\in\mathbb{R}^3$,
the perturbed residual on the manifold is given by
\eqref{eq:slam:online:factor:perturbed}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:online:factor:perturbed}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{e}\!\left(
  \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}\oplus\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}},\,
  \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\oplus\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}}
\right)
& = \log\!\left(\mathbf{z}^{-1}\cdot
                \exp(\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}})^{-1}\cdot
                \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}^{-1}\cdot
                \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\cdot
                \exp(\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}})\right)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The Jacobian matrices
$\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{from}},\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{to}}\in\mathbb{R}^{3\times3}$
are obtained by differentiating
\eqref{eq:slam:online:factor:perturbed} \gls{wrt}
$\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}}$ and
$\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}}$ at
$\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}}=\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}}=\mathbf{0}$.
The derivation applies the chain rule to the logarithmic mapping that
extracts $(t_x, t_y, \theta)$ from an $SE(2)$ pose.
The block structure of both Jacobians, with $\mathbf{R}_E$ the rotation
matrix of the residual $SE(2)$ pose at $\mathbf{\Delta x}=\mathbf{0}$,
the rotation matrix $\mathbf{R}_z$ of the relative pose measurement
$\mathbf{z}$, and $\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{from}}$, $\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{to}}$
the rotation matrices of the variables under linearization, separates
the translational rows $(1{:}2)$ from the rotational row $(3)$,
since the logarithmic mapping extracts these components independently.
\eqref{eq:slam:online:factor:jacobian-from} and
\eqref{eq:slam:online:factor:jacobian-to} formulate
$\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{from}}$ and $\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{to}}$,
respectively, where $\boldsymbol{\partial}_{\atan2}$ is the partial derivative
of the $\atan2$ map \gls{wrt} the second row entries of $\mathbf{R}_E$
under an infinitesimal in-place rotation, $\Delta\mathbf{t} =
\mathbf{t}_{\mathrm{to}}-\mathbf{t}_{\mathrm{from}}$ is the world-frame
translational delta between the two keyframes, and
$\lfloor\cdot\rfloor$ denotes the 2D~skew operator introduced in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:skew}.
The rotational row of both Jacobians arises from differentiating
$\atan2(r_{E,21}, r_{E,22})$ via the chain rule, with
$\boldsymbol{\partial}_{\atan2} = (-r_{E,21},\,r_{E,22})^T /
(r_{E,21}^2 + r_{E,22}^2)$.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:online:factor:jacobian-from}
\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{from}} =
\begin{bmatrix}
-\mathbf{R}_z^T &
\mathbf{R}_z^T\cdot\lfloor\cdot\rfloor^T\cdot \mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{from}}^T\cdot\Delta\mathbf{t} \\[3pt]
\mathbf{0}_{1\times2} &
\boldsymbol{\partial}_{\atan2}^T \cdot
\left[\mathbf{R}_z^T\cdot\lfloor\cdot\rfloor^T\cdot
      \mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{from}}^T\cdot\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{to}}\right]_{(2,:)}^T
\end{bmatrix}
\in\mathbb{R}^{3\times3}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:online:factor:jacobian-to}
\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{to}} =
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{R}_E &
\mathbf{0}_{2\times1} \\[3pt]
\mathbf{0}_{1\times2} &
\boldsymbol{\partial}_{\atan2}^T \cdot
\left[\mathbf{R}_E\cdot\lfloor\cdot\rfloor\right]_{(2,:)}^T
\end{bmatrix}
\in\mathbb{R}^{3\times3}
\end{equation}

The information matrix $\boldsymbol{\Omega}\in\mathbb{R}^{3\times3}$ of the
factor is either inherited from the tracker's aligner (the approximate
Hessian $\mathbf{H}$ at convergence; see
\secref{sec:distance-maps:background:least-squares:uncertainty}) or fixed
to a user-specified diagonal, typically giving higher confidence to the heading
component than to the translational ones. RicoSLAM supports both options
in terms of the framework parametrization.

\subsection{Loop closure detection}\label{sec:slam:online:loop-closure}

Loop closure detection in RicoSLAM considers geometric and topological distance
when searching for candidate keyframes. \algref{alg:slam:online:loop-finder}
summarizes the loop finder procedure implemented in the scope of this PhD~Thesis.
In the first stage, all graph variables whose Euclidean distance to the current
robot pose in the world frame falls below $d_{\ell,\max}$ are inserted into
a short-loop hint set $\mathcal{H}_s$, together with the relative pose
$\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{R,c} =
\mathbf{x}_c^{-1}\cdot\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{kf}}\cdot\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$
that propagates the robot pose into the candidate keyframe's coordinate frame.
This relative pose may be used as the initial guess of the loop aligner.
In the second stage, a graph visitor computes the topological distance
(in number of edges) from the current keyframe to each candidate.
The visitor implements a best-first wavefront expansion analogous to the one
used in \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:computation},
but operating on the pose graph topology rather than on a cell grid:
sources are pushed into a priority queue, and entries are expanded through
the graph factors incident to each variable. A candidate $h$ is promoted from
the short-loop set $\mathcal{H}_s$ to the loop hint set $\mathcal{H}$ only when
its graph length $l_{\ell,h}$ exceeds the threshold $L_{\min}$.
The motivation of considering a minimum topological distance on the loop chain
is to avoid attempting loop closures between graph nodes that are connected
through a short path of odometry edges, since such constraints carry no
significant topological information and are likely to be redundant
with the existing pose-pose factors.

\input{algorithms/slam/loop-closure.tex}

Each hint is then validated by running the loop aligner on the
moving point cloud against the candidate keyframe's local map.
Two initial guesses are attempted in sequence, when enabled by the user:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item the \emph{pose graph guess}
$\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{R,c}$, propagated from the current pose graph estimate;
\item the \emph{same-place guess}
$\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{same}} = \exp\!\left((0, 0, \theta_{\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{R,c}})^T\right)$,
which considers the robot at the origin of the candidate frame but with
the heading from the pose graph guess, equivalent to seeing the same place
from a different perspective.
\end{itemize}
If either guess yields a successful registration of the moving cloud in
the local map of a hint from the loop set $\mathcal{H}$,
the back-end of RicoSLAM inserts the corresponding $SE(2)$ pose-pose geosedic
factor (see~\secref{sec:slam:online:factor}) with a measurement
$\mathbf{z} = \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{R,c}^{\,*} \cdot
\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}^{-1}$, where $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{R,c}^{\,*}$ is
the estimated pose returned by the loop aligner.
When at least one loop closure factor is added to graph, the
back-end solver optimizes the whole pose graph.
This incremental optimization policy avoids the cost of running the
back-end on every scan while ensuring that every newly added loop closure
constraint is propagated through the rest of the graph.

\subsection{Relocalization into the existing graph}
\label{sec:slam:online:relocalization}

A common problem in online graph \gls{slam} is unbounded graph growth when
the robot revisits a previously mapped area where incoming scans continue
to trigger the creation of new keyframes even when existing ones
already cover the same location. A method widely used in
the literature is graph sparsification, which identifies and removes
redundant nodes by marginalizing the removed variables and
introducing edges between their neighbors to preserve
the original information content~\cite{sousa:jfr:2023}.
In contrast, RicoSLAM mitigates unbounded graph growth through
a relocalization mechanism. After a loop closure, RicoSLAM may change
the current keyframe on the tracker to the best-matching existing node
in the short-loop set $\mathcal{H}_s$ rather than inserting a new one.
This approach reuses the existing graph information
instead of expanding it or removing redundant nodes.

Let $\mathcal{H}_s$ be the short-loop hint set returned by the loop finder
(see~\secref{sec:slam:online:loop-closure}). When relocalization is enabled
and $\mathcal{H}_s$ is non-empty, RicoSLAM uses the loop aligner also to
register the moving point cloud from the latest scan message in the local map
of short hints. Then, the system evaluates the two distances formulated in
\eqref{eq:slam:online:relocalization:distances} for the closest candidate
$h_s^*$ with successful registration from the loop aligner.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:online:relocalization:distances}
d_{\mathrm{loop}} =
\left\|\left(\mathbf{x}_{h_s^*}^{-1}\cdot\mathbf{x}_{R,W}\right)
       .\mathrm{translation}()\right\|_2,
\;
d_{\mathrm{local}} =
\left\|\mathbf{t}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}\right\|_2
\end{equation}

The distance $d_{\mathrm{loop}}$ is between the current robot pose and
the best short-loop candidate in the world frame, while $d_{\mathrm{local}}$
measures the distance between the robot and the current keyframe. Whenever
$d_{\mathrm{loop}} < d_{\mathrm{local}}$, RicoSLAM changes the current keyframe
of the tracker to the candidate by resetting its local map to the one
associated with $h_s^*$. This relocalization forces subsequent scan registrations
against an existing graph node rather than creating new ones.
In scan-based mode, the relocalization makes the robot pose being tracked
within the existing graph structure. In local map mode, relocalization not only
tracks the robot within the existing graph but also mergers new observations
into an existing keyframe's local map rather than creating new nodes.
The relocalization policy yields in both cases the benefit of the graph size
becoming bounded by the spatial extent of the explored environment rather than
by the duration of the run. The impact of this mechanism on trajectory accuracy
and graph growth is evaluated experimentally in
\secref{sec:slam:results:iilabs3d} and
\secref{sec:slam:results:relocalization}, respectively.

\subsection{Pose history logging}\label{sec:slam:online:pose-history}

In addition to the laser odometry from the tracker, RicoSLAM logs
two pose history streams that can be used for robot trajectory evaluation or
consumed by other applications: the online and offline corrected pose streams.
The online stream represents the robot pose
$\mathbf{x}_{R,W}^{\mathrm{online}}(t)$ in the world frame during
scan processing, using only the online estimation of the robot \gls{wrt}
the current keyframe $\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}(t)$ and the current
keyframe's pose in the world frame $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{kf}}(t)$,
as formulated in \eqref{eq:slam:online:pose-history:online}. This stream is
consistent with RicoSLAM's real-time operation and is the one consumed by
downstream modules that subscribe to the robot pose topic at runtime.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:online:pose-history:online}
\mathbf{x}_{R,W}^{\mathrm{online}}(t) =
\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{kf}}(t)\cdot\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}(t)
\end{equation}

On the other hand, the offline corrected stream stores only the current keyframe
identifier $\mathrm{id}_{\mathrm{kf}}(t)$ and the local pose of the robot
\gls{wrt} the keyframe $\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}(t)$ together with
the scan timestamp $t$ at runtime. Upon user request, the world-frame are
reconstructed \emph{a posteriori} using the optimized keyframe poses from the
complete pose graph. The resulting offline corrected pose
$\mathbf{x}_{R,W}^{\mathrm{offline}}(t)$ is defined by
\eqref{eq:slam:online:pose-history:offline}, which substitutes the incremental
keyframe global pose from the online counterpart by the optimized keyframe
estimate $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{id}_{\mathrm{kf}}(t)}^{\,*}(t)$.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:online:pose-history:offline}
\mathbf{x}_{R,W}^{\mathrm{offline}}(t) =
\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{id}_{\mathrm{kf}}(t)}^{\,*}(t)\cdot
\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{id}_{\mathrm{kf}}(t)}(t)
\end{equation}

The benefit of the offline corrected stream is that past poses
inherit the corrections from future loop closures. Experiments presented in
\secref{sec:slam:results:iilabs3d} report the results considering both online
and offline pose streams, exposing the impact of loop closures and
back-end graph optimization on the recovered trajectory.

\section{Dynamics Removal}\label{sec:slam:dynamics}

Mobile robots deployed in industrial, intralogistics, and service-oriented
applications typically operate in environments with transient, dynamic obstacles
(people, other robots and manual operated platforms, moving carts, pallets) that
conflict with the static-world assumption underlying classical 2D~\gls{slam}
formulations. RicoSLAM addresses these dynamics at two distinct stages.
\secref{sec:slam:dynamics:online} describes the online dynamics removal
mechanism embedded in RicoSLAM's runtime operation, which maintains
the local map of each keyframe during tracking upon merge-triggering events.
\secref{sec:slam:dynamics:offline} describes the offline dynamics removal that
operates during 2D~occupancy grid map generation, exploiting
Bresenham~\cite{bresenham:ibm:1965,zingl:uas:2016} line rasterization and
the laser ray model to ignore dynamic returns when integrating
all scans into the final map.

\subsection{Online dynamics removal via ray analysis}
\label{sec:slam:dynamics:online}

The online dynamics removal mechanism is executed whenever the merging criterion
triggers (see~\secref{sec:slam:online:splitting-merging}), integrating
the moving cloud from the latest laser scan message into the current keyframe's
local map. Then, the merger (see~\figref{fig:slam:ricoslam}
and~\algref{alg:slam:online:tracker}) evaluates for every laser ray of
the current scan whether the observation is consistent with the points of
the local map that lie on the same ray.

Let $\mathcal{M} = \{\mathbf{m}_j\}_{j=1}^{N_m}$ denote the local map point
cloud of the current keyframe, expressed in the keyframe's sensor frame
$S_{\mathrm{kf}}$. Let $\mathcal{P} = \{\mathbf{z}_i\}_{i=1}^{N_z}$ denote
the current moving cloud, expressed in the latest scan's sensor frame
$S_{\mathrm{curr}}$. Let $\mathbf{T}_{S_{\mathrm{kf}}}^{S_{\mathrm{curr}}}$ be
the $SE(2)$ transformation from the keyframe's sensor frame $S_{\mathrm{kf}}$ to
the current sensor frame $S_{\mathrm{curr}}$, considering
the current robot pose \gls{wrt} the keyframe $\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}$ and
the sensor \gls{wrt} the robot transformation $\mathbf{T}_S^R$
(see \secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:problem}).
For each map point $\mathbf{m}_j$, the merger expresses it in the current
sensor frame, following \eqref{eq:slam:dynamics:online:transform}.
Then, the merger queries $\mathrm{ptToRayIdx}(\cdot)$ based on the current scan
angle parametrization (minimum scanning angle $\alpha_{\mathcal{S},\min}$,
angle increment between consecutive rays $\Delta\alpha_{\mathcal{S}}$) to
recover the ray index $k_j$ on which $\mathbf{m}_j$ would fall if observed by
the current scan (see~\eqref{eq:slam:dynamics:online:ray-assignment}).
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:dynamics:online:transform}
\mathbf{m}_j^{S_{\mathrm{curr}}} =
\mathbf{T}_R^S
\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}^{-1}
\mathbf{T}_S^R
\mathbf{m}_j =
\mathbf{T}_{S_{\mathrm{kf}}}^{S_{\mathrm{curr}}}\,\mathbf{m}_j
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:dynamics:online:ray-assignment}
k_j = \mathrm{ptToRayIdx}\!\left(\mathbf{m}_j^{S_{\mathrm{curr}}}\right) =
\left\lfloor
  \frac{\atan2( m.y_j^{S_{\mathrm{curr}}} , m.x_j^{S_{\mathrm{curr}}} ) -
        \alpha_{\mathcal{S},\min}}{\Delta\alpha_{\mathcal{S}}}
\right\rceil
\end{equation}

For each sensor ray, the merger retains the closest map point along that ray.
This assumption is consistent with the single-return model of a 2D~laser
scanner, in which each ray yields at most one range measurement corresponding to
the nearest surface. Although in the point cloud representation of the local map
multiple points may project onto the same ray, retaining only the closest one
keeps the merger computationally efficient regardless of local map density.
Let ${\mathbf{m}^{S_{\mathrm{curr}}}_{k}}^{*}$ be the closest local map point on
ray $k$, with range
$\rho_{\mathcal{M},k} = \|{\mathbf{m}^{S_{\mathrm{curr}}}_{k}}^{*}\|_2$.
Let $\mathbf{z}_k$ be the current observation on ray $k$, with range
$\rho_{\mathcal{S},k} = \|\mathbf{z}_k\|_2$. The range
difference $\Delta\rho_k = \rho_{\mathcal{S},k} - \rho_{\mathcal{M},k}$
drives the update rules formulated in
\eqref{eq:slam:dynamics:online:rules}, where $\tau_{\mathrm{new}} > 0$ (m)
and $\tau_{\mathrm{through}} > 0$ (m) are user-defined thresholds of RicoSLAM.
These thresholds avoid updating the local map when the range difference is
closer to 0, given that the sensor is affected by measurement noise.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:dynamics:online:rules}
\text{Action on ray } k =
\begin{cases}
\text{add } \mathbf{z}_k \text{ as a new map point} &
\text{if no representative local point on ray } k,\\
\text{add } \mathbf{z}_k \text{ as a new map point} &
\text{if } \Delta\rho_k < -\tau_{\mathrm{new}}
\quad (\mathbf{z}_k \text{ in front of } {\mathbf{m}^{S_{\mathrm{curr}}}_{k}}^{*}),\\
\text{replace map point with } \mathbf{z}_k &
\text{if } \Delta\rho_k > +\tau_{\mathrm{through}}
\quad \text{(see-through)},\\
\text{keep map unchanged} &
\text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

The see-through rule is responsible for removing online dynamics in RicoSLAM.
If the current observation reports a range substantially larger than
the existing map point on the same ray, the laser must have seen past
the previously mapped obstacle, which was therefore likely a dynamic return.
In this case, the merger replaces the map point with the new observation,
erasing the dynamic obstacle from the local map.
Conversely, the add-new rule handles new observations in front of existing map
points following the ray model of the sensor, allowing the local map to update
unobserved regions of the keyframe continuously.
RicoSLAM allows also an optional averaging of existing map points with
the current observation on the same ray using weights
$(w_{\mathrm{old}}, w_{\mathrm{new}})$ with
$w_{\mathrm{old}} + w_{\mathrm{new}} = 1$, smoothing points across multiple
observations, when the keep the map unchanged rule from
\eqref{eq:slam:dynamics:online:rules} fires. Still, this option is not
considered in the experiments in the scope of this PhD~Thesis.

Whenever an update is performed in the local map, the distance map data
structure attached to the keyframe variable is rebuilt from the updated point
cloud using the same propagation algorithm of
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:computation}, with the same
resolution, size, and truncation distance parameters initially set by the user.
This approach guarantees that the next alignment iteration of the tracker
against the updated keyframe queries a distance map representation consistent
with the latest local map state.

\subsection{Offline dynamics removal in 2D occupancy grid mapping}
\label{sec:slam:dynamics:offline}

After the online \gls{slam} run completes, RicoSLAM optionally produces
a 2D~occupancy grid map suitable for \gls{ros}~\cite{ros}-oriented and
downstream navigation applications. The procedure integrates all scans onto
a discrete grid through two mechanisms: an optional ray-based stage that reuses
the merger of the online dynamics removal mechanism
(see~\secref{sec:slam:dynamics:online}) to filter dynamic returns out of
a global map point cloud, and a probabilistic stage in which the per-cell
occupancy probability is estimated from a beam-endpoint sensor model coupled
with Bresenham~\cite{bresenham:ibm:1965,zingl:uas:2016}-based line
rasterization. The ray-based stage represents the explicit offline removal of
dynamics from the 2D~occupancy grid map representation
proposed in the scope of this PhD~Thesis.

RicoSLAM uses the laser scan messages stored in each keyframe's measurement
owner (see~\secref{sec:slam:online:overview}) and on the final pose graph
estimate to produce the 2D~occupancy grid map. For each retained scan
$\mathcal{S}$ with the associated robot \gls{wrt} the local map pose
$\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}^{\mathcal{S}}$ and the optimized parent keyframe
estimate \gls{wrt} the world $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{kf}}^{\mathcal{S}}$, the
sensor pose in the world frame $\mathbf{x}_{S,W}^{\mathcal{S}}$ is reconstructed
by \eqref{eq:slam:dynamics:offline:sensor-in-world}, where $\mathbf{T}_S^R$ is
the sensor \gls{wrt} the robot pose
(see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:problem}).
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:dynamics:offline:sensor-in-world}
\mathbf{x}_{S,W}^{\mathcal{S}} =
\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{kf}}^{\mathcal{S}}\cdot
\mathbf{x}_{R,\mathrm{kf}}^{\mathcal{S}}\cdot\mathbf{T}_S^R
\end{equation}

Before the rasterization step, RicoSLAM invokes the same ray-based merger used
at the online stage to build a cleaned global point cloud from all scans
retained in the graph keyframes. This invocation differs from
the online dynamics removal by globally evaluating the ray-based model
rather than affecting only the local map of the current keyframe at runtime.
The merger is enabled whenever the user configures either of the two ray-based
thresholds (see~\secref{sec:slam:dynamics:online}) to generate the grid map:
$\tau_{\mathrm{new}}$, the minimum distance
for an incoming beam to be considered a see-through of an existing map point,
and $\tau_{\mathrm{new}}$, the minimum distance for a new return to be
considered in front of a previously unobserved point.
When enabled, the merger processes the scans one at a time, using
the world-frame sensor pose $\mathbf{x}_{S,W}^{\mathcal{S}}$
(see~\eqref{eq:slam:dynamics:offline:sensor-in-world}) to project each scan
into the global frame. Map points consistently seen by later beams are removed,
and new returns in front of existing map points are added to the global cloud.
The output of this stage is a cleaned global point cloud paired with a
scan-to-map-point correspondence that records, for each retained scan, which of
its endpoints survived the filtering. This correspondence is required for
Bresenham~\cite{bresenham:ibm:1965,zingl:uas:2016}-like rasterization, ensuring
that endpoints discarded by the merger do not contribute to
the hit-pass frequency counters.

Each grid cell of the 2D~occupancy grid map stores two counters,
$\mathbf{f}(r,c) = (n_{\mathrm{hit}}, n_{\mathrm{pass}}) \in \mathbb{N}^2$,
representing respectively the number of times the cell has been
observed as occupied (beam endpoint) and the number of times it has
been traversed by a beam (free pass). For each valid range
$\rho_\alpha\in[\rho_{\min},\rho_{\max}]$ of the scan, the beam
endpoint is computed in the world frame by
\eqref{eq:slam:dynamics:offline:beam-endpoint}, where
$\alpha\in[\alpha_{\min},\alpha_{\max}]$ is the beam angle in the
sensor frame and $\mathrm{angleDistToPt}(\alpha,\rho)$ converts a
polar pair into its Cartesian representation in the sensor frame.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:dynamics:offline:beam-endpoint}
\mathbf{p}_\alpha^{W} =
\mathbf{x}_{S,W}^{\mathcal{S}}\cdot
\mathrm{angleDistToPt}(\alpha,\rho_\alpha) =
\mathbf{x}_{S,W}^{\mathcal{S}}\cdot
\rho_\alpha\cdot
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos(\alpha)\\
\sin(\alpha)
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

The grid indices $(r_0,c_0)$ and $(r_e,c_e)$ associated, respectively,
with the beam origin $\mathbf{x}_{S,W}^{\mathcal{S}}$ and the beam endpoint
$\mathbf{p}_\alpha^{W}$ are obtained via the affine mapping introduced
in \eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps:conversion:global2local},
adapted to the occupancy grid resolution. Then, the set of intermediate cells
traversed by the beam is computed following an implementation of the
Bresenham~\cite{bresenham:ibm:1965} line rasterization algorithm that handles
negative gradients or reversed lines~\cite{zingl:uas:2016}.
This traversal incrementally walks from $(r_0,c_0)$ to $(r_e,c_e)$ updating
the integer error term that selects the next pixel along the
dominant axis, guaranteeing an integer-only implementation.
For each cell $(r,c)$ visited along the beam, the pass counter is incremented:
$n_{\mathrm{pass}}(r,c) \mathrel{+}= 1$. At the endpoint cell, the
hit counter is also incremented,
$n_{\mathrm{hit}}(r_e,c_e) \mathrel{+}= 1$, provided that the beam
length is within the user-defined usable range. Beams that exceed
the usable range have their endpoints clipped to the maximum range and
update only the pass counter, since no static obstacle was confirmed by
the line rasterization algorithm.

After all scans have been integrated, the occupancy
probability of each cell is estimated as
\eqref{eq:slam:dynamics:offline:occ-probability}.
Cells with $n_{\mathrm{pass}}(r,c) = 0$ are marked as \emph{unknown}.
The remaining cells are classified as \emph{free} when
$p_{\mathrm{occ}}(r,c) \leq \gamma_{\mathrm{free}}$, as \emph{occupied}
when $p_{\mathrm{occ}}(r,c) \geq \gamma_{\mathrm{occ}}$, and as
\emph{unknown} otherwise, with
$0 \leq \gamma_{\mathrm{free}} < p_{\mathrm{occ}}(r,c) < \gamma_{\mathrm{occ}} \leq 1$.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:dynamics:offline:occ-probability}
p_{\mathrm{occ}}(r,c) =
\frac{n_{\mathrm{hit}}(r,c)}{n_{\mathrm{pass}}(r,c)},
\quad n_{\mathrm{pass}}(r,c) > 0
\end{equation}

\section{Global Bundle Adjustment}\label{sec:slam:gba}

The online \gls{slam} described in \secref{sec:slam:online} produces
a pose graph whose constraints are $SE(2)$ pose-pose measurements
generated either by the tracker or by the loop aligner. The online formulation
summarizes the alignment between two keyframes that involves multiple point
correspondences into a single relative-pose constraint, discarding the geometric
information contained in the raw correspondences once the geodesic factor has
been inserted into the graph. When the online graph is used to produce
a map representation of the environment
(see~\secref{sec:slam:dynamics:offline}), the loss of per-point information
contributes to inter-keyframe misalignments that may manifest as
global geometric distortions, blurred and double walls in the resulting map.

This PhD~Thesis explores the limitation of the online graph \gls{slam} in terms
of global inter-keyframe misalignments by proposing novel distance map-based
\glslink{gba}{\glsentryfull{gba}} constraints. These distance-based \gls{gba}
factors augment the pose-pose constraints of the online graph by
per-point distance map factors. Each factor draws a point $\mathbf{z}_i$ from
the moving point cloud held by the \emph{from} keyframe's local map, transforms
it into the sensor frame of the \emph{to} keyframe through the current pose
graph estimate, and evaluates its residual against the distance map data
structure attached to the \emph{to} keyframe. This formulation extends
the pairwise distance map alignment introduced in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment} to a joint multi-keyframe optimization,
where all $SE(2)$ pose variables in the graph are estimated simultaneously by
minimizing the aggregate distance map cost. \secref{sec:slam:gba:p2p} derives
the point-to-point factor and its Jacobians via right-sided perturbation, while
\secref{sec:slam:gba:p2pl} derives the point-to-plane variant.

\subsection{Point-to-point factor with distance maps}
\label{sec:slam:gba:p2p}

Let $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}},\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\in SE(2)$ be the
two keyframe variables connected by the factor, considering the sensor \gls{wrt}
the robot transformation $\mathbf{T}_S^R$ required to transform the keyframes'
local maps from the sensor to the variables' coordinate frames
(see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:problem}).
Let $\mathbf{z}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$ be a point of the
moving cloud held by $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}$, expressed in the sensor
frame $S_{\mathrm{from}}$, and
$\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}_0^+$ denote the
distance map associated to $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}$
(see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:dmaps}), computed in the sensor
frame $S_{\mathrm{to}}$.

The point-to-point distance map \gls{gba} residual proposed in this PhD~Thesis
is the scalar value returned by $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}$ when $\mathbf{z}_i$
is projected from the sensor frame $S_{\mathrm{from}}$ into $S_{\mathrm{to}}$,
as formulated in \eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2p:error}. This projection results into
the projected moving point $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}$.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:gba:p2p:error}
e_i^{\mathrm{p2p}}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}},\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\right) =
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right) =
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(
  \mathbf{T}_R^S\cdot\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}^{-1}\cdot
  \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}\cdot\mathbf{T}_S^R\,\mathbf{z}_i
\right) \in \mathbb{R}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Jacobian matrices via right-sided perturbation}
\label{sec:slam:gba:p2p:jacobian}

Applying the right-sided perturbations
$\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}\oplus\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}}$ and
$\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\oplus\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}}$ to
\eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2p:error}, the perturbed error for the proposed
point-to-point \gls{gba} error formulation based on distance maps is given by
\eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2p:perturbed}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:gba:p2p:perturbed}
e_i^{\mathrm{p2p}}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}\oplus\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}},\,
                          \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\oplus\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}}\right) =
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(
  \mathbf{T}_R^S\cdot
  \exp(\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}})^{-1}\cdot
  \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}^{-1}\cdot
  \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}\cdot
  \exp(\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}})\cdot
  \mathbf{T}_S^R\,\mathbf{z}_i
\right)
\end{equation}

The Jacobians $\mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{from}}^{\mathrm{p2p}},
\mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{to}}^{\mathrm{p2p}}\in\mathbb{R}^{1\times3}$ are
obtained by differentiating \eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2p:perturbed} \gls{wrt}
$\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}}$ and $\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}}$ at
$\mathbf{\Delta x}=\mathbf{0}$, applying the chain rule with the spatial
gradient $\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}$ of the distance map.
\eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2p:jacobian-to} and
\eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2p:jacobian-from} present the resulting Jacobians,
where $\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{from}}$ and $\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{to}}$
denote the rotation matrices of $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}$ and
$\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}$, respectively, and
$\lfloor\cdot\rfloor$ is the 2D~skew operator introduced in
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:problem:skew}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:gba:p2p:jacobian-to}
\mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{to}}^{\mathrm{p2p}} =
\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right)^T
\cdot
\underbrace{
  \mathbf{R}_R^S \cdot
  \left(-\mathbf{I}_{2\times2}\;\big|\;
    \lfloor -\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}^{-1}\cdot
          \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}\cdot
          \mathbf{T}_S^R\,\mathbf{z}_i
    \rfloor
  \right)}_{\mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{to}}^{\mathrm{p2p,ICP}}}
\in\mathbb{R}^{1\times3}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:gba:p2p:jacobian-from}
\mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{from}}^{\mathrm{p2p}} =
\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right)^T \cdot
\underbrace{
  \mathbf{R}_R^S\,\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{to}}^T\,\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{from}} \cdot
  \left(\mathbf{I}_{2\times2}\;\big|\;
    \lfloor \mathbf{T}_S^R\,\mathbf{z}_i\rfloor
  \right)}_{\mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{from}}^{\mathrm{p2p,ICP}}}
\in\mathbb{R}^{1\times3}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Point-to-plane factor with distance maps and moving normals}
\label{sec:slam:gba:p2pl}

The point-to-plane variant of the offline \gls{gba} factors proposed in this
PhD~Thesis follows the same principles of
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl}, adapted to the multi-keyframe
formulation. The surface normal $\mathbf{n}_{\mathbf{z}_i}\in\mathbb{R}^2$ is
estimated on the moving cloud of the variable $\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}$ by
the \gls{pca}-based procedure of
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:normals}, remaining constant
throughout the iterative optimization. Since all rotations in $SO(2)$ are planar
and therefore commutative (see~\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl}),
the sensor \gls{wrt} the robot transformation cancels itself in the rotation
chain when projecting the moving normal $\mathbf{n}_{\mathbf{z}_i}$ in
the sensor frame $S_{\mathrm{from}}$ into $S_{\mathrm{to}}$, as formulated by
\eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:normal-rotation}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:normal-rotation}
\tilde{\mathbf{n}}_{\mathbf{z}_i}^{S_{\mathrm{to}}} =
\mathbf{R}_R^S\,\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{to}}^T\,\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{from}}\,
\mathbf{R}_S^R\,\mathbf{n}_{\mathbf{z}_i} =
\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{to}}^T\,\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{from}}\,\mathbf{n}_{\mathbf{z}_i}
\end{equation}

Let
$\hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right)
= \bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right)
/ \|\bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right)\|_2$
denote the normalized spatial gradient of the distance map at the projected
point. The product
$\hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\cdot
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}$ recovers the displacement vector from the
projected moving point to its nearest reference point in the
sensor frame $S_{\mathrm{to}}$, similar to
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl}. The point-to-plane
residual projects this displacement onto the rotated surface normal, as
formulated in \eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:error}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:error}
e_i^{\mathrm{p2pl}}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}},\,
                           \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\right) =
\left(\tilde{\mathbf{n}}_{\mathbf{z}_i}^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right)^T\cdot
\hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right)\cdot
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right)
\in \mathbb{R}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Jacobian matrices via right-sided perturbation}
\label{sec:slam:gba:p2pl:jacobian}

Applying the right-sided perturbations
$\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}\oplus\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}}$ and
$\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\oplus\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}}$ to
\eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:error}, the perturbed error for the proposed
point-to-plane \gls{gba} error formulation based on distance maps is given by
\eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:perturbed}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:perturbed}
e_i^{\mathrm{p2pl}}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{from}}\oplus\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}},\,
                           \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}}\oplus\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}}\right) =
\mathbf{n}_{\mathbf{z}_i}^T \cdot
\mathbf{R}_{\mathbf{\Delta x},\mathrm{from}}^T \,
\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{from}}^T \cdot
\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{to}} \,
\mathbf{R}_{\mathbf{\Delta x},\mathrm{to}} \cdot
\hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\cdot\right)\cdot
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\cdot\right)
\end{equation}

The differentiation of \eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:perturbed} \gls{wrt}
$\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{to}}$ and $\mathbf{\Delta x}_{\mathrm{from}}$ at
$\mathbf{\Delta x}=\mathbf{0}$ result into the jacobians
$\mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{to}}^{\mathrm{p2pl}},
\mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{from}}^{\mathrm{p2pl}} \in \mathbb{R}^{1\times 3}$
formulated in \eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:jacobian:to} and
\eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:jacobian:from}, respectively.
Similarly to the point-to-plane Jacobian of
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:jacobian} for 2D~point cloud
registration, each Jacobian of the point-to-plane distance map-based \gls{gba}
factor decomposes into three additive contributions by the product rule.
The first contribution accounts for the rotation of the normal under
the perturbation of the corresponding variable, the second for change in
gradient direction as the projected point moves under that perturbation, and
the third contribution is for the change in the distance value at
the new projected point. In the limit
$\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{to}} = \mathbf{I}$,
\eqref{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:jacobian:from} reduces to
\eqref{eq:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:jacobian} of
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl:jacobian}, consistent with the
point-to-plane alignment formulation proposed in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:alignment:pt2pl}.
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:jacobian:to}
\mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{to}}^{\mathrm{p2pl}} & =
\underbrace{
  \tilde{\mathbf{n}}_{\mathbf{z}_i}^{S_{\mathrm{to}},T}
  \left(\mathbf{0}_{2\times 2}\,\Big|\,
    \left\lfloor  \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right) \cdot
                  \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right)
    \right\rfloor
  \right)
}_{\text{rotation of the normal}}
\\
&\quad+
\underbrace{
  \tilde{\mathbf{n}}_{\mathbf{z}_i}^{S_{\mathrm{to}},T} \cdot
  \mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right) \cdot
  \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right) \cdot
  \mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{to}}^{\mathrm{p2p,ICP}}
}_{\text{change in gradient direction}}
\\
&\quad+
\underbrace{
  \tilde{\mathbf{n}}_{\mathbf{z}_i}^{S_{\mathrm{to}},T} \cdot
  \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right) \cdot
  \bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right)^T \cdot
  \mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{to}}^{\mathrm{p2p,ICP}}
}_{\text{change in distance value}}
\in\mathbb{R}^{1\times3}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:slam:gba:p2pl:jacobian:from}
\mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{from}}^{\mathrm{p2pl}} & =
\underbrace{
  \mathbf{n}_{\mathbf{z}_i}^{T}
  \left(\mathbf{0}_{2\times 2}\,\Big|\,
    \left\lfloor -\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{from}}^T \mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{to}} \cdot
                  \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right) \cdot
                  \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right)
    \right\rfloor
  \right)
}_{\text{rotation of the normal}}
\\
&\quad+
\underbrace{
  \tilde{\mathbf{n}}_{\mathbf{z}_i}^{S_{\mathrm{to}},T} \cdot
  \mathbf{H}_{\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right) \cdot
  \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right) \cdot
  \mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{from}}^{\mathrm{p2p,ICP}}
}_{\text{change in gradient direction}}
\\
&\quad+
\underbrace{
  \tilde{\mathbf{n}}_{\mathbf{z}_i}^{S_{\mathrm{to}},T} \cdot
  \hat{\bm{\nabla}}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right) \cdot
  \bm{\nabla}\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{to}}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^{S_{\mathrm{to}}}\right)^T \cdot
  \mathbf{J}_{i,\mathrm{from}}^{\mathrm{p2p,ICP}}
}_{\text{change in distance value}}
\in\mathbb{R}^{1\times3}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\section{Experimental Results and Discussion}\label{sec:slam:results}

This section presents four experiments with the RicoSLAM framework that evaluate
the contributions presented in this chapter.
First, \secref{sec:slam:results:iilabs3d} presents a trajectory accuracy
benchmark on the IILABS~3D dataset~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025}, comparing
RicoSLAM against established 2D graph-based and particle-filter-based \gls{slam}
baselines, evaluating both the online and offline corrected pose streams from
RicoSLAM.
Second, \secref{sec:slam:results:relocalization} analyses the scalability of
RicoSLAM and the \gls{slam} baselines from the first experiment on the same
dataset, quantifying the impact of the relocalization policy on
the back-end graph growth.
Third, \secref{sec:slam:results:dynamics} assesses the effect of
the offline dynamics removal mechanism on the resulting 2D occupancy grid
representation, considering not only the IILABS~3D
dataset~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} but also additional sequences from \gls{amr}
deployments in industrial scenarios with dynamic, noisy observations.
Lastly, \secref{sec:slam:results:gba} evaluates the accuracy of the distance
map-based \gls{gba} formulations introduced in \secref{sec:slam:gba}
in a synthetic benchmark with ground-truth poses.

\subsection{Trajectory accuracy on the IILABS~3D dataset}
\label{sec:slam:results:iilabs3d}

This experiment evaluates the trajectory accuracy of RicoSLAM against
established 2D \gls{slam} baselines
(GMapping~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007} and
SLAM Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021}) on the IILABS~3D
dataset~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025}, extending the laser odometry evaluation of
\secref{sec:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d} from the front-end tracker alone to
the complete online and offline pipelines for pose estimation of RicoSLAM.

\subsubsection{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:slam:results:iilabs3d:setup}

The evaluation reuses the four planar sequences from the
IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} dataset
(Nav~A~Diff, Nav~A~Omni, Loop, and Slippage) described in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:setup} and
the same TUM~\cite{sturm:iros:2012} trajectory format for
the ground-truth poses provided by the OptiTrack motion capture system.
The three methods considered in this experiment are introduced as follows.

\paragraph{RicoSLAM}
All five variants of the RicoSLAM framework introduced in
this chapter share the same front-end and back-end configuration.
The tracker uses the precomputed $\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{p2p}(\mathcal{M}_1)$
point-to-point formulation based on distance maps introduced in
\chapref{chap:distance-maps}, with the parameter values identified as best
performing in \secref{sec:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d}:
a distance map resolution of \SI{0.03}{\metre}, voxelization enabled with the
voxel size the same as the distance map resolution, bi-linear interpolation
disabled, distance map truncation distance set at \SI{0.50}{\metre},
and a \SI{30}{\metre} maximum laser range.
Outlier observations are down-weighted via a Cauchy robustifier
(see \tabref{tab:distance-maps:background:least-squares:robust-losses})
operating in two sequential stages: a coarse one of 5~iterations with
$c^2=0.250$ (\SI{0.50}{\metre} threshold); followed by a fine stage up to
15~iterations with $c^2 = 0.005$ (\SI{0.06}{\metre} threshold), similar to
the same configuration used in \secref{sec:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d}.
The front-end Gauss-Newton solver terminates when the step norm falls below
\num{1e-3} or when reaching the maximum of 20~iterations.
Independently of this termination criterion, the tracker flags the latest scan
alignment as failed when its inlier ratio falls below \SI{20}{\percent},
triggering a keyframe change.
We disable wheel odometry initialization in all variants of the RicoSLAM
tracker. This choice intends to demonstrate that the proposed system does not
depend on wheel odometry, unlike the other two baselines, which do require it.

As for the back-end configuration, the loop aligner in this evaluation re-uses
the tracker's registration formulation, with three differences:
a tighter fine-stage robustifier with $c^2 = 0.001$
(\SI{0.03}{\metre} threshold) to refine the loop candidate alignment,
a higher failure threshold of \SI{30}{\percent} on the inlier ratio,
and configuring an additional loop closure acceptance policy that requires
the mean Mahalanobis distance of the inlier residuals to be below
$\chi^2_{\mathrm{inliers}} \le 0.001$.
Loop candidates are only considered between keyframes separated by at least
a 5~graph-distance and at most \SI{3.00}{\metre} in Euclidean distance.
The back-end solver is the
\texttt{srrg2\_solver}~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020} framework, configured
with a maximum of $25$ iterations, an initial damping factor of $1.0$, and
a $10^{-3}$ step-norm termination criterion.

We evaluate five configurations of RicoSLAM, differing only in the keyframe
split/merge criteria of the tracker's local mapping stage and in whether
relocalization or factor information-matrix updates are enabled:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{scan:} a \SI{50}{\percent} inlier-ratio threshold as splitting
      criterion and the merging criterion disabled, as every registered scan in
      the back-end graph becomes its own keyframe;
\item \textbf{scan w/ reloc.:} same as the \emph{scan} variant, with
      the relocalization policy enabled. Given that the relocalization re-uses
      the loop aligner to decide whether to switch the current keyframe,
      we raise the inlier-ratio failure threshold from \SI{30}{\percent} to
      \SI{70}{\percent} to avoid swapping keyframes without sufficient
      observation overlap;
\item \textbf{local:} local maps mode with relocalization disabled.
      The splitting criterion activated by a \SI{2.50}{\metre} translation
      threshold, which enforces approximately uniform spatial spacing between
      consecutive keyframes along the trajectory and bounds the tracker noise
      that propagates into each keyframe's local map.
      The merging criterion is an inlier-ratio threshold of \SI{80}{\percent},
      and the merger uses a \SI{0.20}{\metre} distance for its
      $\tau_{\mathrm{new}}$ (add new point) and $\tau_{\mathrm{through}}$
      (see-through) threshold for the ray tracing model;
\item \textbf{local w/upd.:} same as the \emph{local} variant, with
      the automatic update enabled of the information matrices of
      the incident factors at keyframe-change time.
      A keyframe's local map accumulates information between its creation and
      the spawning of the next keyframe. As a result, with the information
      matrices of its outgoing factors should be re-evaluated at swap time
      rather than remaining fixed since the keyframes' creation;
\item \textbf{local w/reloc.:} same as \emph{local}, with the relocalization
      policy enabled and the minimum graph-distance for loop candidates reduced
      from $5$ to $3$ edges, since the local maps mode with relocalization
      produces fewer keyframes than scan and standard local map modes.
\end{itemize}

For RicoSLAM, we report results for two pose history streams:
the online estimation
$\mathbf{x}_{R,W}^{\mathrm{online}}(t)$
(see~\eqref{eq:slam:online:pose-history:online}), denoted simply as
\emph{RicoSLAM} in the results, and the offline corrected stream
$\mathbf{x}_{R,W}^{\mathrm{offline}}(t)$
(see~\eqref{eq:slam:online:pose-history:offline}) that corrects all robot
poses \emph{a posteriori} from the optimized keyframe estimates, denoted as
\emph{RicoSLAM$^{\dagger}$}. This distinction exposes the contribution of
the back-end optimization and the loop closures to the final trajectory accuracy
in the offline pose stream, in contrast to the online operation of RicoSLAM.
Also, GMapping~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007} and
SLAM~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021} only expose
the online estimation for the robot pose.

\paragraph{SLAM~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021}}
A state-of-the-art open-source 2D~graph \gls{slam} system, using the
Ceres~\cite{ceres:sw:2023} framework for back-end pose-graph optimization.
Each incoming laser scan is matched against a rasterized grid built from
a sliding window of recent scans, and only the scans accepted by
the splitting criterion are inserted into the pose graph as new keyframes.
Two splitting viewpoint configurations are considered with minimum
translation and rotation displacement values for new keyframes of
\SI{0.3}{\metre}/\SI{0.3}{\radian} and
\SI{0.5}{\metre}/\SI{0.5}{\radian}.
Larger splitting thresholds were also tested
(\SI{1.0}{\metre}/\SI{1.0}{\radian},
\SI{1.5}{\metre}/\SI{1.5}{\radian}, and \SI{2.5}{\metre} translation
with the minimum angular displacement set to $\SI{10.0}{\radian}$ to
disable rotation-driven splits). However,
SLAM~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021} consistently failed to track
the full trajectories of the IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} sequences
with those larger thresholds.
The remaining parameters are similar to the default ones for asynchronous
mapping (process all laser scan messages without throttling) with
SLAM~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021}, but matching the map
resolution with the distance map resolution of RicoSLAM
($\delta = \SI{0.03}{\metre}$) and a \SI{30}{\metre} maximum laser range.
Also, we had to decrease the minimum chain size to look for a loop closure from
10 to 5 and increase the space dimension of the search grid size from 8 to 10
for the SLAM~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021} algorithm to close the
loop in the Loop sequence from the IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} dataset.

Unlike RicoSLAM, which reports a pose for every laser scan message processed by
its front-end, SLAM~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021} only
corrects the robot pose when a scan is registered into its back-end
pose graph. Between registration events, the pose published by
SLAM~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021} follows the wheel odometry
estimation, with the latest graph correction held fixed on
the \texttt{map}~$\rightarrow$~\texttt{odom} transform
of the \gls{ros}~\cite{ros} transform tree.
In order to produce an evaluation trajectory at the same rate as RicoSLAM,
we synchronize the pose estimation from the
SLAM~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021} transform tree with the laser
data by querying the tree with the timestamp of every laser scan message.
The resulting sampled poses are exported as a TUM trajectory
file~\cite{sturm:iros:2012}.

\paragraph{GMapping~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007}}
A particle filter-based 2D~\gls{slam} system, included in this evaluation as
a non-graph baseline. We evaluate three configurations, differing only in
the minimum translation and rotation displacement that trigger
an observation-model update of the particle filter:
\SI{0.3}{\metre}/\SI{0.3}{\radian},
\SI{0.5}{\metre}/\SI{0.5}{\radian}, and
\SI{1.0}{\metre}/\SI{1.0}{\radian}.
Between these updates, the particles are propagated solely by the
wheel-odometry motion model. So, similar to
SLAM~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021},
GMapping~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007} only incorporates new laser information for
its pose estimate at these update events.
In order to produce an evaluation of the trajectory accuracy at the laser scan
rate, comparable with the one reported by RicoSLAM, we modified the official
\gls{ros}~\cite{ros} wrapper with two changes:
(i)~support for offline ROSbag processing without dropping messages, mirroring
the offline mode we implemented in RicoSLAM; and (ii)~logging the pose of the
best particle (selected via \texttt{getBestParticleIndex()}, as in the online
API of the original implementation) at every laser scan message read from
the ROSbag into a TUM trajectory file~\cite{sturm:iros:2012}. The modified
wrapper is available in a public
repository\footnote{\url{https://github.com/sousarbarb/slam_gmapping}}.

The remaining parameters follow the default ones from the official
\gls{ros}~\cite{ros} wrapper of GMapping~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007}.
Still, similar to SLAM~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021},
we set a \SI{0.03}{\metre} resolution for the 2D~occupancy grid map of
GMapping~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007} and a \SI{30}{\metre} maximum laser range
for fair comparison. We tried to increase from 30~to 250~particles,
inflate the odometry-noise covariances to widen the particle distribution, and
enlarge the laser-likelihood sigma to reduce the confidence in
individual laser observations. However, GMapping~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007}
consistently failed to close the loop on the \emph{Loop} sequence from the
IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} dataset.
As a result, we reverted the parameters to the defaults ones and set
the particles count to $N = 100$, only changing the minimum linear and angular
update thresholds across the three configurations evaluated in this work.

\subsubsection{Evaluation metrics}
\label{sec:slam:results:iilabs3d:metrics}

The trajectory accuracy evaluation on the IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025}
dataset considers the same toolchain as in
\secref{sec:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:metrics}:
the open-source evo framework~\cite{grupp:evo} with a maximum time
synchronization difference of \SI{0.01}{\second} between matched poses and
the Umeyama method~\cite{umeyama:tpami:1991} for trajectory alignment,
computing the accuracy metrics from the estimated and ground-truth
TUM trajectory files~\cite{sturm:iros:2012}.
Furthermore, we extend the metrics in this experiment from the laser odometry
drift evaluation to incorporate the absolute trajectory error after the
alignment step. Thus, this experiment considers the following metrics:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{\glslink{ate}{\glsentryfull{ate}}}:
      \gls{rmse} of the translational component of the absolute
      pose error after the Umeyama~\cite{umeyama:tpami:1991}-based alignment
      of the estimated trajectory to the ground-truth one.
      The \gls{ate} reflects the global consistency of the trajectory estimated
      by the \gls{slam} baselines and is sensitive to the loop closure
      performance of the systems, given that loop closures correct accumulated
      drift along the trajectory.
      Let $\mathbf{x}_t$ and $\mathbf{x}_t^*$ denote the estimated and
      ground-truth poses at time $t$, respectively, the \gls{ate} is
      formulated in \eqref{eq:slam:results:iilabs3d:ate}~\cite{geiger:cvpr:2012};

\item \textbf{\glslink{rte}{\glsentryfull{rte}} and
      \glslink{rre}{\glsentryfull{rre}}}:
      relative translation and rotation errors over fixed trajectory segments of
      $\Delta d = \SI{10}{\metre}$, consistent with the IILABS~3D benchmark
      study~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025} and the laser odometry evaluation

three metrics measured at the end of each sequence run reported in
\secref{sec:slam:results:iilabs3d}:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{\#n:} number of keyframe variables (nodes) in the back-end graph
      at the end of the sequence;
\item \textbf{\#s:} the total number of laser scan messages incorporated
      into each system's internal state estimate.

      in scan mode (RicoSLAM in its \emph{scan} and \emph{scan w/reloc.}
      variants, and SLAM~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021}),
      each accepted scan is promoted to a keyframe,

      contributed to any keyframe's local map.
      As for the particle filter-based GMapping~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007}
      evaluation, $\#s$ counts the scans that
      triggered an observation model update of the particle filter,
      following the configured translation and rotation thresholds.
\item \textbf{\#e:} number of pose-pose factors (edges) in the back-end pose
      graph at the end of the sequence, including odometric edges from the
      tracker and loop closure edges from loop detection.
\end{itemize}

therefore reported for RicoSLAM and

this experiment are reported once per RicoSLAM variant, rather than

\subsection{Mapping with dynamics removal}\label{sec:slam:results:dynamics}

This experiment evaluates the offline dynamics removal mechanism proposed in
\secref{sec:slam:dynamics:offline}, measuring the impact of the ray-based
filtering stage before rasterization on the resulting 2D~occupancy grid
representation generated by RicoSLAM.

\subsubsection{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:slam:results:dynamics:setup}

The evaluation in this experiment considers five different 2D~laser scan
datasets, covering laboratory conditions, an academic indoor environment, and
\gls{amr} deployments in industrial scenarios. Together, these datasets span
sensor sampling rates from \SI{6}{\hertz} to \SI{40}{\hertz},
maximum sensor ranges from \SI{30}{\metre} to \SI{200}{\metre},
\glspl{fov} from \SI{270}{\degree} to the full \SI{360}{\degree}, total sequence
durations from $\sim$\SI{1.5}{\minute} up to $\sim$\SI{78}{\minute}, and
path lengths from tens of meters up to kilometers.
This heterogeneity is intentional, exposing the proposed offline dynamics
removal mechanism to a representative range of real-world sensing conditions and
dynamic observations.

As a result, \tabref{tab:slam:results:dyn-rmv:datasets} presents statistics
(duration, total path length) across the datasets introduced in this experiment,
namely, \emph{Avantgarde}, \emph{Cappero}, \emph{Flowbotic}, and \emph{Kuka}.
The sequence-level statistics of the IILABS~3D~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025}
dataset are documented in \secref{sec:distance-maps:results:iilabs3d:setup}.
The description and configurations used for the five datasets considered in
this experiment are the following ones:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{IILABS~3D}~\cite{ribeiro:access:2025}: the same four planar

For each dataset sequence, RicoSLAM is executed with the configuration
described in the corresponding dataset entry above. Next, the back-end factor
graph is serialized to a human-readable JSON file at the end of the run through
the serialization framework made available by the
srrg2\_solver~\cite{grisetti:robotics:2020}. This serialized graph includes
the final pose estimates of the keyframe variables, the $SE(2)$ pose-pose
geodesic factors and their alignment measurements, the 2D~point clouds
associated with each keyframe variable, and the laser scan messages
aggregated within each keyframe's local map.
Then, the offline 2D~occupancy grid mapper described in
\secref{sec:slam:dynamics:offline} is applied to the final pose graph
and the retained scan messages, producing two grid maps for each sequence and
operating mode:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{standard}: the rasterization stage is invoked on
      the retained scans and their corresponding world-frame poses through
      the Bresenham~\cite{bresenham:ibm:1965,zingl:uas:2016} line
      algorithm, accumulating hit and pass counts per cell.
      This grid map serves as the baseline for this experiment;
\item \textbf{dyn-rmv}: the ray tracing-based offline merger discussed in
      \secref{sec:slam:dynamics:offline} is first applied to the
      retained scans, removing the dynamic observations from the point cloud
      representation for the global map. The cleaned set of scan-to-global map
      point correspondences is then processed by the same rasterization stage
      with the Bresenham~\cite{bresenham:ibm:1965,zingl:uas:2016} line algorithm
      to accumulate the hit and pass counts per cell.
\end{itemize}

Both \emph{standard} and \emph{dyn-rmv} grid maps for each sequence and
operating mode are generated with a cell resolution of \SI{0.03}{\metre},
a maximum laser range matching the value used by the corresponding RicoSLAM
configuration, and a threshold of $\gamma_{\mathrm{occ}} = 0.3$ on the cell
occupancy probability $p_{\mathrm{occ}}(r,c)$ defined in
\eqref{eq:slam:dynamics:offline:occ-probability}.
In the case of the \emph{dyn-rmv} grid map, the add-new and see-through
thresholds of the offline ray-based merger
(see~\eqref{eq:slam:dynamics:online:rules}) are set to
$\tau_{\mathrm{new}} = \tau_{\mathrm{through}} = \SI{0.10}{\metre}$.

\subsubsection{Evaluation metrics}
\label{sec:slam:results:dynamics:metrics}

Since this experiment focuses on the removal of dynamic observations from
the environment map representation, the evaluation assesses the empirical
probability distribution of the cell occupancy values $p_{\mathrm{occ}}(r,c)$
defined in \eqref{eq:slam:dynamics:offline:occ-probability} over the observed
cells of each generated grid map (\emph{standard}, \emph{dyn-rmv}).
Cells marked as unknown (equivalent to those for which
$n_{\mathrm{pass}}(r,c) = 0$) are excluded from this analysis.
The principle behind this evaluation is that a sharper occupancy distribution,
where most observed cells are concentrated near the cell states
$p_{\mathrm{occ}} = 0$ (free) or $p_{\mathrm{occ}} = 1$ (occupied), corresponds
to a less ambiguous environment representation. Conversely, cells with
$p_{\mathrm{occ}} \approx 0.5$ reflect inconsistent hit-pass evidence,
which may be due to dynamic returns in the integrated scans or to
registration noise. The dynamics removal mechanism is expected to
reduce the latter category by filtering out dynamic returns before
integrating the scan observations into the occupancy grid map.

In order to quantify this effect, the evaluation of this experiment adopts the
peak-versus-middle mass partition of the empirical occupancy distribution.
For a threshold $\tau \in (0, 0.5)$, the peak mass $m_{\mathrm{peak}}(\tau)$
counts the fraction of observed cells with strong evidence of being either
free or occupied, while the middle mass $m_{\mathrm{mid}}(\tau)$ counts
the fraction of cells with weak evidence, as formulated in
\eqref{eq:slam:results:dynamics:metrics:peak-vs-middle}.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:results:dynamics:metrics:peak-vs-middle}
m_{\mathrm{peak}}(\tau) =
\Pr\!\left[p_{\mathrm{occ}}(r,c) \in
           \left[0,\tau\right] \cup \left[1-\tau, 1\right]\right],
\;
m_{\mathrm{mid}}(\tau) =
\Pr\!\left[p_{\mathrm{occ}}(r,c) \in
           \left(\tau, 1-\tau\right)\right]
\end{equation}

By construction, $m_{\mathrm{peak}}(\tau) + m_{\mathrm{mid}}(\tau) = 1$
for any value of $\tau$. The experiment reports the
$(m_{\mathrm{peak}}, m_{\mathrm{mid}})$ pair for two threshold values
$\tau \in \{0.05, 0.10\}$, in order to verify that the effect of the
dynamics removal mechanism is consistent across reasonable values of
$\tau$ rather than being a consequence of a specific threshold value.
In addition, the number of valid (observed) cells $N_{\mathrm{obs}}$
and its delta $\Delta N_{\mathrm{obs}}$ between the \emph{standard} and
\emph{dyn-rmv} results quantify how many cells are reclassified as
unknown by the ray-based filtering. This metric exposes a
complementary contribution of the proposed mechanism: cells whose only
supporting beam endpoints were dynamic returns are removed from the
occupancy grid representation entirely.

\subsection{Global Bundle Adjustment~(GBA) with synthetic data}\label{sec:slam:results:gba}

This experiment evaluates the distance map-based \gls{gba} factors proposed in
\secref{sec:slam:gba} on a synthetic benchmark with known ground-truth poses,
isolating the contribution of the \gls{gba} factor formulations from
the online operation of RicoSLAM.
The integration of the proposed \gls{gba} stage with the online RicoSLAM
pipeline for end-to-end evaluation on real-world sequences and global map
refinement remains a work in progress at the time of writing this PhD~Thesis,
and is deferred to future work. Nevertheless, this synthetic benchmark intends
to validate the convergence behavior and accuracy of the proposed \gls{gba}
factors with ground-truth data.

\subsubsection{Evaluation metrics}
\label{sec:slam:results:gba:metrics}

For each non-fixed pose $i$ in the graph, the post-optimization pose error
is defined by \eqref{eq:slam:results:gba:metrics:residual},
where $\mathbf{x}_i$ is the pose estimate optimized by the \gls{gba} factors
proposed in \secref{sec:slam:gba} and $\mathbf{x}_i^*$ the ground-truth pose
introduced in \secref{sec:slam:results:gba:setup}.
The translational and rotational components of $\mathbf{e}_i$ are
extracted as in \secref{sec:slam:online:factor} and aggregated over
all $N$ non-fixed poses using the \gls{rmse} formulated in
\eqref{eq:slam:results:gba:metrics:trans} for the translational error
$\varepsilon_{\mathbf{t}}$ and in
\eqref{eq:slam:results:gba:metrics:rot} for the rotational error
$\varepsilon_{\theta}$.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:results:gba:metrics:residual}
\mathbf{e}_i = {\mathbf{x}_i^{*}}^{-1}\cdot\mathbf{x}_i =
\left[ {\mathbf{R}_i^{*}}^T | -{\mathbf{R}_i^{*}}^T  \, \mathbf{t}_i^{*} \right]
\cdot \left[ \mathbf{R}_i | \mathbf{t}_i \right] =
\left[ {\mathbf{R}_i^{*}}^T \mathbf{R}_i |
       {\mathbf{R}_i^{*}}^T \cdot \left( \mathbf{t}_i - \mathbf{t}_i^{*} \right) \right] \in SE(2),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:results:gba:metrics:trans}
\varepsilon_{\mathbf{t}} =
\sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\|\mathbf{t}_{\mathbf{e}_i}\|_2^2}
\quad(\si{\metre})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slam:results:gba:metrics:rot}
\varepsilon_{\theta} =
\sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\theta_{\mathbf{e}_i}^2}
\quad(\si{\degree})
\end{equation}

The residual definition in \eqref{eq:slam:results:gba:metrics:residual} differs
from the one adopted in the synthetic registration benchmark of
\secref{sec:distance-maps:results:synthetic}, where the error was defined as
$\mathbf{e}_i = \mathbf{x}_i\cdot\mathbf{x}_i^{*,-1}$. The latter definition was
required to express the residual in the target frame of the aligner due to its
diagnostic value of the estimated information matrix $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$ in
degenerate scenes. In contrast, this experiment focuses exclusively on the pose
estimation accuracy of the proposed \gls{gba} factors and does not report an
information matrix calibration analysis.
Thus, the residual defined by \eqref{eq:slam:results:gba:metrics:residual}
is the natural choice for pose error evaluation on the $SE(2)$ manifold,
given the independence of the translation and orientation errors contribution.
A unified residual formulation that supports the simultaneous evaluation of
pose accuracy and information matrix calibration is left for future work,
possibly considering a full Lie-group algebraic formulation of both quantities
on a common tangent space of $SE(2)$.
