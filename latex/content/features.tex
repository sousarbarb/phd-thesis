\chapter{Line Fitting-Based Corner-Like Detector for 2D Laser Scanners Data}\label{chap:features}

This chapter presents a novel line-fitting-based corner-like detector for
2D~laser-scanning data~\cite{sousa:icara:2024},
developed within the scope of this PhD~Thesis (see \appxref{appx:features}).
Even though the most common way to represent 2D~environments is via
occupancy grid maps, an alternative is to represent the scene
as a set of features. Accordingly, this chapter explores
the extraction of features that may resemble geometric corners
in the environment. Our methodology explores local line fitting
at each scan point and the intersection of the fitted lines
before and after the point, with respect to the angular scanning direction.
Experimental results reveal viewpoint and geometric invariance of
the corner-like features extracted using the proposed methodology,
demonstrating the potential of this work for
localization and mapping problems on mobile robots.

Thus, this chapter is divided into four sections.
First, \secref{sec:features:background} introduces feature extraction algorithms
for 2D~laser scanner data from the literature.
\secref{sec:features:method} outlines our methodology for extracting
corner-like features from 2D~laser data using local line fitting
and multiple neighborhood-scale analysis.
Then, \secref{sec:features:results} presents experimental results to investigate
the observability ratio of the proposed corner-like feature detector,
the viewpoint- and geometry-invariance properties of the extracted features,
and the extraction of line segments in parallel with
the proposed corner-like feature detector.
Lastly, \secref{sec:features:conclusions} presents a summary of this chapter,
the main conclusions, and future work.

\section{Background}\label{sec:features:background}

Laser scanners allow building a dense representation of the environment as
a 2D~occupancy grid map, where each cell represents occupied, free,
or unknown space~\cite{grisetti:tro:2007}.
Although grid maps can represent arbitrary objects, they are computationally
intensive and require substantial memory~\cite{wurm:ras:2010}.
An alternative method is to represent the environment as a set of features.
In the case of 2D~lasers, the features may include
beacons\cite{sobreira:icarsc:2015}, geometric cues
(line~\cite{castellanos:mfi:1996,roumeliotis-bekey:iros:2000,mohamed:isprs-arxiv:2016,liu:sensors:2023,nunez:icra:2006,vazquez-martin:sensors:2009,feng:aici:2009,pfister:icra:2003,gao:ijars:2018}
and curve~\cite{falomir:prl:2013,nunez:icra:2006,vazquez-martin:sensors:2009,feng:aici:2009} segments,
corners~\cite{castellanos:mfi:1996,roumeliotis-bekey:iros:2000,jung:tits:2008,falomir:prl:2013,mohamed:isprs-arxiv:2016,lin:yac:2017,liu:electronics:2022,liu:sensors:2023,nunez:icra:2006,vazquez-martin:sensors:2009,feng:aici:2009,certad:andescon:2012},
and free endpoints of segments~\cite{castellanos:mfi:1996,liu:electronics:2022,vazquez-martin:sensors:2009,certad:andescon:2012}),
and semantic features such as
parked vehicles~\cite{jung:tits:2008,qu:robio:2018}
and doors~\cite{roumeliotis-bekey:iros:2000}.

The correspondence between geometric features and the environment
(\eg, lines and corners) may be of interest for localization and mapping
problems on mobile robots, as well as for user interaction
with autonomous mobile systems.
The current literature presents several algorithms for extracting
line segments from 2D~laser scan
data~\cite{castellanos:mfi:1996,roumeliotis-bekey:iros:2000,mohamed:isprs-arxiv:2016,liu:sensors:2023,nunez:icra:2006,vazquez-martin:sensors:2009,feng:aici:2009,pfister:icra:2003,gao:ijars:2018},
including an open-source implementation based on the \gls{ros}~\cite{ros}
framework\footnote{\label{note:features:line-feature-ros}\url{https://github.com/kam3k/laser_line_extraction}}.
As for corner detection, the most common methodology found in the literature
is to extract corners from the intersection of line segments detected in the
laser scan~\cite{castellanos:mfi:1996,roumeliotis-bekey:iros:2000,jung:tits:2008,falomir:prl:2013,fernandez-moral:ijrr:2015,mohamed:isprs-arxiv:2016,zhao:icsess:2016,lin:yac:2017,qu:robio:2018,liu:electronics:2022,liu:sensors:2023}.
Another typical approach in the literature is to estimate curvature from the
scan data~\cite{nunez:icra:2006,vazquez-martin:sensors:2009,feng:aici:2009,certad:andescon:2012}.
Next, both trends for extracting corner-like features are analyzed
based on methods found in the literature.

\subsection{Line fitting intersection}\label{sec:features:background:line-fitting}

A trend in the literature is to extract corners at the intersections of
lines detected in the 2D~laser
scan~\cite{castellanos:mfi:1996,roumeliotis-bekey:iros:2000,jung:tits:2008,falomir:prl:2013,fernandez-moral:ijrr:2015,mohamed:isprs-arxiv:2016,zhao:icsess:2016,lin:yac:2017,qu:robio:2018,liu:electronics:2022,liu:sensors:2023}.
First, the laser data may be filtered to remove invalid values
(zero range values~\cite{jung:tits:2008}) and possible noise
(\eg, considering the minimum distance to the two neighbors of
a certain point to remove isolated data~\cite{jung:tits:2008},
mean average filtering~\cite{liu:electronics:2022}, or bilateral filters
to remove non-linear noise~\cite{liu:sensors:2023}).
Next, a segmentation step extracts sets of continuous points
in the environment for line fitting estimation.
Castellanos~\etal~\cite{castellanos:mfi:1996} segment the scan into
polygonal lines and subsequently divide them into
segments using an iterative method.
Falomir~\etal~\cite{falomir:prl:2013} search for discontinuities
in the data to form point subsets and use a recursive line-fitting
algorithm~\cite{xavier:icra:2005} to obtain line segments.
Fernández-Moral~\etal~\cite{fernandez-moral:ijrr:2015} search for
parameters of a 2D~line that maximize the number of points
supporting the line model using the \gls{ransac}
algorithm~\cite{fischler-bolles:cacm:1981}.
Mohamed~\etal~\cite{mohamed:isprs-arxiv:2016} use the line tracking
algorithm~\cite{munoz:ropec:2013} to iteratively segment lines
in the scan and perform \gls{pca} to compute the lines' parameters.
Then, the intersection points between consecutive line segments
define a corner feature in the environment.

Furthermore, Lin~\etal~\cite{lin:yac:2017} and
Liu~\etal~\cite{liu:electronics:2022,liu:sensors:2023} use adaptive threshold
segmentation to cluster range data into continuous spatial subsets.
Although Lin~\etal~\cite{lin:yac:2017} state that an adaptive segmentation
was used due to the non-uniform distribution of the 2D~laser data,
the authors do not specify the threshold used in their work.
In Liu~\etal~\cite{liu:electronics:2022} work, the threshold is based on
the laser's angular resolution and the distance to
the current point under analysis.
Liu~\etal~\cite{liu:sensors:2023} also establish a threshold depending on
the angular resolution. Instead of considering only the distance of
the point under analysis, the proposed approach~\cite{liu:sensors:2023} selects
the larger distance between adjacent points.
Even though Roumeliotis and Bekey~\cite{roumeliotis-bekey:iros:2000} do not use
a specific adaptive threshold, the proposed SEGMENTS algorithm with two-level
\gls{ekf} exhibits adaptive behavior. A stricter filter,
in terms of the system's noise covariance, attempts to follow a straight line,
while a more flexible \gls{ekf} tries to follow a new segment during each scan.
The four methods~\cite{lin:yac:2017,liu:electronics:2022,liu:sensors:2023,roumeliotis-bekey:iros:2000}
also define a corner feature as the intersection
between consecutive line segments.

A specific application of corner detection with 2D~laser data in the literature
is vehicle detection~\cite{jung:tits:2008,qu:robio:2018}.
Jung~\etal~\cite{jung:tits:2008} cluster laser data using a fixed threshold
between consecutive range values. Then, the method performs template matching
for every point under analysis, assuming the point is
the vertex of the L-shape of a vehicle in the scan.
Jung~\etal~\cite{jung:tits:2008} use \gls{svd} to estimate the L-shape's
line parameters and decompose the problem into line-curve estimation
when the vehicle has a curved corner in the data.
In contrast, Qu~\etal~\cite{qu:robio:2018} segment the data using
\gls{dbscan}~\cite{ester:kdd:1996}.
The method performs L-shape fitting in two steps:
searching for the L-shape vertices and corner-point localization,
the latter based on \gls{ransac}~\cite{fischler-bolles:cacm:1981}.

However, corner detection algorithms that rely on global search during
line segment scanning may be limited to well-structured environments.
Thus, we propose a corner-like detection method based on local line fitting,
without requiring global line detection or recursive data search.
Also, the experiments presented in \secref{sec:features:results} demonstrate
the ability of the proposed detector to extract more points of interest
from the scan in addition to environment corners.

\subsection{Curvature-based detection}\label{sec:features:background:curvature}

Other approaches to detecting corners in 2D~laser data estimate the curvature.
N\'{u}\~{n}ez~\etal~\cite{nunez:icra:2006} propose an adaptive curvature
estimator that compares the $k$-nearest points on
both sides of the point being evaluated.
The $k$ size is adjusted based on the distance between possible corners.
A segmentation step precedes the curvature estimation to detect breakpoints
using the adaptive breakpoint detector proposed by
Borges~and~Aldon~\cite{borges-aldon:jint:2004}.
Then, N\'{u}\~{n}ez~\etal~\cite{nunez:icra:2006} extract corner, line, and
curve segment features from the scan data by analyzing whether
there are peaks in curvature, flat segments with zero average curvature,
and flat segments with non-zero average curvature, respectively.
V\'{a}zquez-Mart\'{i}n~\etal~\cite{vazquez-martin:sensors:2009} use
the adaptive curvature estimator of N\'{u}\~{n}ez~\etal~\cite{nunez:icra:2006}
to segment the scan into clusters of homogeneous curvature.
Even though the existence of a corner is determined from the curvature function,
its keypoint definition is based on the intersection of the two line features
that generate the corner, to improve robustness to noise.
Also, Certad~\etal~\cite{certad:andescon:2012} modify the work of
N\'{u}\~{n}ez~\etal~\cite{nunez:icra:2006} to consider
two curvature estimations based on different noise-related thresholds,
thereby improving the rate of true detection for
corner and edge features in the experiments.

Moreover, Feng~\etal~\cite{feng:aici:2009} and
Zhao~\etal~\cite{zhao:icsess:2016} report a correlation between
the angle difference between the two fitted lines on
both sides of a scan point and the curvature value.
Feng~\etal~\cite{feng:aici:2009} introduce the micro-tangent line function
as the variance between the tangents at two consecutive points in the scan.
Similarly to N\'{u}\~{n}ez~\etal~\cite{nunez:icra:2006},
line and curve segments are extracted from consecutive range readings
with variance close to zero and with constant value larger than a threshold,
respectively, and corner features are extracted from peaks in the variance
function. Instead of only considering two consecutive points,
Zhao~\etal~\cite{zhao:icsess:2016} define two distance-based regions on
both sides of a point to estimate direction vectors and,
consequently, the angle difference between those vectors.
Candidate corner points are selected based on a fixed threshold
for the angle difference. Then, the corner point is estimated
via non-minimal suppression.

Still, most curvature-based methods for corner feature extraction associate
the keypoint with a laser reading. Given the discrete, radial nature
of 2D~laser, the estimated corner may vary farther away.
Also, there is the question of whether the curvature estimators
are invariant to scale, angular resolution, and different viewpoints.
We propose a detector based on the stability of intersection points
from fitted lines at different distance-based scales.
The experiments in \secref{sec:features:results} focus
on evaluating the invariance of the proposed corner-like features.

\section{Corner-Like Feature Detector}\label{sec:features:method}

We propose a feature extraction algorithm on 2D~laser data performing
local line fitting to detect corner-like points in the scan.
In summary, first, we transform the scan data from polar to Cartesian
coordinates. This transformation maintains the data's angular coordinate order.
For each point in the scan, our methodology considers multiple distance-based
scales to extract arrival and departure neighborhoods,
depending on whether the latter are in the negative or positive directions
relative to the point's angular coordinate, respectively.
Next, a linear regression algorithm fits lines to each neighborhood.
The intersection point between the arrival and departure neighborhoods
is a candidate feature keypoint at a given scale.
Then, the following indicators are estimated to determine
if the candidate keypoint is a corner-line feature or not:
minimum number of points to fit a line,
angle between arrival and departure lines,
maximum \gls{rmse} allowed for the line fitting procedure, maximum distance
of the candidate point to the intersection centroid on the multiple scales,
and maximum distance to the laser point on analysis.
The candidate keypoint is validated if it passes
all previous indicators over a minimum number of consecutive scales,
thereby improving robustness to noise in the laser data.

\subsection{Linear least-squares regression}\label{sec:features:method:linear-regression}

A generic definition for the 2D~line model is presented in
\eqref{eq:feature:line-models:generic}, where $\left(x,y\right)$ are
the 2D~point coordinates and $\gamma_0$, $\gamma_1$, and $\gamma_2$
are the model parameters. However, the solution for the generic definition
estimated with a linear least-squares formulation
from a set of 2D~points would be a null vector.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:feature:line-models:generic}
\gamma_0 + \gamma_1 x + \gamma_2 y = 0
\end{equation}

An alternative formulation is to characterize the 2D~line relative to one
of the point coordinates, reducing from three to two parameters in the model.
The 2D~line formulation illustrated in \eqref{eq:feature:line-models:ymxb}
is relative to the $y$ coordinate with respect to $x$, where $\beta_1$ is
the line slope and $\beta_0$ is the line intersection on the y-axis.
The least-squares solution for the parameters $\beta_1$ and $\beta_0$
from a set of points $\left(x_i,y_i\right),i=1,\dots,N$ is formulated
in \eqref{eq:feature:line-models:ymxb:m} and
\eqref{eq:feature:line-models:ymxb:b}, where $\left(\bar{x},\bar{y}\right)$ is
the centroid point of the data~\cite{kenney-keeping:book:1962}.
Still, this model would not be valid when
$\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(x_i - \bar{x}\right)^2=0$,
given the indeterminate form caused in the parameter $\hat{\beta}_1$ (\eg,
all the data points with the same $x$ coordinate and different $y$ values).
\begin{equation}\label{eq:feature:line-models:ymxb}
y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:feature:line-models:ymxb:m}
\hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(x_i - \bar{x}\right)\left(y_i - \bar{y}\right)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(x_i - \bar{x}\right)^2}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:feature:line-models:ymxb:b}
\hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x}
\end{equation}

The 2D~line can be defined relative to $x$ with respect to the $y$ coordinate,
as formulated in \eqref{eq:feature:line-models:xnya}.
The parameters of this model are the line slope
($\alpha_1$) and the line intersection on the x-axis ($\alpha_0$).
Nevertheless, this model has also a undetermined form
when $\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(y_i - \bar{y}\right)^2=0$
(\eg, all the data points with the same $y$ coordinate and different $x$ values).
\begin{equation}\label{eq:feature:line-models:xnya}
x = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 y
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:feature:line-models:xnya:n}
\hat{\alpha}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(x_i - \bar{x}\right)\left(y_i - \bar{y}\right)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(y_i - \bar{y}\right)^2}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:feature:line-models:xnya:a}
\hat{\alpha}_0 = \bar{x} - \hat{\alpha}_1 \bar{y}
\end{equation}

Instead of only considering a single line model for data fitting,
we select the best line model that fits the laser data.
This selection is based on which model has the lower fitting \gls{rmse},
$\varepsilon_{RMSE}$. \eqref{eq:feature:line-models:ymxb:error} and
\eqref{eq:feature:line-models:xnya:error} represent the fitting \gls{rmse} of
the 2D~line models formulated in \eqref{eq:feature:line-models:ymxb} and
\eqref{eq:feature:line-models:xnya}, respectively.
The validation of the proposed selection approach for the line models is shown
in Figure~\ref{fig:feature:model-error-analysis}, where selecting the model
with the lowest \gls{rmse} achieves the best fitting line estimation.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:feature:line-models:ymxb:error}
\varepsilon_{RMSE,\{\hat{\beta}_0,\hat{\beta}_1\}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i \right)^2}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:feature:line-models:xnya:error}
\varepsilon_{RMSE,\{\hat{\alpha}_0,\hat{\alpha}_1\}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( x_i - \hat{\alpha}_0 - \hat{\alpha}_1 y_i \right)^2}
\end{equation}

Other models and fitting algorithms could have been considered to fit a line to
the 2D~laser data, such as formulating a line as the vector to the normal of
the infinite line and the position of the weighted mean of the contributing
points~\cite{pfister:icra:2003}, or using \gls{ransac} to estimate
the line parameters of the generic line model~\cite{fischler-bolles:cacm:1981}.
However, these models and fitting algorithms may require
iterative-based optimization procedures and matrices inversions.
In contrast, our methodology has the advantage of having closed-form equations
for both the line parameters and the fitting error.

\subsection{Feature extraction}\label{sec:features:extraction}

In terms of extracting features from 2D~laser data, first, we need
to transform the scan data from polar to cartesian coordinates.
This transformation preserves the data ordering by its angular coordinate
to speed up the search for the closest consecutive laser points
in the angular direction at a given distance-based neighborhood.

Next, we evaluate each of the laser points $\left(x_i,y_i\right),i=1,\dots,N$
as a possible feature keypoint.
\figref{fig:feature:feature-extraction} presents an example of the clustering
and line fitting results following the proposed methodology on a laser point
$(x_j,y_j)$ at a distance scale $R_k=0.15$~m, where this scale defines
the distance-based neighborhood around the scan point.
These results illustrate the clustering of the arrival
($\left(x_i,y_i\right) \in \{(x_j,y_j)_{\text{arr},k}\}$) and departure
($\left(x_i,y_i\right) \in \{(x_j,y_j)_{\text{dep},k}\}$) neighborhoods,
representing the closest consecutive laser points at the negative and positive
sides of the point $(x_j,y_j)$ in the laser angular direction, respectively.
The intersection point between the fitted lines $line_{\text{arr},k}$ and
$line_{\text{dep},k}$ is considered a candidature feature keypoint
($(x_j,y_j)_{\text{cand},k}$). This intersection point is the result of
the lines intersection depending on which line model
is selected for each neighborhood:
\begin{itemize}
\item $y=\beta_0^{\text{arr},k} + \beta_1^{\text{arr},k} x \enspace$;$\enspace y = \beta_0^{\text{dep},k} + \beta_1^{\text{dep},k} x$
\end{itemize}
\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{smallmatrix}
x_{j,\text{cand},k} = \left(\hat{\beta}_{0}^{\text{dep},k}-\hat{\beta}_{0}^{\text{arr},k}\right) / \left(\hat{\beta}_{1}^{\text{arr},k}-\hat{\beta}_{1}^{\text{dep},k}\right)\\
y_{j,\text{cand},k} = \hat{\beta}_{0}^{\text{arr},k} + \hat{\beta}_{1}^{\text{arr},k} \cdot x_{j,\text{cand},k}
\end{smallmatrix}
\right.
\end{equation}
\begin{itemize}
\item $x=\alpha_0^{\text{arr},k} + \alpha_1^{\text{arr},k} y \enspace$;$\enspace y = \beta_0^{\text{dep},k} + \beta_1^{\text{dep},k} x$
\end{itemize}
\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{smallmatrix}
y_{j,\text{cand},k} = \left(\hat{\alpha}_{0}^{\text{arr},k}\hat{\beta}_{1}^{\text{dep},k}+\hat{\beta}_{0}^{\text{dep},k} \right) / \left(1-\hat{\alpha}_{1}^{\text{arr},k}\hat{\beta}_{1}^{\text{dep},k} \right)\\
x_{j,\text{cand},k} = \hat{\alpha}_{0}^{\text{arr},k} + \hat{\alpha}_{1}^{\text{arr},k} \cdot y_{j,\text{cand},k}
\end{smallmatrix}
\right.
\end{equation}
\begin{itemize}
\item $y=\beta_0^{\text{arr},k} + \beta_1^{\text{arr},k} x \enspace$;$\enspace x=\alpha_0^{\text{dep},k} + \alpha_1^{\text{dep},k} y$
\end{itemize}
\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{smallmatrix}
y_{j,\text{cand},k} = \left(\hat{\alpha}_{0}^{\text{dep},k}\hat{\beta}_{1}^{\text{arr},k}+\hat{\beta}_{0}^{\text{arr},k} \right) / \left(1-\hat{\alpha}_{1}^{\text{dep},k}\hat{\beta}_{1}^{\text{arr},k}\right)\\
x_{j,\text{cand},k} = \hat{\alpha}_{0}^{\text{dep},k} + \hat{\alpha}_{1}^{\text{dep},k} \cdot y_{j,\text{cand},k}
\end{smallmatrix}
\right.
\end{equation}
\begin{itemize}
\item $x=\alpha_0^{\text{arr},k} + \alpha_1^{\text{arr},k} y \enspace$;$\enspace x=\alpha_0^{\text{dep},k} + \alpha_1^{\text{dep},k} y$
\end{itemize}
\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{smallmatrix}
y_{j,\text{cand},k} = \left(\hat{\alpha}_{0}^{\text{dep},k}-\hat{\alpha}_{0}^{\text{arr},k}\right) / \left(\hat{\alpha}_{1}^{\text{arr},k}-\hat{\alpha}_{1}^{\text{dep},k}\right)\\
x_{j,\text{cand},k} = \hat{\alpha}_{0}^{\text{arr},k} + \hat{\alpha}_{1}^{\text{arr},k}\cdot y_{j,\text{cand},k}
\end{smallmatrix}
\right.
\end{equation}

The following indicators determine if a candidate keypoint
is a corner-like feature or not:
minimum of 5 points in each neighborhood,
an absolute angle between arrival and departure fitted lines in the range of
$\left[45\text{º},135\text{º}\right]$,
maximum $\varepsilon_{RMSE}$ in line fitting of 0.025~m,
maximum distance of the lines intersection points on the multiple scales to
the intersection centroid of 0.025~m,
and maximum distance of the intersection point to the scan point
being evaluated of 0.05~m.
The latter indicator avoids having features not representing physical points.
Then, the candidate keypoint is validated if the point passes
all previous indicators in a minimum number of 3 consecutive scales,
to improve robustness to noise on the laser data. The scales considered in the
experiments presented in this chapter are the following ones:
$\left[0.05, 0.075, 0.1, 0.125, 0.15, 0.175, 0.2, 0.225, 0.25\right]$~m.
However, the indicators' thresholds and the scale may be parametrized in
run-time. The feature keypoint definition is
the lines intersection centroid on the multiple scales.

\section{Experimental Results and Discussion}\label{sec:features:results}

The experiments were performed using a three-wheeled omnidirectional robot
developed by the 5dpo robotics team~\cite{5dpo}.
This platform was adapted to have a 2D~laser scanner, namely,
the Hokuyo UST-10LX with 270º~\gls{fov}, 0.25º angular resolution,
and 40~Hz sampling rate.
The sensor is aligned with the geometric center of the robot,
and the wheel odometry parameters were estimated using the
OptiOdom~\cite{sousa:jint:2022} calibration method.
The implementation of the feature extraction algorithm is in C++ and \gls{ros}.
The algorithm ran on a computer with a processor Intel Core i7-6700HQ
with 8GB of RAM, and a Nvidia GPU GeForce GTX~960M.

In terms of the experimental methodology, first, a map of the scene
was retrieved using the \gls{slam}~Toolbox~\cite{macenski-jambrecic:joss:2021}
algorithm with a resolution of 0.03~m.
Although this algorithm already estimates the robot pose relative to the map
being created, this estimation may be improved
if the whole map is used to localize the robot.
So, the \gls{amcl}~\cite{thrun:book:2005} algorithm available in \gls{ros}
was used to estimate the robot's localization relative to the map obtained
from the \gls{slam} process.
With this localization estimation, the point clouds from the 2D~laser scanner
can be transformed to the map coordinate frame that is a static one,
instead of being referenced to the moving laser frame.
All parameters of the algorithms, launch configuration files,
and scripts used in the experiments are also available in a public GitHub
repository\footnote{\url{https://github.com/INESCTEC/inesctec_mrdt_line_fit_based_corner_detector_2d_laser}}.

\subsection{Feature extraction results}

Figure~\ref{fig:features:example-results} is an example of the feature
extraction results. This example represents the feature keypoints
$\left(x,y\right)^{t=t_i}$ at a time instant $t_i$,
where the blue circle is the robot pose estimated by the

The first observation is the lower number of false positives.

\subsection{Features association distance}

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering
  \subfloat[][]{\includegraphics[height=0.365\textwidth]{features/standard-deviation.eps}%
  \label{fig:features:standard-deviation:matrix}}

  \caption[Standard deviation analysis of the distance between
  corresponding corner-like features]{Standard deviation analysis of
  the distance between corresponding corner-like features:
  (a) association matrix; (b) histogram
  distribution~\cite{sousa:icara:2024}.}
  \label{fig:features:standard-deviation}
\end{figure}

Even though the analysis on the features observability indicate
if the features are consistently seen in space,
this evaluation is highly dependent on the location estimator
required to transform the point cloud to a static frame.
Another possible analysis in the context of feature-based localization systems
is to evaluate the distance between different features throughout time,
assessing their relative position instead of the absolute one in space.
So, \figref{fig:features:standard-deviation} presents the analysis
on the feature association distance in terms of standard deviation
to evaluate the uncertainty on the relative position between
the 39~most persistent features selected previously
(see \figref{fig:features:features-observation-ratio}).
The estimation of the standard deviation only considers
valid contributing measures, \ie, only consider the distance
between two features if both are visible in the same time instant.
This estimation was possible by associating the features
observable in each time instant to one of the 39~persistent ones
if the error distance to the location manually selected is lower than 0.06~m.
The latter threshold was needed to account
both the unknown uncertainty of the location estimator and
the noise on the laser measures (zero mean and 0.03~m standard deviation
according to the official specifications for the Hokuyo UST-10LX sensor).

The maximum standard deviation of features association distance
obtained in the experiments is 0.051~m.
Although this value is greater than the one characteristic of the sensor,
most of the associations have a non-zero standard deviation
lower or equal to 0.03~m, representing 91.5\%
of the possible associations between features.
Note that only non-zero values must be considered because,
due to the sensor not having a 360º~\gls{fov}
and to possible physical obstructions on the laser view,
not all associations are physically possible
(in this case, 20 associations are not possible).
Even when considering associations with a deviation lower or equal to 0.03~m,
the proportion remains very significant at 59.3\%.
Thus, these results on the feature association distance
underline the usefulness of the proposed corner-like features detector
for localization processes, given that the geometric relations
between features remain consistent and invariant.

\subsection{Integration with a line-segment feature extraction algorithm}

The final experiment in the scope of this chapter is shown in a
video\footnote{\url{https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7xMKtlvd0wU}},
where our corner-like feature extractor and an open-source line-segment
feature extraction algorithm\footref{note:features:line-feature-ros}
run simultaneously. In most cases, there is a complementarity
between our corner-like features and line-segment features.
Indeed, there are situations in which no corners are detected
while line features are present. At the same time, other situations
in which the expected lines are too small and are therefore possibly rejected
by the line extractor, leaving only corners to be detected.
Although the situations shown in the video are also dependent
on the environment surrounding the robot, this complementarity
between different types of features is advantageous
for localization and mapping processes,
thereby improving the robustness and stability of the robot pose
estimate by increasing the number of features observable in the environment.
Still, the main goal of this experiment is to show that
the proposed feature extraction algorithm may be run in parallel
with other algorithms, leveraging the \gls{ros}~\cite{ros} modular architecture
of having independent nodes subscribing to the same data, in this case,
the range data from the 2D~laser scanner sensor.

\section{Summary and Conclusions}\label{sec:features:conclusions}

This chapter addresses a feature extraction algorithm for corner-like features
from 2D~lasers data. Although the most common way to represent the environment
from 2D~lasers is via 2D~occupancy grid maps, an alternative is to represent
the scene as a set of features. These features may include
beacons\cite{sobreira:icarsc:2015}, geometric cues
(line~\cite{castellanos:mfi:1996,roumeliotis-bekey:iros:2000,mohamed:isprs-arxiv:2016,liu:sensors:2023,nunez:icra:2006,vazquez-martin:sensors:2009,feng:aici:2009,pfister:icra:2003,gao:ijars:2018}
and curve~\cite{falomir:prl:2013,nunez:icra:2006,vazquez-martin:sensors:2009,feng:aici:2009} segments,
corners~\cite{castellanos:mfi:1996,roumeliotis-bekey:iros:2000,jung:tits:2008,falomir:prl:2013,mohamed:isprs-arxiv:2016,lin:yac:2017,liu:electronics:2022,liu:sensors:2023,nunez:icra:2006,vazquez-martin:sensors:2009,feng:aici:2009,certad:andescon:2012},
and free endpoints of segments~\cite{castellanos:mfi:1996,liu:electronics:2022,vazquez-martin:sensors:2009,certad:andescon:2012}),
and semantic features~\cite{jung:tits:2008,qu:robio:2018,roumeliotis-bekey:iros:2000}.
In the specific case of corner-like features, the most common approach
in the literature is to intersect line-segment features to extract corners
from 2D~lasers data~\cite{castellanos:mfi:1996,roumeliotis-bekey:iros:2000,jung:tits:2008,falomir:prl:2013,fernandez-moral:ijrr:2015,mohamed:isprs-arxiv:2016,zhao:icsess:2016,lin:yac:2017,qu:robio:2018,liu:electronics:2022,liu:sensors:2023}.
However, this methodology makes corner detection highly dependent on
the line-detection algorithm and may be limited to well-structured environments.
Another common approach is to estimate curvature from the scan
data~\cite{nunez:icra:2006,vazquez-martin:sensors:2009,feng:aici:2009,certad:andescon:2012}.
Still, the influence of sensor noise on curvature estimation may lead
to variant features and false positives.

Given the previous limitations, we propose a feature-extraction algorithm
to detect points of interest in 2D~laser scanners data
that resemble physical corners. The proposed algorithm performs
local line fitting at multiple distance scales around a given point,
similar to \gls{surf}~\cite{bay:eccv:2006}. These scales define arrival and
departure distance-based neighborhoods around the points in the scan.
The intersection of the fitted lines defines candidate feature keypoints.
Then, a set of indicators based on line-fitting error, the angle between
the arrival and departure lines, and consecutive observations of the same
candidate keypoint across different scales defines a corner-like feature.
Our methodology is independent of global search during line segment scanning, allowing other feature extraction methods to run concurrently.

Experimental results show that the proposed corner-like features correspond
not only to well-defined 90º corners but may also detect
other corner-like points of interest.
More points of interest yield more features that may help stabilize
the robot's localization when estimating its pose using
the proposed corner-like features.
Furthermore, we analyze the observability ratio of the corner-like features
throughout the experiment, based on the robot pose and sensor \gls{fov}.
When considering the robot pose uncertainty and sensor, 6~features had a 100\%
observability ratio, while 20~and 16~features had a ratio greater or equal
to 50\% and 75\%, respectively. These results reveal viewpoint invariance
of the proposed feature extractor algorithm. As for geometric invariance,
when analyzing the association distance between the 39~most persistent
features from the experiments, the maximum standard deviation of
these distances for each pair of associated features was 0.051~m.
In contrast, 91.5\% of possible feature associations had a standard deviation
of 0.03~m or less, which is equivalent to the sensor noise.
These results demonstrate that geometric relations between
corner-like features extracted through our methodology
remain consistent and geometrically invariant.

Overall, our corner-like features demonstrate their usefulness
for localization and mapping processes through their viewpoint- and
geometry-invariant properties in the experiments.
These features can be extracted simultaneously with line segments
from 2D~laser scanner data, and combining different feature modalities
can provide complementary observations, thereby improving
the robustness and stability of localization.
A natural continuation of this work is the development of
a feature-based 2D~localization estimator for mobile robots
using 2D~laser scan data, which could later be integrated
as a front-end within a \gls{slam} framework.
Although feature-based localization and mapping
constitute a promising research direction,
it also requires additional components, such as data association algorithms,
uncertainty models for feature observations,
possibly feature descriptors, and specific map data structures.
These requirements are not commonly supported in \gls{ros}~\cite{ros},
in which the most widely adopted representation of a 2D~environment is
the occupancy grid map rather than a feature-based map.
Therefore, the next chapters of this PhD~Thesis focus on
the localization and mapping of mobile robots using wheeled odometry and
raw 2D~laser scanner data, rather than relying on
feature-based environment representations.
